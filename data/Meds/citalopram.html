Citalopram

Stocked on board

  • Citalopram 20mg Tablets

Adult dosing

  • Major depressive disorder (initial therapy)
    • Starting dose: 20 mg PO once daily (typically in AM or PM; consistent timing)
    • May increase to 40 mg once daily after minimum 1 week if needed and tolerated
    • Maximum dose: 40 mg/day (do NOT exceed due to QT prolongation risk)
  • Generalized anxiety disorder / panic disorder (off-label but commonly used)
    • Starting dose: 10–20 mg PO once daily
    • Titrate as needed to 20–40 mg once daily based on response/tolerability
  • Special populations
    • Older adults (>60 years): Maximum 20 mg/day (increased QT risk)
    • Hepatic impairment: Maximum 20 mg/day
    • CYP2C19 poor metabolizers: Maximum 20 mg/day
  • Discontinuation
    • Taper gradually over several weeks to months when stopping (reduce withdrawal symptoms)
    • Do NOT stop abruptly after prolonged use

Indications

  • Major depressive disorder (approved indication)
  • Generalized anxiety disorder (common off-label use)
  • Panic disorder (off-label)
  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (off-label; other SSRIs preferred)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to citalopram or escitalopram
  • Concurrent use or within 14 days of MAO inhibitors (serotonin syndrome risk; contraindicated)
  • Concurrent use with pimozide (QT prolongation; contraindicated)
  • Black box warning: Increased risk of suicidal thinking and behavior in children, adolescents, and young adults (up to age 24) with major depressive disorder and other psychiatric disorders
    • Monitor closely for worsening depression, suicidality, or unusual behavior changes, especially during initial months and dose changes
  • QT prolongation risk: dose-dependent; do NOT exceed 40 mg/day (20 mg/day in high-risk groups)
    • Caution with other QT-prolonging drugs, cardiac disease, electrolyte abnormalities
    • ECG monitoring recommended in high-risk patients
  • Serotonin syndrome risk: especially with other serotonergic drugs (SSRIs, SNRIs, triptans, tramadol, linezolid, St. John's wort, etc.)
  • Bleeding risk: SSRIs affect platelet function; caution with NSAIDs, ASA, anticoagulants
  • Hyponatremia risk (especially older adults; SIADH mechanism)
  • Mania/hypomania activation in bipolar disorder
  • Pregnancy: weigh risks/benefits; potential neonatal complications, persistent pulmonary hypertension of newborn (PPHN) if used in late pregnancy
  • Sexual dysfunction (common; dose-related)

Adverse effects

  • Nausea, dry mouth, diarrhea, constipation
  • Insomnia or somnolence, fatigue
  • Sexual dysfunction (decreased libido, delayed ejaculation, anorgasmia; common)
  • Sweating, tremor
  • Headache, dizziness
  • Weight changes (usually gain over time)
  • QT prolongation (dose-dependent)
  • Hyponatremia (especially older adults)
  • Serotonin syndrome (rare but serious; with other serotonergic drugs)
  • Discontinuation syndrome if stopped abruptly (dizziness, nausea, headache, irritability, paresthesias)

Preparation & administration

  • Take once daily, same time each day (morning or evening; consistency important)
  • May take with or without food
  • Full therapeutic effect typically takes 4–6 weeks; counsel patient to continue even if not immediate improvement
  • Counsel about suicidality risk (especially young adults); ensure close follow-up during initial weeks
  • Counsel about sexual side effects and management strategies (common reason for discontinuation)
  • When discontinuing: taper gradually over weeks to months (do NOT stop abruptly)

Monitoring

  • Suicidal ideation/behavior, especially during first few months and after dose changes (black box warning)
  • Mood, anxiety symptoms, functional improvement (typically reassess at 2–4 weeks, then regularly)
  • Activation/agitation, mania symptoms (especially if bipolar disorder possible)
  • Serotonin syndrome symptoms if on multiple serotonergic drugs (agitation, confusion, tremor, hyperthermia, hyperreflexia)
  • Bleeding events (especially if concurrent antiplatelet/anticoagulant therapy)
  • Sodium level if high risk for hyponatremia (older adults, diuretics, SIADH risk factors)
  • QT interval if high-risk patient, doses >20 mg, or concurrent QT-prolonging drugs (ECG when feasible)
  • Sexual function (ask directly; often underreported)

At-sea considerations

  • SSRIs like citalopram are generally NOT initiated at sea unless under direct medical supervision/telemedicine guidance (require close monitoring, especially initially)
  • If crew member already stabilized on citalopram before deployment: ensure adequate supply, document current dose and duration, and have plan for mental health follow-up/monitoring
  • Black box warning for suicidality: critical concern in isolated maritime environment; ensure mental health support and evacuation plan if needed
  • Do NOT stop abruptly if supply runs out: taper if possible and arrange resupply/evacuation for psychiatric continuity
  • Operational considerations: initial weeks may cause somnolence or insomnia; assess fitness for safety-critical duties
  • Document mental health baseline and ongoing status clearly; maintain communication with shore-based mental health provider
  • QT prolongation: if ECG available, consider baseline and follow-up monitoring especially if dose >20 mg or cardiac risk factors
