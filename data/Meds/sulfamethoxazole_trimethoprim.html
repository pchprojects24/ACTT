Sulfamethoxazole-Trimethoprim

Stocked on board

  • Sulfamethoxazole-Trimethoprim 800/160mg Tablets

Adult dosing

  • Urinary tract infection (uncomplicated cystitis)
    • 1 double-strength (DS) tablet (800/160 mg) PO twice daily for 3 days
    • Alternative regimen: 3–5 days if clinical response uncertain or complicated factors
  • Urinary tract infection (complicated / pyelonephritis)
    • 1 DS tablet PO twice daily for 7–14 days (duration depends on severity and response)
    • Consider alternate agents if severely ill or local resistance high
  • Skin and soft tissue infection (MRSA-suspected)
    • 1–2 DS tablets PO twice daily for 5–10 days depending on severity and response
    • Typical outpatient dose: 1 DS tablet twice daily
  • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) prophylaxis
    • 1 DS tablet PO daily (or 1 single-strength tablet daily as alternative)
    • Follow local immunocompromised patient protocols
  • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) treatment
    • 15–20 mg/kg/day (based on trimethoprim component) divided every 6–8 hours for 21 days
    • Typical adult dose: 2 DS tablets PO three times daily (or 4 DS tablets twice daily)
    • Requires close monitoring; often managed with medical direction/evacuation
  • Traveler's diarrhea (treatment; if bacterial suspected and appropriate)
    • 1 DS tablet PO twice daily for 3–5 days
    • Not first-line in many regions due to resistance; consider fluoroquinolone or azithromycin per local guidelines

Indications

  • Urinary tract infections (uncomplicated and complicated; susceptible organisms)
  • Skin and soft tissue infections (including MRSA coverage)
  • Acute bacterial exacerbation of chronic bronchitis (selected cases)
  • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia prophylaxis and treatment (immunocompromised patients)
  • Traveler's diarrhea (when susceptibility likely and other agents not preferred)
  • Selected other infections per susceptibility and guidelines (e.g., toxoplasmosis, nocardiosis)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to sulfonamides or trimethoprim (including severe cutaneous reactions: Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis)
  • Pregnancy: contraindicated in first trimester and at term (risk of kernicterus in neonate); use with caution in second trimester only if benefit outweighs risk
  • Breastfeeding: avoid in infants <2 months, G6PD deficiency, prematurity, or hyperbilirubinemia
  • Severe renal impairment (CrCl <15 mL/min): contraindicated; dose adjustment required for moderate impairment
  • Severe hepatic impairment
  • Megaloblastic anemia due to folate deficiency
  • Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency (risk of hemolysis)
  • Caution with concurrent medications: warfarin (INR monitoring), methotrexate, phenytoin, sulfonylureas
  • Hyperkalemia risk: caution with renal impairment, high-dose therapy, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics

Adverse effects

  • GI upset (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, anorexia)
  • Rash (common; ranges from mild to severe/life-threatening)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions: Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS)
  • Hematologic: bone marrow suppression, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia, megaloblastic anemia
  • Hyperkalemia (especially with high doses, renal impairment, or concurrent ACE inhibitors/ARBs)
  • Renal: crystalluria, interstitial nephritis, acute kidney injury
  • Hepatotoxicity (elevated liver enzymes, cholestatic jaundice)
  • Photosensitivity
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea

Preparation & administration

  • Take with full glass of water; maintain adequate hydration to reduce crystalluria risk
  • May take with or without food (food may reduce GI upset)
  • Space doses evenly (e.g., 12 hours apart for twice-daily dosing)
  • Complete full course unless severe adverse reaction occurs
  • Sun protection: advise use of sunscreen and protective clothing (photosensitivity risk)

Monitoring

  • Clinical response within 48–72 hours (symptoms, fever)
  • Rash: stop immediately if severe rash, mucosal involvement, or systemic symptoms (possible SJS/TEN)
  • CBC if prolonged therapy or high-risk patient (monitor for cytopenias)
  • Renal function and electrolytes (especially potassium) if available and patient at risk
  • INR monitoring if on warfarin (increased bleeding risk)
  • Adequate hydration (ensure good urine output)

At-sea considerations

  • Antibiotic stewardship: confirm indication and consider local resistance patterns (SMX-TMP resistance common in some regions for UTI and GI infections)
  • Rash vigilance: educate patient to report rash immediately; stop drug and seek evaluation if severe or progressive
  • Hydration critical: ensure adequate fluid intake, especially in hot environments, to prevent crystalluria and renal complications
  • Drug interactions: review medication list carefully (warfarin, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, methotrexate, sulfonylureas)
  • Limited labs: be conservative in older adults, renal impairment, or prolonged courses; consider alternative agents if monitoring not feasible
  • Document allergy history: important to distinguish true allergy from intolerance for future prescribing
