Thiamine

Stocked on board

  • Thiamine 100mg/mL Injection 1mL

Adult dosing

  • Suspected Wernicke's encephalopathy / Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (acute presentation)
    • 100 mg IV/IM immediately, then 100 mg IV/IM daily until normal diet resumed or no further improvement
    • Some guidelines recommend higher doses (e.g., 200–500 mg TID IV) in confirmed/high-risk cases; follow local protocols or medical direction
    • Administer BEFORE giving IV dextrose to prevent precipitating or worsening Wernicke's
  • Suspected thiamine deficiency (non-emergent)
    • 100 mg IM/IV daily for several days, followed by oral supplementation as available
  • Prophylaxis in high-risk patients (e.g., chronic alcohol use, malnutrition, prolonged vomiting)
    • 100 mg IM/IV prior to IV glucose administration when indicated

Indications

  • Treatment or prevention of Wernicke's encephalopathy (altered mental status, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia in at-risk patients)
  • Thiamine deficiency states (beriberi, chronic alcoholism, malnutrition, hyperemesis)
  • Prevention of Wernicke's before glucose administration in high-risk patients
  • Metabolic emergencies (e.g., lactic acidosis from suspected thiamine deficiency)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to thiamine (rare but can cause anaphylaxis with rapid IV administration)
  • Administer slowly if IV route used; IM route preferred if anaphylaxis risk concern
  • No absolute contraindications in emergency Wernicke's treatment—benefits outweigh risks
  • Large doses are generally well-tolerated; excess thiamine is renally excreted

Adverse effects

  • Generally well-tolerated
  • Rare: hypersensitivity reactions (pruritus, urticaria), anaphylaxis (very rare, more common with rapid IV bolus)
  • Local irritation at IM injection site

Preparation & administration

  • IM injection: administer into large muscle (gluteal or deltoid); 100 mg/mL concentration
  • IV route: dilute in 50–100 mL normal saline or D5W and infuse over 30 minutes to reduce hypersensitivity risk; IM route safer if IV monitoring limited
  • In suspected Wernicke's: give thiamine FIRST, then IV dextrose (not the reverse)
  • Store at room temperature; protect from light

Monitoring

  • Clinical response: improvement in confusion, ataxia, eye movement abnormalities (Wernicke's signs)
  • Monitor for hypersensitivity reaction during/after administration (especially first dose IV)
  • Neurological exam repeated over hours-to-days to assess response
  • If prolonged therapy required, monitor nutritional status and consider transition to oral thiamine when feasible

At-sea considerations

  • Low threshold to give thiamine in altered mental status + known/suspected alcohol use or malnutrition—early treatment critical
  • Document triad: confusion/AMS, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia (Wernicke's), but absence of full triad does not exclude diagnosis
  • Always give thiamine BEFORE glucose in at-risk patients to prevent precipitating Wernicke's
  • IM route is practical and safer onboard if IV infusion monitoring is limited; anaphylaxis risk very low
  • If limited diagnostic capability, treat empirically in high-risk presentations and arrange timely evacuation if no improvement
