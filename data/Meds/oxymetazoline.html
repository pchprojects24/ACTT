Respiratory / Allergy

Oxymetazoline

Stocked on board

  • Oxymetazoline 0.05% Nasal Spray

Adult dosing

  • Nasal congestion (allergic rhinitis, common cold, sinusitis)
    • 2â€“3 sprays per nostril twice daily (morning and evening)
    • Maximum duration: 3 days (to avoid rebound congestion/rhinitis medicamentosa)

Indications

  • Short-term symptomatic relief of nasal congestion due to common cold, allergic rhinitis, or sinusitis
  • Adjunct to facilitate nasal examination or procedures when nasal vasoconstriction needed

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to oxymetazoline or sympathomimetic amines
  • Use beyond 3 consecutive days (risk of rebound congestion)
  • Caution in cardiovascular disease, hypertension, hyperthyroidism, diabetes
  • Narrow-angle glaucoma
  • Prostatic hypertrophy with urinary retention
  • Concurrent or recent (within 2 weeks) MAO inhibitor use
  • Children <6 years (not recommended without medical direction; different formulations/concentrations exist)

Adverse effects

  • Local: nasal dryness, burning, stinging, sneezing
  • Rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa) with prolonged use (>3 days)
  • Systemic (uncommon but possible): headache, dizziness, insomnia, nervousness
  • Cardiovascular: palpitations, increased BP (rare, typically with excessive use or systemic absorption)

Preparation & administration

  • Blow nose gently before use to clear nasal passages
  • Shake bottle before use if directed by product labeling
  • Keep head upright; insert nozzle into nostril and spray while inhaling gently
  • Avoid touching nozzle to nasal mucosa to prevent contamination
  • Wipe nozzle and replace cap after use
  • Do not share nasal spray bottles between individuals

Monitoring

  • Symptom relief (nasal congestion)
  • Duration of use: counsel patient to stop after 3 days maximum
  • Watch for rebound congestion if used beyond recommended duration
  • In hypertensive or cardiovascular disease patients: monitor for increased BP or palpitations (rare)

At-sea considerations

  • Emphasize 3-day maximum use to prevent rhinitis medicamentosa (which requires prolonged discontinuation to resolve)
  • If congestion persists beyond 3 days, reassess for underlying cause (bacterial sinusitis, allergic triggers, etc.) and consider alternate therapies
  • For longer voyages with recurrent nasal congestion: consider oral antihistamines, nasal saline, or nasal corticosteroids (if stocked) rather than repeated oxymetazoline courses
  • Document use and counsel patient on proper technique and duration limits
