Sodium Phosphate

Stocked on board

  • Sodium Phosphate Enema 133mL

Adult dosing

  • Constipation / bowel preparation (rectal enema)
    • One enema (133 mL) rectally as a single dose
    • Effect typically occurs within 2–15 minutes
    • May repeat once after 24 hours if no response and clinically appropriate; reassess need and consider other causes

Indications

  • Relief of constipation when oral laxatives ineffective or not feasible
  • Bowel preparation for procedures (when appropriate for setting)
  • Fecal impaction (in conjunction with manual disimpaction if needed)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Acute abdominal pain, nausea/vomiting of unknown cause (risk of perforation or obstruction)
  • Known or suspected bowel obstruction, ileus, or megacolon
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (active ulcerative colitis or Crohn's disease)
  • Recent colorectal surgery or anorectal pathology (fissure, fistula, severe hemorrhoids)
  • Renal impairment (risk of hyperphosphatemia, electrolyte disturbances)
  • Heart failure, edema, or sodium-restricted states (contains sodium)
  • Dehydration (correct fluid status before use)
  • Older adults and frail patients: increased risk of adverse effects, use with caution

Adverse effects

  • Abdominal cramping, bloating, urgency
  • Rectal irritation, anal discomfort
  • Electrolyte disturbances: hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, hypokalemia, hypernatremia (especially with renal impairment or dehydration)
  • Dehydration, fluid shifts
  • Rare: severe hypotension, syncope, arrhythmias (electrolyte-related), seizures, acute kidney injury

Preparation & administration

  • Patient positioning: Left lateral position (Sims position) preferred for administration
  • Remove protective shield from enema tip; insert tip gently into rectum (do not force)
  • Squeeze bottle steadily until nearly all liquid is expelled
  • Retain enema for 2–5 minutes if possible before evacuating (though urgency may prevent prolonged retention)
  • Ensure patient has access to toilet facilities immediately after administration
  • Encourage oral hydration before and after use to prevent dehydration

Monitoring

  • Bowel movement response (timing, character, completeness of evacuation)
  • Signs of dehydration or electrolyte disturbance (weakness, dizziness, palpitations, muscle cramps)
  • Abdominal pain: reassess if severe or worsening (could indicate complication)
  • Rectal bleeding (if significant, investigate cause)
  • Renal function and electrolytes if available and patient at risk (renal impairment, older adults)

At-sea considerations

  • Rule out serious pathology: ensure no red flags (acute abdomen, obstruction, GI bleeding) before using enema
  • Hydration status critical: ensure adequate oral/IV hydration, especially in hot environments or limited water access
  • Timing: plan administration when patient can remain near toilet facilities; may disrupt operational duties
  • Limited labs: be conservative in older patients and those with known renal disease; consider gentler laxatives first if feasible
  • Document indication, response, and any adverse effects for continuity of care
