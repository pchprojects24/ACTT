Escitalopram

Stocked on board

  • Escitalopram 10mg Tablets

Adult dosing

  • Major depressive disorder (MDD)
    • Initial: 10 mg PO once daily
    • Maintenance: 10 mg PO once daily (may increase to 20 mg daily after minimum 1 week if needed and tolerated)
  • Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)
    • Initial: 10 mg PO once daily
    • Maintenance: 10 mg PO once daily (may increase to 20 mg daily after minimum 1 week if needed)
  • Other uses (off-label but common): panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, OCD
    • Dosing typically similar to above; follow specialist guidance when available
  • Special populations
    • Older adults or hepatic impairment: consider starting at lower dose (5 mg daily if tablets can be split, or 10 mg every other day)
    • Discontinuation: taper gradually (reduce by 50% every 1–2 weeks) to minimize withdrawal symptoms; do not stop abruptly

Indications

  • Major depressive disorder
  • Generalized anxiety disorder
  • Off-label: panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)
  • Continuation of established therapy (most common use onboard)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Concurrent use of MAOIs (within 14 days of stopping MAOI; risk of serotonin syndrome)
  • Hypersensitivity to escitalopram or citalopram
  • Avoid in patients with known QT prolongation or concurrent QT-prolonging medications (escitalopram can prolong QTc, especially at higher doses)
  • Caution in seizure disorders (may lower seizure threshold, though uncommon)
  • Bleeding risk: SSRIs affect platelet function; caution with concurrent anticoagulants/antiplatelets or history of bleeding
  • Activation of mania/hypomania in bipolar disorder (screen for bipolar history before initiating)
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: discuss risks/benefits; SSRIs cross placenta and enter breast milk; follow current guidelines and specialist consultation
  • Suicidality warning: monitor for worsening depression/suicidal ideation, especially in young adults <25 years during initial weeks of treatment

Adverse effects

  • Common: nausea, insomnia or somnolence, sexual dysfunction (decreased libido, delayed ejaculation), dry mouth, sweating
  • Headache, dizziness, fatigue
  • Weight changes (variable)
  • Discontinuation syndrome if stopped abruptly: dizziness, paresthesias, irritability, nausea, "brain zaps" (taper to avoid)
  • Serotonin syndrome (rare; risk with multiple serotonergic agents): agitation, confusion, tremor, hyperthermia, autonomic instability
  • Hyponatremia (rare; more common in older adults or with diuretics)

Preparation & administration

  • Take once daily, with or without food
  • Morning or evening dosing—adjust based on individual response (if insomnia, take in morning; if sedation, take at bedtime)
  • Counsel: therapeutic effect takes 2–4 weeks (sometimes up to 6–8 weeks for full benefit); do not expect immediate mood improvement
  • Emphasize adherence: missing doses or stopping abruptly can worsen symptoms and cause withdrawal
  • Avoid alcohol (may worsen sedation/impairment)
  • Starting therapy onboard: generally avoid initiating new psychiatric medications in isolated maritime environments unless under specialist guidance; onboard use typically for continuation of established therapy

Monitoring

  • Mood, anxiety symptoms, and functional status (weekly to monthly during adjustment phase)
  • Suicidal ideation, especially in first 4–8 weeks or after dose changes (high-risk period)
  • Adverse effects: nausea (usually improves after 1–2 weeks), sexual dysfunction, sleep disturbance, weight changes
  • Activation/agitation or emergence of manic symptoms (stop and seek specialist consultation if occurs)
  • If starting therapy: close follow-up by qualified provider (in-person or telehealth)
  • For established therapy: ensure patient has adequate supply for voyage duration and plan for continuity of care

At-sea considerations

  • Most commonly used for continuation of established therapy rather than new starts; ensure patient has stable dose and adequate supply before departure
  • Screen medication history during pre-voyage medical: confirm psychiatric medication use, stability, adherence, and refill needs
  • If patient requests to start new antidepressant: coordinate with mental health provider ashore; avoid initiating in isolation without ongoing specialist support
  • Monitor for adherence and side effects; check in regularly with patients on psychiatric medications (isolation, stress, and operational demands can impact mental health)
  • If patient reports worsening depression, suicidal thoughts, or emergent mania: immediate telehealth consultation and consider evacuation if safety concerns
  • Discontinuation: if patient wishes to stop, ensure gradual taper plan and close monitoring; coordinate with mental health provider
  • Document doses, adherence, side effects, and mental status clearly for continuity of care at next port or if evacuation needed
  • Ensure confidentiality and reduce stigma around mental health treatment
