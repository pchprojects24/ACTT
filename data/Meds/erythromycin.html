Erythromycin

Stocked on board

  • Erythromycin 2% Ointment

Adult dosing

  • Bacterial conjunctivitis (superficial ocular infections)
    • Apply approximately 1 cm ribbon of ointment to the affected eye(s) 2–6 times daily depending on severity
    • Typical regimen: apply 2–4 times daily for 5–7 days
  • Blepharitis (adjunctive treatment)
    • Apply small amount to eyelid margin 1–2 times daily after lid hygiene/warm compresses
    • Duration varies; follow clinical response and local guidance
  • Prophylaxis of ophthalmia neonatorum (newborn eye infection prevention)
    • 1 cm ribbon to lower conjunctival sac of each eye within 1 hour of birth (single application)

Indications

  • Superficial bacterial ocular infections (conjunctivitis, blepharitis)
  • Prophylaxis of neonatal conjunctivitis (ophthalmia neonatorum)
  • Appropriate for gram-positive organisms and some gram-negative pathogens; limited coverage compared to fluoroquinolone eye drops

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to erythromycin or macrolide antibiotics
  • Not for deep ocular infections, corneal ulcers, or penetrating eye injuries (ophthalmology referral required)
  • Ointment may cause temporary blurred vision; counsel accordingly
  • Contact lens wear: remove lenses before application; avoid wearing during active infection

Adverse effects

  • Temporary blurred vision immediately after application
  • Minor eye irritation, stinging, burning (uncommon)
  • Allergic reactions (rare): itching, redness, swelling
  • Overgrowth of non-susceptible organisms with prolonged use

Preparation & administration

  • Wash hands thoroughly before and after application
  • Tilt head back, pull down lower eyelid to create a small pocket
  • Apply approximately 1 cm ribbon of ointment into the lower conjunctival sac (avoid touching tip to eye or eyelid)
  • Close eye gently and roll eyeball to distribute ointment
  • Wipe away excess with clean tissue
  • Replace cap tightly after use; do not share between patients (infection control)
  • Counsel: vision may be blurry for several minutes after application; apply at bedtime if possible to minimize functional impact

Monitoring

  • Symptom improvement: reduction in redness, discharge, discomfort within 48–72 hours
  • If no improvement after 2–3 days or worsening symptoms (increased pain, vision changes, purulent discharge), reassess diagnosis and consider:
    • Resistant organism (may need fluoroquinolone drops if available)
    • Viral conjunctivitis (no antibiotic needed)
    • Serious ocular pathology requiring specialist evaluation
  • Monitor for local allergic reaction (stop if occurs)

At-sea considerations

  • Conjunctivitis is common in close-quarters; implement infection control (hand hygiene, avoid touching eyes, separate towels/linens)
  • Differentiate bacterial from viral conjunctivitis:
    • Bacterial: purulent discharge, unilateral onset (may spread), responds to antibiotics
    • Viral: watery discharge, often bilateral, may have URI symptoms, self-limited (no antibiotics needed)
  • Ointment formulation preferred over drops in maritime setting (longer contact time, easier storage, less spillage)
  • If vision loss, severe pain, corneal opacity, or suspected penetrating injury: urgent ophthalmology consultation and evacuation planning
  • Document laterality, discharge character, and treatment response to guide ongoing therapy
