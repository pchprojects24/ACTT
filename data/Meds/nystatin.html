Nystatin

Stocked on board

  • Nystatin 100,000 units/mL Suspension 100mL
  • Nystatin 100,000 units/g Cream

Adult dosing

  • Oral candidiasis (thrush)
    • Suspension: 4–6 mL (400,000–600,000 units) swish and swallow 4 times daily
    • Continue for at least 48 hours after symptoms resolve (typically 7–14 days total)
  • Cutaneous/intertriginous candidiasis
    • Cream: Apply thin layer to affected area 2–3 times daily
    • Continue for 7–14 days or until lesions have resolved

Indications

  • Treatment of oropharyngeal candidiasis (oral thrush)
  • Treatment of cutaneous and mucocutaneous candidal infections (skin folds, groin, axillae)
  • Topical treatment of superficial Candida infections

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to nystatin or any component of the formulation
  • Not for systemic fungal infections (not absorbed orally)
  • Oral suspension contains sucrose; consider in diabetic patients
  • Pregnancy/lactation: Generally considered safe for topical use; oral suspension use acceptable when indicated

Adverse effects

  • Oral: GI upset (nausea, diarrhea), unpleasant taste
  • Topical: Local irritation, contact dermatitis (uncommon)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rare)

Preparation & administration

  • Oral suspension: Shake well before use; swish in mouth as long as possible before swallowing (or spit if instructed)
  • Administer after meals or at least 30 minutes before eating when possible to maximize contact time
  • For denture wearers: remove dentures during treatment and soak/clean dentures separately
  • Topical cream: Clean and dry affected area before application; wash hands before and after use
  • Avoid occlusive dressings unless directed

Monitoring

  • Clinical response: resolution of white patches (oral), erythema/scaling (skin) within 48–72 hours
  • If no improvement after 7 days or worsening symptoms, reassess diagnosis (possible resistant organism or non-candidal etiology)
  • For cutaneous infections: monitor for secondary bacterial infection

At-sea considerations

  • Confirm diagnosis when feasible (oral thrush: white plaques that scrape off leaving red base)
  • Consider predisposing factors: antibiotic use, inhaled corticosteroids, immunosuppression, diabetes, dentures
  • If recurrent or refractory cases: consider systemic antifungal need and medical consultation/evacuation planning
  • Document treatment response to guide duration and need for escalation
