Folic Acid

Stocked on board

  • Folic Acid 1mg Tablets

Adult dosing

  • Folic acid deficiency prevention / supplementation
    • Typical: 1 mg PO once daily
    • Higher-risk groups may require different doses per provider guidance
  • Pregnancy / preconception (neural tube defect prevention)
    • Standard risk: 0.4–1 mg PO once daily (begin at least 2–3 months before conception and continue through first trimester)
    • High risk (prior NTD, certain medications, diabetes, etc.): 4–5 mg daily per provider direction
  • Treatment of folate deficiency anemia
    • Typical: 1–5 mg PO once daily (dose and duration per provider/lab guidance)
  • Methotrexate-associated toxicity reduction (when methotrexate used for inflammatory conditions)
    • Typically 1 mg daily or 5 mg weekly (per provider protocol)

Indications

  • Prevention and treatment of folate deficiency
  • Prevention of neural tube defects in pregnancy (preconception and early pregnancy supplementation)
  • Folate deficiency anemia (megaloblastic anemia due to folate deficiency)
  • Adjunct therapy with methotrexate (to reduce side effects)
  • Selected malabsorption conditions or chronic hemolytic anemias (per provider direction)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Known hypersensitivity to folic acid or formulation components (rare)
  • Critical caution: Do not use folic acid alone to treat undiagnosed megaloblastic anemia—must rule out B12 deficiency first (folic acid can mask B12 deficiency while allowing neurologic damage to progress)
  • High-dose folic acid may reduce effectiveness of certain anticonvulsants (phenytoin, phenobarbital); monitor seizure control if applicable
  • Caution in patients with seizure disorders on antiepileptics: coordinate with provider

Adverse effects

  • Generally very well tolerated at recommended doses
  • Rare: GI upset (nausea, bloating), allergic reactions (rash, pruritus, bronchospasm—very rare)
  • High doses: may interfere with zinc absorption (controversial; relevant at very high doses)

Preparation & administration

  • PO tablet; may take with or without food
  • If taking for pregnancy: start preconception (ideally 2–3 months before) and continue at least through first trimester (often continued throughout pregnancy as part of prenatal vitamin)
  • If treating deficiency: ensure B12 status has been evaluated before starting folic acid (critical to avoid masking B12 deficiency)
  • Counsel on adherence, especially in pregnancy prevention context

Monitoring

  • Clinical response in deficiency states: improved energy, resolution of anemia symptoms
  • Laboratory: CBC, reticulocyte count, serum folate (when treating deficiency; follow provider guidance)
  • If treating megaloblastic anemia: confirm B12 levels are adequate (rule out combined deficiency)
  • In pregnancy: generally minimal monitoring needed for standard supplementation; ensure adherence

At-sea considerations

  • Safe, well-tolerated supplement for pregnancy prevention or planned pregnancies on board
  • If evaluating anemia at sea: remember to consider B12 deficiency before initiating folic acid alone (seek medical guidance if unsure)
  • Stock adequate supply for crew members who may become pregnant or are planning pregnancy
  • Stable storage; long shelf life
  • Document indication (pregnancy prevention vs deficiency treatment) to guide duration of therapy
