Vasopressor / Antidiuretic Hormone

Vasopressin

Stocked on board

  • Vasopressin 20 units/mL Injection 1 mL Vial (20 units total)

Adult dosing

Cardiac arrest (ACLS)

  • 40 units IV/IO single dose (may replace first or second dose of epinephrine)
  • Can be used as alternative to epinephrine but not superior; no additional benefit if given with epinephrine

Vasopressor support (septic shock, refractory shock)

  • Fixed-dose infusion: 0.03–0.04 units/min IV continuous infusion
  • Typically added to norepinephrine when high doses of norepinephrine needed (>0.5 mcg/kg/min)
  • Do NOT titrate; use fixed low dose (reduces norepinephrine requirement)

Indications

  • Cardiac arrest (alternative to epinephrine; single 40-unit dose)
  • Septic shock or distributive shock requiring high-dose norepinephrine (adjunct vasopressor)
  • Vasodilatory shock refractory to catecholamines

Contraindications / cautions

  • Use with caution: Coronary artery disease (may cause coronary vasoconstriction and myocardial ischemia), peripheral vascular disease (may worsen ischemia), chronic renal failure
  • Drug interactions: Indomethacin, carbamazepine (potentiate antidiuretic effect), alcohol, lithium (reduce antidiuretic effect)
  • Not recommended as first-line vasopressor; use as adjunct when norepinephrine requirements high

Preparation & administration

  • Cardiac arrest (single dose): Administer 40 units IV/IO push undiluted
  • Vasopressor infusion: Dilute 20 units in 100 mL NS or D5W (0.2 units/mL); infuse at 0.03–0.04 units/min (9–12 mL/hr) via infusion pump
  • Administer via dedicated IV line (preferably central line)
  • Compatible with normal saline, D5W; do not mix with other medications
  • Refrigerate vials; stable at room temperature for short periods

Monitoring

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring and blood pressure
  • Target MAP ≥65 mmHg; monitor perfusion (mental status, urine output, lactate clearance)
  • Monitor for arrhythmias, myocardial ischemia (chest pain, ECG changes)
  • Monitor for peripheral ischemia (check extremities for perfusion and color)
  • Urine output and fluid balance
  • Hyponatremia risk (antidiuretic effect; monitor sodium if prolonged use)

Adverse effects

  • Myocardial ischemia (coronary vasoconstriction; monitor ECG and chest pain)
  • Peripheral ischemia (skin mottling, digital ischemia; check perfusion frequently)
  • Mesenteric ischemia (abdominal pain, elevated lactate)
  • Arrhythmias (rare)
  • Hyponatremia (with prolonged use due to antidiuretic effect)
  • Water intoxication (rare; monitor fluid balance)

At-sea considerations

  • Vasopressin is an adjunct vasopressor added when norepinephrine requirements are high (>0.5 mcg/kg/min)
  • Fixed-dose infusion: Use 0.03–0.04 units/min; do NOT titrate (higher doses increase ischemia risk without additional benefit)
  • Reduces norepinephrine requirement; allows catecholamine-sparing effect
  • In cardiac arrest, may replace first or second dose of epinephrine (40 units IV/IO single dose) but not superior to epinephrine
  • Monitor for myocardial and peripheral ischemia; vasopressin causes non-selective vasoconstriction
  • Not a first-line vasopressor; add only when norepinephrine is already running at moderate-high dose
  • Label infusion clearly; dedicate IV line (preferably central)
  • Document indication, infusion rate, MAP trend, and perfusion status
  • Wean once shock resolves; typically wean norepinephrine first, then discontinue vasopressin
