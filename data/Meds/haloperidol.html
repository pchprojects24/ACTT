Haloperidol

Stocked on board

  • Haloperidol 1mg Tablets
  • Haloperidol 5mg/mL Injection 1mL

Adult dosing

  • Acute agitation / acute psychosis (oral)
    • 0.5–5 mg PO; may repeat every 4–8 hours as needed based on response and tolerability
    • Start low (0.5–2 mg) in elderly or debilitated patients
    • Typical effective dose range: 1–5 mg per dose
  • Acute agitation / acute psychosis (IM)
    • 2–5 mg IM; may repeat every 30–60 minutes as needed (reassess frequently)
    • Use lower doses in elderly/debilitated patients (0.5–2 mg)
    • Maximum: individualize; avoid excessive cumulative doses (QTc/EPS risk)
  • Nausea/vomiting (refractory; when other agents inappropriate)
    • 0.5–2 mg PO/IM every 6–8 hours as needed (off-label use; consult protocols)

Indications

  • Acute agitation or acute psychotic symptoms (emergency management)
  • Schizophrenia and other psychotic disorders (usually provider-directed chronic therapy; onboard use typically acute only)
  • Severe behavioral disturbance requiring rapid control
  • Refractory nausea/vomiting (off-label; when first-line agents unavailable or contraindicated)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to haloperidol or butyrophenones
  • CNS depression, coma
  • Parkinson's disease or Lewy body dementia (can worsen extrapyramidal symptoms)
  • QTc prolongation, cardiac arrhythmia risk, or concurrent QT-prolonging drugs (ECG monitoring ideal when available)
  • Severe cardiovascular disease (monitor closely if used)
  • Caution in elderly: increased risk of falls, sedation, extrapyramidal symptoms, and stroke/mortality in dementia patients (boxed warning)
  • Hepatic impairment: use lower doses and monitor closely

Adverse effects

  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism (dose-related; may occur acutely)
  • Sedation, drowsiness
  • QTc prolongation, arrhythmia (especially at higher doses or with risk factors)
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (rare but serious: fever, rigidity, altered mental status, autonomic instability)
  • Orthostatic hypotension (especially IM route)
  • Tardive dyskinesia (with chronic use)

Preparation & administration

  • Oral: Give with water; may take with or without food
  • IM injection: Deep IM into large muscle (gluteal or deltoid); monitor for hypotension and EPS
  • Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration
  • Avoid IV route in non-monitored settings (QTc/arrhythmia risk; follow institutional protocols if IV considered)
  • Have benztropine/diphenhydramine available for acute dystonic reactions if possible

Monitoring

  • Level of agitation/psychosis; de-escalate dose as symptoms improve
  • Vital signs (BP, HR, temperature) especially after IM administration
  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (muscle stiffness, tremor, restlessness, abnormal movements)
  • ECG/QTc monitoring when feasible (especially if risk factors or higher doses)
  • Watch for neuroleptic malignant syndrome (fever, rigidity, confusion, autonomic instability): stop drug and seek urgent help
  • Sedation/fall risk (especially in elderly)

At-sea considerations

  • Use for severe agitation/acute psychosis when de-escalation and non-pharmacologic approaches have failed or are unsafe
  • Document mental status, vital signs, and clear indication before and after administration
  • If limited monitoring capability: use conservatively and escalate/evacuate if severe behavioral disturbance or complications develop
  • Consider need for benztropine or diphenhydramine onboard for acute dystonic reactions
  • If patient on chronic haloperidol: verify dose and compliance history before additional dosing
