Analgesia / Anti-inflammatory

Hydromorphone

Stocked on board

  • Hydromorphone 1mg/mL Injection 1mL
  • Hydromorphone 2mg Tablets

Adult dosing

  • Moderate to severe acute pain (opioid-naive; oral)
    • Initial: 1–2 mg PO every 4–6 hours as needed
    • Titrate carefully based on response and tolerability
    • Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration
  • Moderate to severe acute pain (opioid-naive; IM/SC)
    • Initial: 0.5–1 mg IM/SC every 4–6 hours as needed
    • Onset ~15 minutes IM; duration ~4–5 hours
    • Lower doses in elderly or debilitated patients (0.25–0.5 mg)
  • Moderate to severe acute pain (opioid-tolerant)
    • Individualize dose based on prior opioid use and equianalgesic conversion
    • Hydromorphone is ~5–7 times more potent than oral morphine

Indications

  • Moderate to severe acute pain (trauma, postoperative, renal colic, musculoskeletal injury)
  • Severe pain not controlled by non-opioid analgesics
  • Cancer pain or palliative care (provider-directed; usually chronic dosing)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to hydromorphone or opioid components
  • Respiratory depression or severe respiratory compromise (COPD exacerbation, severe asthma, etc.)
  • Acute or severe bronchial asthma in unmonitored setting
  • Paralytic ileus or GI obstruction
  • CNS depression, head injury with altered mental status or increased ICP (can mask neurologic changes)
  • Concurrent CNS depressants (benzodiazepines, alcohol): increased respiratory depression and sedation risk
  • Elderly, debilitated, or renal/hepatic impairment: use lower doses and titrate carefully
  • Risk of opioid use disorder: assess history and monitor closely

Adverse effects

  • Respiratory depression (dose-dependent; life-threatening if severe)
  • Sedation, drowsiness, dizziness
  • Nausea, vomiting, constipation
  • Pruritus
  • Hypotension, bradycardia (especially with rapid IV administration or volume depletion)
  • Physical dependence and withdrawal with prolonged use
  • Risk of opioid use disorder

Preparation & administration

  • Oral: Give with water; may take with food to reduce nausea
  • IM/SC injection: Inject deep IM or SC; rotate sites if repeated dosing
  • Avoid IV route in non-monitored settings (risk of severe respiratory depression, hypotension)
  • Have naloxone available for opioid reversal in case of overdose/severe respiratory depression
  • Titrate dose to effect; reassess pain and sedation before each subsequent dose
  • Educate patient: avoid alcohol, CNS depressants, driving, operating machinery

Monitoring

  • Pain relief (use pain scale; reassess 30–60 min after dose)
  • Respiratory rate and depth (monitor closely; hold dose if RR <10/min or shallow breathing)
  • Level of sedation (use sedation scale; stop opioid if oversedated)
  • Vital signs (BP, HR, O2 saturation when available)
  • Nausea/vomiting (consider antiemetic if needed)
  • Bowel function (start bowel regimen if prolonged use anticipated)
  • Watch for signs of overdose: severe sedation, pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression (treat with naloxone and supportive care)

At-sea considerations

  • Use for severe pain when non-opioid analgesics insufficient; assess risk vs benefit carefully
  • Start low, go slow, especially in opioid-naive or elderly patients
  • Ensure naloxone is readily accessible; know dose and administration
  • Document pain score, dose, time, route, and response clearly
  • If limited monitoring capability: use conservative dosing and monitor respiratory status closely
  • Avoid in head injury if neurologic assessment is critical and limited
  • Plan for bowel regimen if multiple doses anticipated
  • If prolonged use needed: consider evacuation for comprehensive pain management
