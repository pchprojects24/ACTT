Valacyclovir

Stocked on board

  • Valacyclovir 500mg Tablets

Adult dosing

  • Herpes zoster (shingles)
    • 1000 mg (2 x 500 mg tablets) PO three times daily for 7 days
    • Most effective if started within 72 hours of rash onset
  • Genital herpes (first episode)
    • 1000 mg (2 x 500 mg tablets) PO twice daily for 10 days
  • Genital herpes (recurrent episode)
    • 500 mg PO twice daily for 3 days (or 1000 mg once daily for 5 days)
    • Most effective if started at first sign of prodrome or lesions
  • Herpes labialis (cold sores)
    • 2000 mg (4 x 500 mg tablets) PO twice daily for 1 day (total 2 doses, 12 hours apart)
    • Start at earliest sign of cold sore (tingling, itching)
  • Dose adjustments for renal impairment
    • CrCl 30–49 mL/min: reduce dose by ~50% (e.g., 1000 mg BID for zoster instead of TID)
    • CrCl 10–29 mL/min: 1000 mg once daily for zoster; 500 mg once daily for genital herpes
    • CrCl <10 mL/min or dialysis: 500 mg once daily for zoster

Indications

  • Herpes zoster (shingles) treatment
  • Genital herpes (HSV-2) treatment (initial and recurrent episodes)
  • Herpes labialis (cold sores / HSV-1) treatment
  • Varicella zoster virus (VZV) and herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections in immunocompetent adults

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to valacyclovir, acyclovir, or any component
  • Renal impairment: dose adjustment required; risk of neurotoxicity and renal dysfunction with standard doses
  • Dehydration: ensure adequate hydration to reduce risk of renal crystallization
  • Immunocompromised patients: may require higher doses and longer duration; follow specialist guidance
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: generally considered safe when indicated (converted to acyclovir); discuss risk/benefit
  • Thrombotic microangiopathy risk in severely immunocompromised patients (rare)

Adverse effects

  • Headache, nausea, abdominal pain
  • Dizziness, fatigue
  • Renal dysfunction (especially in dehydration or pre-existing renal disease)
  • CNS effects (confusion, hallucinations, seizures) — rare, more common in renal impairment or high doses
  • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura/hemolytic uremic syndrome (TTP/HUS) — very rare, mainly in immunocompromised

Preparation & administration

  • PO tablet; may take with or without food
  • Ensure adequate hydration during therapy to reduce risk of renal crystallization
  • Start as early as possible: within 72 hours for zoster, at first sign of prodrome for recurrent HSV
  • Complete full course even if symptoms improve
  • Counsel on transmission prevention: avoid contact with lesions, use barrier protection for genital herpes, avoid kissing/sharing utensils for oral herpes

Monitoring

  • Clinical response: reduction in pain, lesion crusting/healing, resolution of new lesion formation
  • Renal function if prolonged therapy or pre-existing renal impairment (ensure hydration, monitor for oliguria)
  • CNS symptoms: confusion, agitation, hallucinations (especially in older adults or renal impairment)
  • For zoster: monitor for post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN); consider analgesics and referral if pain persists after rash heals
  • Complications: bacterial superinfection, ophthalmic involvement (zoster ophthalmicus), disseminated disease

At-sea considerations

  • Herpes zoster: start within 72 hours of rash onset for maximum benefit; older adults and immunocompromised patients benefit most from early treatment
  • Ophthalmic zoster (V1 dermatome, lesions on nose tip): requires urgent ophthalmology evaluation—arrange evacuation
  • Disseminated zoster or severe presentations: consider IV acyclovir (if available) and evacuation
  • Ensure hydration status adequate before starting therapy and throughout treatment—risk of renal dysfunction in dehydrated patients
  • If renal function unknown and patient has risk factors (older age, diabetes, hypertension), use lower doses or extend dosing intervals
  • Document dose, indication, start date, and planned duration; counsel on completing full course
  • For genital herpes: encourage testing for other STIs when feasible and partner notification/treatment per local guidelines
