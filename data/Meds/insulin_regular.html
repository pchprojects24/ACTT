Insulin Regular

Stocked on board

  • Insulin Regular 100 units/mL 10mL

Adult dosing

  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) / hyperglycemic emergency
    • Initial IV bolus: 0.1 units/kg (may omit bolus in some protocols; follow institutional guidelines)
    • Continuous IV infusion: 0.1 units/kg/hour (typical range 5–10 units/hour for average adult)
    • Adjust rate based on blood glucose response (target decrease ~50–75 mg/dL per hour)
    • Requires close monitoring, IV fluids, electrolyte replacement, and medical direction
  • Hyperglycemia (non-emergency; SC correction dose)
    • Highly individualized; depends on insulin sensitivity, current glucose, and home regimen
    • Example correction scale (for reference; adjust per patient/protocol):
      • Glucose 10–15 mmol/L (180–270 mg/dL): 2–4 units SC
      • Glucose 15–20 mmol/L (270–360 mg/dL): 4–6 units SC
      • Glucose >20 mmol/L (>360 mg/dL): 6–8 units SC and reassess urgently
    • Reassess blood glucose in 1–2 hours; repeat as needed
  • Hyperkalemia (adjunct treatment with dextrose)
    • 10 units regular insulin IV with 25–50 g dextrose (e.g., 50 mL D50W) to shift potassium intracellularly
    • Monitor blood glucose closely (risk of hypoglycemia)
    • Follow emergency hyperkalemia protocols
  • Sliding scale / meal coverage (if no other insulin available)
    • Give SC 30 minutes before meals; dose based on carbohydrate intake and blood glucose
    • Not ideal for routine meal coverage (rapid-acting analogs preferred when available)

Indications

  • Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state (HHS)
  • Acute hyperglycemia requiring rapid correction
  • Hyperkalemia management (with dextrose)
  • Blood glucose control in hospitalized/critically ill patients (IV infusion)
  • Basal-bolus insulin regimen (when other insulin formulations unavailable; not ideal for routine use)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypoglycemia (do not give if blood glucose is low)
  • Hypersensitivity to insulin or product components (rare)
  • Ensure correct insulin type and dose; insulin errors can be fatal
  • Use insulin syringes (U-100) to avoid dosing errors; do not use tuberculin syringes
  • IV use: only regular insulin can be given IV; do NOT give other insulin types IV
  • Renal/hepatic impairment: insulin requirements may change; monitor closely
  • Illness, infection, stress: may increase insulin requirements

Adverse effects

  • Hypoglycemia (most common and serious): sweating, tremor, confusion, seizure, loss of consciousness
  • Hypokalemia (especially with treatment of DKA; monitor and replace potassium)
  • Injection site reactions (pain, redness, lipohypertrophy with repeated use at same site)
  • Weight gain (with chronic use)
  • Rarely: severe allergic reaction, insulin antibody formation

Preparation & administration

  • SC injection:
    • Use U-100 insulin syringe; draw up dose carefully and verify
    • Inject into SC tissue (abdomen, thigh, upper arm); rotate sites
    • Onset ~30 minutes; peak 2–4 hours; duration ~6–8 hours
    • Give 30 minutes before meals if using for meal coverage
  • IV administration (DKA, critical care):
    • Dilute in 0.9% NaCl (typically 100 units in 100 mL = 1 unit/mL)
    • Use infusion pump; ensure dedicated IV line or compatible with other infusions
    • Prime tubing (insulin binds to plastic; first 20–50 mL may be wasted)
    • Monitor blood glucose hourly initially
  • Storage: Refrigerate unopened vials; opened vials can be kept at room temperature for 28 days; protect from heat/light
  • Check expiration date; do not use if discolored or contains particles (regular insulin is clear)

Monitoring

  • Blood glucose: frequently (hourly during IV infusion; every 1–4 hours with SC dosing depending on stability)
  • Signs/symptoms of hypoglycemia: sweating, tremor, confusion, tachycardia, seizure (treat immediately with glucose/dextrose)
  • Electrolytes (especially potassium) during DKA treatment or IV insulin infusion
  • Ketones (if DKA): monitor until resolution
  • Acid-base status (if treating DKA)
  • Patient's usual insulin regimen and adherence (if chronic diabetic)
  • Have dextrose/glucagon readily available for hypoglycemia treatment

At-sea considerations

  • High-risk medication: double-check dose, concentration, route, and patient before administration
  • For DKA: requires intensive monitoring, IV access, fluids, electrolyte replacement—consider urgent evacuation if feasible
  • If limited lab capability: use point-of-care glucose meter; monitor clinical response closely
  • Hypoglycemia is life-threatening: have oral glucose, dextrose IV (D50W or D10W), and glucagon available
  • If patient is on home insulin: verify type, dose, timing, and last dose before giving additional insulin
  • For hyperglycemia without DKA: conservative SC dosing with frequent glucose checks; reassess if not improving
  • Document dose, time, route, blood glucose before/after, and clinical response clearly
  • Insulin storage critical: refrigerate when possible; if no refrigeration, keep cool and use within stability window
