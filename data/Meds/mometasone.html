Mometasone

Stocked on board

  • Mometasone 0.1% Cream

Adult dosing

  • Inflammatory dermatoses (eczema, atopic dermatitis, psoriasis, contact dermatitis)
    • Apply a thin layer to affected area once daily
    • Use the least amount necessary to control symptoms
    • Typical duration: 1–3 weeks; reassess if longer therapy needed
  • Potency classification
    • Mometasone furoate 0.1% cream is a medium-potency (Class IV–V) topical corticosteroid

Indications

  • Relief of inflammatory and pruritic manifestations of corticosteroid-responsive dermatoses
  • Atopic dermatitis (eczema), contact dermatitis, seborrheic dermatitis
  • Psoriasis (mild to moderate)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to mometasone or corticosteroids
  • Do not use on infected skin (bacterial, viral, fungal) unless infection is being concurrently treated
  • Avoid use on face, groin, axillae for prolonged periods (higher risk of skin atrophy, striae, telangiectasia)
  • Do not use under occlusive dressings unless directed (increases systemic absorption and local side effects)
  • Prolonged use or large surface area application can cause systemic corticosteroid effects (HPA axis suppression, Cushing's syndrome, hyperglycemia)
  • Children: use lowest effective dose for shortest duration; higher risk of systemic absorption

Adverse effects

  • Local skin reactions: burning, itching, dryness, irritation (usually mild and transient)
  • Skin atrophy, striae, telangiectasia (with prolonged use, especially on thin skin areas)
  • Perioral dermatitis, acneiform eruptions
  • Secondary infection (if used on infected skin without concurrent antimicrobial therapy)
  • Systemic effects (rare with topical use): HPA axis suppression, hyperglycemia, glaucoma (periocular use)

Preparation & administration

  • Apply a thin layer to affected area once daily; rub in gently until absorbed
  • Wash hands before and after application (unless hands are the treatment area)
  • Avoid contact with eyes and mucous membranes
  • Do not apply to open wounds or weeping lesions
  • If treating infected dermatosis (e.g., infected eczema), use concurrent appropriate antimicrobial therapy
  • Use the lowest potency steroid and shortest duration that controls symptoms

Monitoring

  • Symptom relief (reduced itching, redness, inflammation) within 3–7 days
  • Local skin changes: check for atrophy, striae, or worsening of condition
  • Signs of secondary infection (pustules, increasing redness/warmth, purulent discharge)
  • If no improvement after 2 weeks or condition worsens: reassess diagnosis and consider alternate therapy
  • If prolonged use needed (>2–3 weeks): consider step-down to lower-potency steroid or intermittent dosing

At-sea considerations

  • Useful for acute flares of eczema, contact dermatitis, or irritant dermatitis (common with occupational exposures, detergents, friction)
  • Emphasize that this is a short-term treatment; address underlying triggers when possible (irritant avoidance, moisturizers, skin protection)
  • If skin infection suspected (pustules, honey-crusting, spreading erythema), do not use steroid alone—treat infection first or concurrently
  • Document treatment start date and duration; plan for reassessment and step-down/discontinuation
  • For chronic conditions requiring ongoing steroid use: consider medical consultation for long-term management plan
