Analgesia / Anti-inflammatory

Tramadol

Stocked on board

  • Tramadol 50mg Capsules

Adult dosing

  • Moderate pain (acute, short-term use)
    • Initial: 50 mg PO every 6 hours as needed
    • May increase to 50–100 mg every 4–6 hours as needed if tolerated
    • Maximum: 400 mg/day (use lower max in older adults or renal/hepatic impairment)
  • Dose adjustments
    • Older adults (≥65 years): start 25–50 mg and increase cautiously; maximum 300 mg/day
    • Renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min): 50 mg every 12 hours, maximum 200 mg/day
    • Hepatic impairment: 50 mg every 12 hours; avoid in severe cirrhosis

Indications

  • Moderate to moderately severe acute pain not adequately controlled by non-opioid analgesics
  • Short-term pain management when NSAIDs/acetaminophen contraindicated or insufficient

Contraindications / cautions

  • Acute intoxication with alcohol, opioids, or other CNS depressants
  • Concomitant or recent (within 14 days) MAO inhibitor use
  • Severe respiratory depression, uncontrolled asthma, or significant respiratory compromise
  • Seizure history or conditions that lower seizure threshold (dose-related risk)
  • Serotonin syndrome risk: avoid/use caution with SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, triptans, linezolid, St. John's wort
  • Abuse/addiction potential: screen for substance use disorder; tramadol is a controlled substance in many jurisdictions
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: avoid; risk of neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome with chronic use
  • CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolizers: increased risk of toxicity (rare but important genetic consideration)
  • Older adults: increased fall risk, confusion, constipation; dose reduction required

Adverse effects

  • Dizziness, drowsiness, headache
  • Nausea, vomiting, constipation
  • Dry mouth, sweating
  • Seizures (dose-related; increased risk >400 mg/day or with predisposing factors)
  • Serotonin syndrome (when combined with other serotonergic agents)
  • Respiratory depression (less than traditional opioids but still possible, especially with high doses or CNS depressant co-administration)
  • Dependence/withdrawal with prolonged use

Preparation & administration

  • PO capsule; may take with or without food (taking with food may reduce nausea)
  • Swallow whole; do not crush or chew (immediate-release formulation onboard)
  • Avoid alcohol and other CNS depressants
  • If transitioning from other opioids, follow local guidance for equianalgesic conversions
  • Short-term use only; reassess pain control and discontinue when pain resolves
  • Do not stop abruptly if used for more than a few days—taper if needed to avoid withdrawal

Monitoring

  • Pain relief (use pain scales); adjust dose if inadequate or excessive sedation
  • Sedation level, respiratory rate (especially first dose and dose increases)
  • Signs of serotonin syndrome if on serotonergic meds (agitation, confusion, hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, tremor)
  • Seizure activity (especially in predisposed patients or high doses)
  • Functional impairment (falls, watchkeeping ability)
  • Bowel function (constipation common; consider stool softener/laxative if prolonged use)
  • Misuse/diversion risk: document dispensing clearly; limit supply to shortest effective duration

At-sea considerations

  • Avoid tramadol if patient on SSRIs/SNRIs or other serotonergic meds unless benefit outweighs serotonin syndrome risk; consider alternatives
  • Seizure risk: do not exceed 400 mg/day; avoid in patients with seizure history when possible
  • Operational safety: warn about sedation/dizziness—first dose should not be taken before watchkeeping or critical duties
  • Document indication, dose, planned duration clearly; reassess frequently and stop when pain improves
  • Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration; prefer scheduled NSAIDs/acetaminophen over chronic tramadol when appropriate
  • If patient has no improvement or worsening pain, reassess for alternate diagnosis or need for escalation/evacuation
