Clindamycin

Stocked on board

  • Clindamycin 300mg Capsules

Adult dosing

  • Skin and soft tissue infections (when appropriate pathogen coverage)
    • 300 mg PO every 6 hours OR 450 mg PO every 8 hours
    • Duration typically 5–10 days depending on severity/response
  • MRSA skin infections (when MRSA suspected/confirmed and oral therapy appropriate)
    • 300–450 mg PO every 6–8 hours for 7–14 days (based on severity)
    • Confirm local susceptibility patterns (inducible clindamycin resistance exists)
  • Dental infections / odontogenic infections
    • 300 mg PO every 6 hours for 5–7 days
  • Aspiration pneumonia (when anaerobic coverage needed and appropriate for severity)
    • 300–450 mg PO every 6–8 hours for 7–14 days (or per clinical response)
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis (penicillin allergy; alternative agent)
    • 300 mg PO every 8 hours for 10 days
  • Toxin-mediated streptococcal/staphylococcal infections (e.g., necrotizing fasciitis adjunct; toxic shock syndrome)
    • Used to reduce toxin production; typically combined with other antibiotics
    • 300–450 mg PO every 6–8 hours (or IV in severe cases; follow specialist guidance)

Indications

  • Skin and soft tissue infections (especially when MSSA/MRSA coverage needed; anaerobic coverage)
  • Dental/odontogenic infections (good oral anaerobe coverage)
  • Aspiration pneumonia (anaerobic pathogen coverage)
  • Bone and joint infections (when appropriate organism and susceptibility)
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis (penicillin-allergic patients; alternative)
  • Toxin-mediated infections (adjunct to reduce toxin production in necrotizing fasciitis, toxic shock syndrome)
  • Intra-abdominal infections (usually combined with other agents for gram-negative coverage)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to clindamycin or lincomycin
  • Black box warning: Clostridioides difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD) and pseudomembranous colitis
    • Risk with nearly all antibiotics but historically notable with clindamycin
    • Can occur during therapy or up to several months after discontinuation
    • STOP drug if severe diarrhea develops; consider C. difficile testing and treatment
  • History of GI disease (especially inflammatory bowel disease or antibiotic-associated colitis)
  • Hepatic impairment: use with caution; monitor liver function if prolonged use
  • Caution in patients with history of atopy/allergies (higher hypersensitivity risk)
  • Pregnancy/lactation: generally considered compatible when indicated; clindamycin crosses placenta and appears in breast milk
  • Inducible clindamycin resistance: MRSA and some streptococci may have inducible resistance (D-test); confirm susceptibility when possible

Adverse effects

  • GI upset (diarrhea, nausea, abdominal pain; very common)
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea / pseudomembranous colitis (important; black box warning)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, urticaria; rarely severe reactions including Stevens-Johnson syndrome)
  • Elevated liver enzymes
  • Metallic or bitter taste
  • Esophagitis (take with full glass of water; do not lie down immediately after)

Preparation & administration

  • May take with or without food; taking with food may reduce GI upset
  • Take capsule with full glass of water; remain upright for at least 30 minutes after dose to reduce esophagitis risk
  • Take at evenly spaced intervals to maintain blood levels
  • Counsel: finish prescribed course; report severe or persistent diarrhea immediately (C. difficile risk)
  • If diarrhea develops: do NOT use antidiarrheal agents (e.g., loperamide) without medical guidance (may worsen C. difficile colitis)

Monitoring

  • Clinical response within 48–72 hours (symptoms, fever, local signs of infection)
  • Diarrhea: monitor frequency and severity; STOP drug and evaluate for C. difficile if severe/bloody/persistent diarrhea develops
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, allergic symptoms)
  • Liver function if prolonged course or baseline hepatic impairment

At-sea considerations

  • Excellent MRSA coverage for skin/soft tissue infections when organism susceptible (confirm local resistance patterns when possible)
  • Good anaerobic coverage: useful for dental infections, aspiration pneumonia, and polymicrobial infections
  • C. difficile risk is significant: counsel patients clearly; monitor for diarrhea and stop drug if severe diarrhea develops
  • If C. difficile colitis suspected: stop clindamycin, initiate supportive care, consider empiric C. difficile treatment if severe, and arrange evacuation if needed
  • Ensure adequate hydration and avoid antidiarrheal agents if diarrhea develops
  • Document indication, start date, planned duration, and allergy status clearly
  • If infection not responding within 48–72 hours: reassess for resistant organism, alternate diagnosis, or need for escalation/evacuation
