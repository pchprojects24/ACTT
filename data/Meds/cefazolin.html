Cefazolin

Stocked on board

  • Cefazolin 1gm Injection 10mL

Adult dosing

  • Skin and soft tissue infections (moderate to severe)
    • 1–2 g IV/IM every 8 hours
    • For severe infections: 1 g IV every 6 hours or 2 g IV every 8 hours
  • Urinary tract infections (complicated)
    • 1 g IV/IM every 12 hours
  • Pneumonia (community-acquired, moderate severity)
    • 1 g IV every 8 hours
  • Surgical prophylaxis (single preoperative dose)
    • 1–2 g IV within 30–60 minutes before incision
    • Redose every 3–4 hours during prolonged procedures
  • Endocarditis (staphylococcal; methicillin-susceptible)
    • 2 g IV every 8 hours (provider-directed; typically in combination therapy)
  • Renal impairment dosing
    • Dose reduction required: follow product monograph or clinical reference based on creatinine clearance

Indications

  • Susceptible bacterial infections caused by gram-positive cocci (MSSA, streptococci) and selected gram-negative organisms
  • Skin and soft tissue infections (cellulitis, abscesses)
  • Respiratory tract infections (pneumonia when appropriate coverage)
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Bone and joint infections
  • Surgical prophylaxis (clean and clean-contaminated procedures)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to cefazolin or other cephalosporins
  • History of immediate/severe hypersensitivity to penicillins (risk of cross-reactivity; assess severity and type of reaction)
  • Renal impairment: dose adjustment required to prevent accumulation and toxicity
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea risk: reassess if significant diarrhea develops during/after therapy
  • Not effective against MRSA, Enterococcus, atypical pathogens, or most anaerobes
  • Caution with concurrent nephrotoxic agents (aminoglycosides, loop diuretics)

Adverse effects

  • Injection site reactions (pain, phlebitis, inflammation)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, urticaria; rarely anaphylaxis)
  • GI upset (nausea, diarrhea)
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea
  • Hematologic effects (eosinophilia, thrombocytopenia – uncommon)
  • Elevated liver enzymes (transient)
  • Nephrotoxicity (rare; more likely with high doses or renal impairment)

Preparation & administration

  • Reconstitution: Reconstitute powder with sterile water for injection or 0.9% sodium chloride per manufacturer's instructions
  • IV administration: May give as slow IV push over 3–5 minutes or dilute further and infuse over 30–60 minutes
  • IM administration: May be given IM (can be painful; consider lidocaine if allowed per protocol)
  • Administer at evenly spaced intervals to maintain therapeutic levels
  • Discard unused reconstituted solution per stability guidelines (typically 24 hours refrigerated or less at room temperature)
  • Inspect for particulate matter and discoloration before administration

Monitoring

  • Clinical response within 48–72 hours (fever, infection signs, symptoms)
  • Allergy/hypersensitivity reactions (rash, anaphylaxis): stop immediately if anaphylaxis suspected
  • Renal function (baseline and during therapy if prolonged course or high risk)
  • Diarrhea severity (assess for C. difficile if severe/persistent)
  • IV site for phlebitis/infiltration
  • CBC if prolonged therapy (monitor for hematologic effects)

At-sea considerations

  • First-generation cephalosporin with excellent MSSA coverage; good choice for skin/soft tissue infections when IV therapy needed
  • Confirm indication and avoid antibiotics for likely viral/self-limited illness when possible (stewardship)
  • Limited gram-negative and no MRSA coverage: reassess early non-response and consider alternate diagnosis or need for escalation/evac
  • Ensure proper reconstitution and storage; document preparation date/time
  • Document start date, dose, planned duration, and allergy status clearly
  • If IM administration: rotate sites and warn patient about injection discomfort
