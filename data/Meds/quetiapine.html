Adult dosing

IMPORTANT: Quetiapine initiation/dosing in acute settings should generally be done under psychiatric/medical direction. The following represents typical dosing ranges; adjust per specialist guidance.

  • Acute agitation/acute psychosis (short-term emergency use; medical direction strongly advised)
    • Typical starting dose: 25–50 mg PO; may repeat after 2–4 hours if needed and tolerated
    • Do not exceed 400 mg/day in acute settings without specialist consultation
  • Continuation of established therapy (patient already on quetiapine)
    • Continue home dose as prescribed (commonly 50–800 mg/day in divided doses or once daily depending on formulation and indication)
    • Immediate-release: typically dosed 2–3 times daily; extended-release: once daily
  • Schizophrenia/bipolar disorder (long-term management; specialist-directed)
    • Initial titration typically starts at 25–50 mg twice daily and increases gradually over several days to weeks
    • Target dose varies by indication (schizophrenia: 150–750 mg/day; bipolar: varies widely)

Indications

  • Schizophrenia (acute and maintenance treatment)
  • Bipolar disorder (manic episodes, depressive episodes, maintenance therapy)
  • Acute agitation in psychotic disorders (short-term emergency management)
  • Continuation of established psychiatric therapy when patient is unable to access usual care
  • Off-label uses (e.g., insomnia, anxiety) should be under specialist direction and are not recommended for acute onboard initiation

Contraindications / cautions

  • Known hypersensitivity to quetiapine
  • Caution in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis (increased risk of mortality; black box warning)
  • Risk of neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS): fever, rigidity, altered mental status, autonomic instability—medical emergency
  • Risk of tardive dyskinesia with prolonged use (involuntary movements)
  • Metabolic effects: hyperglycemia, diabetes, weight gain, dyslipidemia (monitor in long-term use)
  • Orthostatic hypotension (especially during initiation or dose increases; fall risk)
  • QT prolongation: caution in patients with cardiac disease, electrolyte imbalances, or concurrent QT-prolonging medications
  • Sedation/drowsiness: impairs ability to perform tasks requiring alertness (operating machinery, etc.)
  • Hepatic impairment: dose reduction may be needed
  • Pregnancy/breastfeeding: use only if benefit outweighs risk; consult specialist
  • Abrupt discontinuation may cause withdrawal symptoms; taper when stopping

Adverse effects

  • Sedation, drowsiness, fatigue (very common, especially initially)
  • Orthostatic hypotension, dizziness, tachycardia
  • Weight gain, increased appetite
  • Hyperglycemia, new-onset diabetes or worsening of existing diabetes
  • Dyslipidemia (elevated cholesterol/triglycerides)
  • Anticholinergic effects: dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision
  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): less common than with typical antipsychotics but possible (tremor, rigidity, akathisia)
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (rare but life-threatening)
  • Tardive dyskinesia (risk increases with duration of use)
  • QT prolongation, arrhythmias (rare)
  • Increased prolactin (less common than with some other antipsychotics)

Preparation & administration

  • May be taken with or without food
  • Immediate-release tablets: can be divided if scored; typically dosed 2–3 times daily
  • Extended-release formulation (if available): swallow whole; do not crush, chew, or split; dosed once daily
  • Start at low dose and titrate slowly to minimize orthostatic hypotension and sedation
  • Advise patient to rise slowly from sitting/lying to reduce dizziness/falls
  • Do not stop abruptly; taper gradually under medical supervision to avoid withdrawal

Monitoring

  • Mental status and psychiatric symptoms (efficacy and safety)
  • Orthostatic vital signs (BP and HR lying and standing) especially after initiation or dose changes
  • Sedation level and fall risk
  • Blood glucose and HbA1c (baseline and periodically during long-term use)
  • Lipid profile (baseline and periodically during long-term use)
  • Weight and BMI (monitor for significant weight gain)
  • Movement disorders (extrapyramidal symptoms, tardive dyskinesia) with ongoing assessment
  • ECG if risk factors for QT prolongation or cardiac disease (baseline and as indicated)
  • Signs of neuroleptic malignant syndrome (fever, rigidity, confusion, autonomic instability)—medical emergency

At-sea considerations

  • Initiation of quetiapine in acute settings should be done under psychiatric/medical consultation when possible
  • Primarily useful for continuation of established home therapy in patients unable to access their usual medications
  • For acute agitation/psychosis: use lowest effective dose; monitor closely for sedation, hypotension, and falls
  • Ensure patient safety (fall precautions, supervision) given sedation and orthostatic hypotension risk
  • Document baseline vital signs, mental status, and any changes during treatment
  • Plan for psychiatric follow-up and specialist consultation as soon as feasible
  • If NMS suspected (fever, rigidity, altered mental status), discontinue immediately and arrange urgent evacuation
  • Limited lab monitoring capability at sea: prioritize clinical monitoring and early consultation for metabolic concerns
