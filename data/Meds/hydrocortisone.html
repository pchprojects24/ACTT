Hydrocortisone

Stocked on board

  • Hydrocortisone 1% Cream
  • Hydrocortisone 100mg Injection (powder)

Adult dosing

Topical (1% cream)

  • Mild inflammatory skin conditions (eczema, dermatitis, pruritus)
    • Apply thin layer to affected area 2–4 times daily until symptoms controlled
    • Use the lowest frequency that maintains control
    • Limit duration to 2 weeks for facial/intertriginous areas unless directed otherwise

Injectable (100 mg)

  • Acute adrenal insufficiency / adrenal crisis (emergency)
    • 100 mg IV/IM immediately, then 50–100 mg IV/IM every 6–8 hours until stable
    • Transition to oral corticosteroids when appropriate
    • Requires concurrent fluid resuscitation and electrolyte management
  • Severe allergic reaction (adjunct; when epinephrine and antihistamines primary)
    • 100–500 mg IV/IM (follow local anaphylaxis protocols)
    • Not a substitute for epinephrine in anaphylaxis
  • Severe inflammatory conditions (when IV corticosteroid indicated)
    • Dosing highly variable by condition; follow clinical protocols

Indications

  • Topical: Mild to moderate inflammatory dermatoses (eczema, contact dermatitis, pruritus, insect bites)
  • Injectable: Acute adrenal insufficiency/adrenal crisis, severe allergic reactions (adjunct), severe inflammatory/immunologic conditions requiring IV corticosteroid (provider-directed)

Contraindications / cautions

Topical:

  • Hypersensitivity to hydrocortisone or cream components
  • Skin infections (fungal, bacterial, viral) unless treated concurrently (can worsen infection)
  • Rosacea, perioral dermatitis, acne (can exacerbate)
  • Prolonged use on face/intertriginous areas: skin atrophy risk
  • Do not use on open wounds or eyes

Injectable:

  • Hypersensitivity to hydrocortisone
  • Systemic fungal infection (relative contraindication unless treating adrenal crisis)
  • Caution in patients with infection (can mask symptoms/signs and impair immune response)
  • Diabetes: monitor glucose closely (hyperglycemia risk)
  • GI ulcer risk, psychiatric symptoms, electrolyte disturbances with prolonged or high-dose use

Adverse effects

Topical:

  • Skin atrophy, striae (with prolonged use or occlusion)
  • Burning, itching at application site
  • Secondary skin infection
  • Rarely: systemic absorption with extensive/prolonged use

Injectable:

  • Hyperglycemia, fluid retention
  • Hypertension, electrolyte disturbances (hypokalemia)
  • Mood changes, insomnia, psychosis (dose/duration dependent)
  • Immunosuppression, increased infection risk
  • GI upset, peptic ulcer (higher doses/prolonged use)
  • Adrenal suppression with prolonged use (do not abruptly discontinue)

Preparation & administration

Topical:

  • Apply thin layer to clean, dry skin; rub in gently
  • Wash hands before and after application (unless treating hands)
  • Avoid occlusive dressings unless directed (increases absorption)
  • Do not apply to face for prolonged periods without reassessment

Injectable:

  • Reconstitute 100 mg powder with provided diluent or sterile water for injection per product instructions
  • May give IV push (over 30 seconds to several minutes) or IM injection
  • For adrenal crisis: give immediately alongside IV fluids and management of underlying trigger
  • Store reconstituted solution per product instructions; use promptly

Monitoring

Topical:

  • Symptom improvement (itch, erythema, inflammation)
  • Skin integrity (watch for atrophy, secondary infection, worsening)
  • If no improvement after 2 weeks or symptoms worsen: reassess diagnosis

Injectable:

  • Vital signs, clinical response (especially in adrenal crisis or severe allergy)
  • Blood glucose (especially in diabetics or prolonged use)
  • Electrolytes, BP if prolonged therapy
  • Watch for infection, GI bleeding, psychiatric symptoms with repeated/high doses

At-sea considerations

Topical:

  • Good first-line for minor inflammatory skin conditions; safe for short-term use
  • If skin infection suspected: do not use steroid alone; treat infection concurrently
  • Avoid prolonged unsupervised use on face or intertriginous areas

Injectable:

  • Critical for adrenal crisis: recognize symptoms (hypotension, shock, confusion in patient with adrenal insufficiency or recent steroid withdrawal) and treat urgently with hydrocortisone + IV fluids
  • In anaphylaxis: prioritize epinephrine; hydrocortisone is adjunctive
  • If limited labs: monitor clinical response and blood glucose when feasible
  • Document indication, dose, time, and response clearly; plan for evacuation if severe underlying condition
