Opioid Analgesic

Fentanyl

Stocked on board

  • Fentanyl 50 mcg/mL Injection 2 mL Vial (100 mcg total)

Adult dosing

Acute pain management (opioid-naive)

  • 25–50 mcg IV slow push every 5–10 minutes; titrate to effect
  • Onset ~1–2 minutes IV; peak 3–5 minutes; duration 30–60 minutes
  • Reduce dose in elderly (start 12.5–25 mcg)

Procedural analgesia (adjunct to sedation)

  • 50–100 mcg IV slow push before procedure
  • Titrate in 25 mcg increments; reassess before repeat dosing

Post-intubation analgesia / prolonged hold

  • Bolus 50–100 mcg IV then infusion 25–100 mcg/hr
  • Titrate to pain control and sedation depth

Indications

  • Moderate to severe acute pain (trauma, procedural pain, renal colic)
  • Procedural analgesia (adjunct to sedation for painful procedures)
  • Post-intubation analgesia in ventilated patients
  • Rapid-onset analgesia when IV opioid needed

Contraindications / cautions

  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to fentanyl, severe respiratory depression without ventilatory support, acute or severe bronchial asthma in unmonitored setting
  • Use with caution: Concurrent CNS depressants (benzodiazepines, alcohol; increased respiratory depression), elderly or debilitated patients, COPD or sleep apnea, head injury or increased ICP (can mask neurologic changes), renal/hepatic impairment, bradycardia or hypotension risk
  • Chest wall rigidity: Rare but serious complication with rapid large-dose IV push; may impair ventilation; treat with naloxone or neuromuscular blocker if severe
  • Risk of opioid use disorder: Use shortest effective duration; assess history and monitor closely

Preparation & administration

  • Administer slow IV push over 1–2 minutes (rapid push increases chest wall rigidity and respiratory depression risk)
  • Titrate in small increments (25 mcg); wait 5 minutes between doses to assess effect
  • Compatible with normal saline, D5W, lactated Ringer's
  • Protect from light; do not use if precipitate or discoloration present
  • Have naloxone (reversal agent) and airway equipment immediately available

Monitoring

  • Continuous pulse oximetry, respiratory rate and depth
  • Level of sedation (use sedation scale; hold dose if oversedated)
  • Cardiac monitoring and blood pressure
  • Pain relief assessment (use pain scale; reassess 5–10 min after dose)
  • Monitor for chest wall rigidity (rare; more common with rapid large doses)
  • Watch for signs of overdose: severe sedation, pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression (treat with naloxone)

Adverse effects

  • Respiratory depression and apnea (dose-dependent; life-threatening if severe)
  • Sedation, drowsiness, dizziness
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Bradycardia, hypotension (especially with rapid IV push or volume depletion)
  • Chest wall rigidity (rare; more common with rapid large-dose IV push; can impair ventilation)
  • Pruritus
  • Physical dependence and withdrawal with prolonged use

At-sea considerations

  • Fentanyl is a potent, rapid-onset opioid ideal for acute pain and procedural analgesia
  • Respiratory depression risk: Monitor closely; titrate slowly; avoid rapid IV push
  • ~100 times more potent than morphine; small dosing errors can have serious consequences
  • Short duration (30–60 min) requires repeat dosing or infusion for prolonged analgesia
  • Excellent adjunct to ketamine or midazolam for procedural sedation
  • Have naloxone immediately available for reversal (0.04–0.4 mg IV; titrate to effect)
  • Naloxone re-sedation risk: Fentanyl duration may exceed naloxone duration (30–90 min); monitor for re-sedation for minimum 2–4 hours after last dose
  • Avoid in head injury if serial neuro checks critical (masks pupil response and level of consciousness)
  • Label syringes clearly with mcg dose (not mL); use micrograms to prevent dosing errors
  • Document dose (mcg), time, route, indication, pain score, and response clearly
