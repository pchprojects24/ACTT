Ketamine

Formulary status

Note: Ketamine is NOT clearly confirmed in standard Sick Bay stock. Verify availability with FSMO before sailing. If ketamine is unavailable, see RSI procedure for alternative strategies.

Stocked on board (if available)

  • Ketamine 50 mg/mL Injection 10 mL Vial (500 mg total)

Adult dosing

RSI induction (dissociative dose)

  • 1.5–2 mg/kg IV push over 30–60 seconds
  • Typical dose: 100–200 mg for 70 kg patient
  • Onset ~30–60 seconds; duration 10–20 minutes

Procedural sedation (dissociative dose)

  • 1–2 mg/kg IV slow push; or 4–5 mg/kg IM
  • Onset IV ~1 minute; IM ~3–5 minutes
  • Duration 10–20 minutes (IV) or 15–30 minutes (IM)

Sub-dissociative analgesia (pain control)

  • 0.1–0.3 mg/kg IV slow push (typical: 10–30 mg for 70 kg patient)
  • Repeat q20–30 min as needed for ongoing pain
  • Avoid mid-range dosing (0.3–0.8 mg/kg) which may cause dysphoria without full dissociation

Post-intubation sedation / prolonged hold

  • Bolus 0.5–1 mg/kg IV then infusion 0.1–0.5 mg/kg/hr (typical range: 5–40 mg/hr)
  • Titrate to sedation depth; combine with opioid for multimodal analgesia

Indications

  • RSI induction agent (preferred in hemodynamically unstable patients; preserves airway reflexes better than other agents)
  • Procedural sedation for painful procedures (fracture reduction, chest tube, cardioversion, burn care)
  • Sub-dissociative analgesia for severe acute pain (trauma, burns, renal colic)
  • Post-intubation sedation in prolonged-hold scenarios
  • Bronchodilator properties may benefit severe bronchospasm/status asthmaticus

Contraindications / cautions

  • Relative contraindications: Severe uncontrolled hypertension, recent stroke/TIA, severe coronary artery disease, acute myocardial ischemia, acute globe injury/elevated intraocular pressure, psychosis or severe agitation from psychiatric cause
  • Emergence reactions: Vivid dreams, hallucinations, agitation upon emergence (10–20% incidence; reduced with benzodiazepine co-administration)
  • Hypersalivation: Consider antisialagogue (glycopyrrolate) if excessive secretions problematic
  • Pregnancy: Category B; use if benefit justifies risk

Preparation & administration

  • Draw up dose and label syringe clearly with mg dose and concentration
  • Pre-oxygenate patient; have suction, airway equipment, and monitoring ready
  • IV push over 30–60 seconds for induction; slower for sub-dissociative analgesia
  • Protect from light during storage
  • Compatible with normal saline; avoid mixing with barbiturates (precipitation risk)

Monitoring

  • Continuous pulse oximetry, cardiac monitoring, BP
  • Capnography (ETCO2) strongly recommended during procedural sedation and mandatory post-intubation
  • Airway patency and ventilation (ketamine preserves airway reflexes but does not guarantee airway protection)
  • Laryngospasm risk (~0.4%); have positive-pressure ventilation and advanced airway equipment immediately available
  • Monitor for hypertension, tachycardia, emergence reactions

Adverse effects

  • Hypertension and tachycardia (sympathomimetic effect; may be beneficial in shock)
  • Hypersalivation, nausea/vomiting
  • Emergence reactions: hallucinations, agitation, dysphoria (mitigate with low-dose benzodiazepine)
  • Laryngospasm (rare but serious; have BVM and advanced airway ready)
  • Nystagmus, diplopia (expected; not harmful)

At-sea considerations

  • Ketamine is the preferred RSI induction agent for hemodynamically unstable patients (trauma, shock, sepsis)
  • Preserves respiratory drive and airway reflexes better than other sedatives (but NOT a guarantee; airway equipment must be ready)
  • Ideal for procedural sedation in austere settings due to hemodynamic stability and short duration
  • Sub-dissociative analgesia (0.1–0.3 mg/kg) provides powerful pain relief without full dissociation
  • If ketamine unavailable: For procedural sedation in lower-acuity stable patients, consider hydromorphone + midazolam combination. For RSI without ketamine, acknowledge hard limitation and prepare for surgical airway if needed
  • Label all syringes clearly; avoid mid-range dosing (0.3–0.8 mg/kg)
  • Warn patient/crew about emergence reactions; minimize stimulation during recovery
  • Use checklists and dedicated monitoring personnel during procedural sedation
