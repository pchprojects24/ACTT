Ceftriaxone

Stocked on board

  • Ceftriaxone 250mg Injection

Adult dosing

  • Gonorrhea (uncomplicated urethral, cervical, rectal, pharyngeal)
    • 250 mg IM single dose (single-dose vial stocked onboard)
    • Note: Current Canadian guidelines may recommend dual therapy with azithromycin; verify local protocol
  • Community-acquired pneumonia (moderate to severe; inpatient)
    • 1–2 g IV/IM once daily
  • Meningitis (bacterial; empiric or specific pathogen)
    • 2 g IV every 12 hours (typically in combination therapy; provider-directed)
  • Intra-abdominal infections (moderate to severe)
    • 1–2 g IV/IM once daily (often combined with metronidazole for anaerobic coverage)
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease (inpatient treatment)
    • 250 mg IM single dose, then continue with oral doxycycline (or per protocol)
  • Skin and soft tissue infections (severe)
    • 1–2 g IV/IM once daily
  • Urinary tract infections (complicated; pyelonephritis)
    • 1–2 g IV/IM once daily
  • Renal impairment dosing
    • No dose adjustment required unless concurrent severe hepatic impairment (dual elimination: renal + biliary)

Indications

  • Uncomplicated gonorrhea (single-dose IM therapy)
  • Severe bacterial infections requiring parenteral therapy (pneumonia, meningitis, sepsis)
  • Intra-abdominal infections (often combined with anaerobic coverage)
  • Pelvic inflammatory disease
  • Skin and soft tissue infections (severe)
  • Complicated urinary tract infections / pyelonephritis
  • Third-generation cephalosporin with excellent gram-negative and some gram-positive coverage; long half-life allows once-daily dosing

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to ceftriaxone or other cephalosporins
  • History of immediate/severe hypersensitivity to penicillins (risk of cross-reactivity; assess severity and type of reaction)
  • Hyperbilirubinemic neonates (can displace bilirubin; contraindicated in neonates)
  • Concurrent IV calcium-containing solutions (precipitation risk; do not mix)
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea risk: reassess if significant diarrhea develops during/after therapy
  • Not effective against MRSA, Enterococcus, Pseudomonas, or anaerobes (add metronidazole for anaerobic coverage if needed)
  • Biliary sludging/pseudolithiasis (rare; more common with prolonged high-dose therapy)

Adverse effects

  • Injection site reactions (pain, induration at IM site; phlebitis with IV)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, urticaria; rarely anaphylaxis)
  • GI upset (nausea, diarrhea)
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea
  • Elevated liver enzymes (transient)
  • Hematologic effects (eosinophilia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia – uncommon)
  • Biliary sludging/pseudolithiasis (usually reversible; more common with high doses or prolonged therapy)

Preparation & administration

  • IM administration (gonorrhea; 250 mg dose):
    • Reconstitute powder with 1% lidocaine (without epinephrine) per manufacturer's instructions to reduce injection pain
    • Administer deep IM into large muscle mass (gluteal or lateral thigh)
    • Typical reconstitution: 250 mg vial with 0.9 mL diluent
  • IV administration (higher doses):
    • Reconstitute with sterile water for injection or 0.9% sodium chloride
    • May give as slow IV push over 2–4 minutes (concentrated) or dilute further and infuse over 30 minutes
  • Once-daily dosing: administer at the same time each day
  • Do not mix with calcium-containing solutions (precipitation risk)
  • Discard unused reconstituted solution per stability guidelines (varies by diluent; typically 24 hours refrigerated)
  • Inspect for particulate matter and discoloration before administration

Monitoring

  • Clinical response within 48–72 hours (fever, infection signs, symptoms)
  • Allergy/hypersensitivity reactions (rash, anaphylaxis): stop immediately if anaphylaxis suspected
  • Diarrhea severity (assess for C. difficile if severe/persistent)
  • For gonorrhea treatment: test of cure recommended (especially pharyngeal infections); follow local STI guidelines and ensure partner notification
  • Liver function tests and CBC if prolonged therapy (monitor for elevated enzymes, hematologic effects)
  • Signs of biliary sludging if prolonged high-dose therapy (right upper quadrant pain, nausea)

At-sea considerations

  • Excellent single-dose IM therapy for uncomplicated gonorrhea (250 mg dose stocked onboard)
  • Long half-life allows once-daily dosing for severe infections requiring parenteral therapy
  • For gonorrhea: verify current dosing recommendations and dual therapy guidelines; ensure partner management per public health protocol
  • Broad-spectrum coverage but lacks anaerobic activity: add metronidazole for intra-abdominal/gynecologic infections when needed
  • Confirm indication and avoid antibiotics for likely viral/self-limited illness when possible (stewardship)
  • IM injection can be painful: use lidocaine reconstitution and warn patient
  • Document start date, dose, planned duration, and allergy status clearly
