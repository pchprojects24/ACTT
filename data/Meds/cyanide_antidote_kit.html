Cyanide Antidote Kit

Adult dosing

Suspected or confirmed acute cyanide poisoning (fire victims with smoke inhalation, altered mental status, seizures, cardiovascular collapse; industrial exposure)

  • Traditional two-step cyanide antidote kit (amyl nitrite + sodium nitrite + sodium thiosulfate)
    • Step 1: Amyl nitrite inhalation (while IV access being established)
      • Break 1 ampule and hold under patient's nose; inhale for 15–30 seconds every minute
      • Discontinue once IV sodium nitrite is ready
    • Step 2: Sodium nitrite IV
      • 300 mg (10 mL of 3% solution) IV over 5–10 minutes
      • Pediatric dosing: varies by hemoglobin level; follow kit instructions or toxicology guidance
    • Step 3: Sodium thiosulfate IV
      • 12.5 g (50 mL of 25% solution) IV over 10–30 minutes
      • May repeat at half the initial dose if symptoms persist or recur
  • Newer agent: Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit) (if available; preferred in many settings)
    • 5 g IV over 15 minutes; may repeat 5 g dose if needed
    • Consult product monograph or poison center for specific administration instructions
  • NOTE: Treatment must be initiated immediately based on clinical suspicion; do not wait for laboratory confirmation

Indications

  • Known or suspected acute cyanide poisoning
  • Common scenarios: enclosed-space fires with smoke inhalation + altered mental status/cardiovascular collapse, industrial chemical exposure, ingestion of cyanide-containing substances
  • Clinical clues: unexplained severe lactic acidosis, altered mental status, seizures, cardiovascular instability in appropriate exposure context

Contraindications / cautions

  • Sodium nitrite: causes methemoglobinemia by design (converts hemoglobin to methemoglobin to bind cyanide); use with extreme caution if co-existing carbon monoxide poisoning (common in fire victims)—risk of worsening hypoxia
  • Consider hydroxocobalamin (if available) as safer alternative in smoke inhalation with possible CO exposure
  • Hypotension risk with rapid IV nitrite administration; monitor BP closely
  • Pregnancy: use if life-threatening; potential fetal risks from both cyanide and methemoglobin formation
  • G6PD deficiency: increased risk of hemolysis with nitrites

Adverse effects

  • Sodium nitrite: hypotension, tachycardia, methemoglobinemia (by design but may be excessive), headache, dizziness
  • Sodium thiosulfate: generally well tolerated; nausea, vomiting (uncommon)
  • Amyl nitrite: headache, dizziness, hypotension, flushing
  • Hydroxocobalamin: chromaturia (red-colored urine/plasma), transient hypertension, rash, nausea; can interfere with lab testing (colorimetric assays)

Preparation & administration

  • Scene safety first: ensure rescuers are protected from ongoing cyanide exposure; remove patient from source
  • Ensure high-flow oxygen for all suspected cyanide poisoning patients
  • Traditional kit: follow stepwise administration as above; ensure IV access before starting nitrite/thiosulfate
  • Monitor vital signs continuously during administration
  • Contact poison control center and medical toxicology support early (1-800-222-1222 in North America)
  • Document exposure history, time of antidote administration, and clinical response

Monitoring

  • Continuous vital signs (BP, HR, oxygen saturation, ECG if available)
  • Mental status and neurological exam
  • Methemoglobin level if nitrites used (goal <30%; higher levels worsen oxygen delivery)
  • Lactate/acid-base status (improvement suggests effective treatment)
  • Co-oximetry if available (to assess both carboxyhemoglobin from CO and methemoglobin)
  • Observe for recurrent symptoms (may require repeat thiosulfate dosing)

At-sea considerations

  • Cyanide poisoning is rare but rapidly fatal; high index of suspicion needed in fire victims with unexplained severe symptoms or industrial chemical exposures
  • If diagnostic uncertainty and patient is critically ill with suspected exposure: treat empirically (risk of antidote generally lower than risk of untreated cyanide poisoning)
  • Ensure kit is stored properly, not expired, and crew trained in recognition/administration
  • Immediate evacuation planning: cyanide poisoning requires intensive care-level support even after antidote administration
  • If only traditional kit available and concern for CO co-poisoning, consider consulting toxicology before nitrite administration (may preferentially use thiosulfate alone or await hydroxocobalamin)
  • Document all exposure details, antidote doses/times, and response for receiving facility and regulatory reporting
