Azithromycin

Stocked on board

  • Azithromycin 250mg Tablets
  • Azithromycin 500mg Intravenous

Adult dosing

  • Community-acquired pneumonia (outpatient)
    • PO: 500 mg on day 1, then 250 mg PO once daily on days 2–5 (total 5 days)
    • Alternative (if severe or unable to take PO initially): 500 mg IV once daily for at least 2 days, then 500 mg PO once daily to complete 7–10 days
  • Acute bacterial sinusitis (when antibiotics indicated)
    • 500 mg PO once daily for 3 days
  • Acute exacerbation of chronic bronchitis
    • 500 mg PO on day 1, then 250 mg PO once daily on days 2–5
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis (alternative to penicillin if allergic)
    • 500 mg PO once daily for 3 days OR 500 mg PO on day 1, then 250 mg once daily on days 2–5
  • Skin and soft tissue infections (uncomplicated)
    • 500 mg PO on day 1, then 250 mg PO once daily on days 2–5
  • Traveler's diarrhea (selected cases)
    • 1000 mg PO as a single dose (or per local protocol)

Indications

  • Community-acquired pneumonia (mild-moderate)
  • Acute bacterial sinusitis, acute bronchitis exacerbations
  • Pharyngitis/tonsillitis (alternative to penicillin)
  • Skin and soft tissue infections caused by susceptible organisms
  • Selected sexually transmitted infections (per infectious disease guidelines)
  • Atypical infections (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Hypersensitivity to azithromycin or other macrolide antibiotics (erythromycin, clarithromycin)
  • History of cholestatic jaundice or hepatic dysfunction associated with prior azithromycin use
  • QT prolongation risk: caution in patients with known prolonged QT interval, uncorrected electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia), or concurrent QT-prolonging drugs
  • Cardiovascular disease: increased risk of cardiovascular death reported in some populations; use with caution
  • Myasthenia gravis: exacerbation possible
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea risk with any antibiotic use
  • Dose adjustment generally not required for renal impairment but use caution in severe hepatic impairment

Adverse effects

  • GI upset (nausea, diarrhea, abdominal pain) – common
  • Headache
  • QT prolongation and risk of torsades de pointes (rare but serious)
  • Hepatotoxicity (rare; monitor for jaundice, severe abdominal pain)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, angioedema, anaphylaxis)
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea
  • Ototoxicity (rare; reversible hearing loss with high doses)

Preparation & administration

  • PO tablets: May take with or without food. Taking with food may reduce GI upset
  • IV administration: Reconstitute 500 mg vial per product monograph; dilute to 1–2 mg/mL in compatible IV fluid. Infuse over 60 minutes (or as per product monograph). Do not give as IV bolus or IM
  • Long half-life allows once-daily dosing and short courses
  • Counsel: complete the full course even if feeling better; report palpitations, fainting, or severe diarrhea

Monitoring

  • Clinical response within 48–72 hours (symptom improvement, fever resolution)
  • Cardiac: assess baseline cardiac risk factors; monitor for palpitations, syncope (QT prolongation concern)
  • GI symptoms: nausea is common but monitor for severe or persistent diarrhea (C. difficile)
  • Hepatic: monitor for signs of hepatotoxicity (jaundice, dark urine, severe abdominal pain)
  • Allergic reactions (rash, angioedema)

At-sea considerations

  • Useful for atypical respiratory infections and when penicillin allergy present
  • Be aware of QT prolongation risk: avoid in patients with cardiac history when possible or ensure monitoring capability
  • Short course and once-daily dosing improve adherence in operational settings
  • IV formulation available for severe infections when oral not feasible; plan for transition to oral as soon as appropriate
  • Document indication clearly (avoid unnecessary macrolide use to preserve effectiveness and reduce resistance)
  • If non-responsive within 48–72 hours, reassess diagnosis and consider alternate therapy or evacuation
