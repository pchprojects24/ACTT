Triamcinolone

Stocked on board

  • Triamcinolone 0.1% Cream

Adult dosing

  • Inflammatory dermatoses (eczema, atopic dermatitis, contact dermatitis, psoriasis)
    • Apply thin film to affected area 2–3 times daily
    • Once improvement seen, reduce frequency to once or twice daily or as needed
    • 0.1% cream is a medium-potency topical corticosteroid
  • Duration of use
    • Use for shortest duration necessary to control symptoms (typically 1–2 weeks for acute flares)
    • Reassess if no improvement after 2 weeks or if worsening
    • Avoid prolonged continuous use to minimize risk of skin atrophy and HPA axis suppression

Indications

  • Inflammatory and pruritic dermatoses responsive to topical corticosteroids
  • Eczema/atopic dermatitis flares
  • Contact dermatitis (allergic or irritant)
  • Seborrheic dermatitis
  • Psoriasis (limited areas; not as first-line for extensive disease)

Contraindications / cautions

  • Untreated bacterial, viral, or fungal skin infections (can worsen infection; treat infection first or use with appropriate antimicrobial)
  • Rosacea, acne vulgaris, perioral dermatitis (may worsen)
  • Avoid use on face, groin, and axillae if possible (higher risk of skin atrophy and absorption); if needed, use for shortest duration and lower potency when feasible
  • Open wounds or broken skin (absorption and delayed healing)
  • Prolonged use or large surface area application: risk of systemic absorption and HPA axis suppression
  • Children: use lower potency and limit duration; increased systemic absorption risk

Adverse effects

  • Local: burning, itching, dryness at application site (usually mild and transient)
  • Skin atrophy (thinning), striae, telangiectasia (with prolonged use or high-potency formulations)
  • Hypopigmentation (reversible after discontinuation)
  • Acneiform eruptions, perioral dermatitis
  • Secondary infection (bacterial or fungal)
  • Systemic effects (rare with appropriate topical use): HPA axis suppression, Cushing's syndrome, hyperglycemia (with prolonged use over large areas)

Preparation & administration

  • Topical cream: apply thin film to clean, dry affected area; gently rub in
  • Wash hands before and after application (unless treating hands)
  • Do not apply to face or intertriginous areas (groin, axillae) unless directed and for shortest duration
  • Avoid occlusive dressings unless specifically indicated (increases absorption and potency)
  • Do not use on open wounds or near eyes
  • Counsel: use as directed, do not apply more frequently or for longer than recommended

Monitoring

  • Clinical response: reduction in erythema, scaling, pruritus
  • Signs of skin atrophy: thinning, striae, telangiectasia (especially with prolonged use)
  • Secondary infection: worsening redness, pustules, increased pain or drainage
  • If prolonged use or large area coverage: monitor for systemic effects (hyperglycemia, signs of Cushing's)
  • Reassess diagnosis if no improvement after 2 weeks of appropriate therapy

At-sea considerations

  • Confirm dermatitis/inflammatory condition before use—avoid if infection suspected (consider swab/culture if available or evacuate if uncertain and severe)
  • If infection present (impetigo, cellulitis, fungal): treat infection first with appropriate antimicrobial; may use triamcinolone concurrently only if inflammatory component significant and under medical guidance
  • For facial rashes or perioral dermatitis: avoid medium-potency steroids; consider lower potency or non-steroidal options if available
  • Document area treated, frequency, and duration; educate on taper/discontinuation once improved
  • If rash spreads or worsens despite treatment, reassess for alternate diagnosis (drug reaction, systemic process) and consider evacuation
