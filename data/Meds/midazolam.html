Midazolam

Stocked on board

  • Midazolam 5 mg/mL Injection 2 mL Vial (10 mg total)
  • Midazolam 1 mg/mL Injection 5 mL Vial (5 mg total)

Adult dosing

Procedural sedation (moderate sedation)

  • 0.02–0.05 mg/kg IV slow push (typical: 1–3 mg) given in 0.5–1 mg increments
  • Titrate to effect; wait 2–3 minutes between doses
  • Onset ~1–2 minutes IV; peak 3–5 minutes; duration 30–60 minutes
  • Reduce dose in elderly (start 0.5–1 mg) and with concurrent opioids

Anxiolysis (pre-procedure or acute anxiety)

  • 0.5–2 mg IV slow push; or 2–5 mg IM
  • Titrate to minimal effective dose

Seizure management (status epilepticus)

  • 5 mg IV/IO/IM; repeat every 5 minutes until seizure stops (typical max 3 doses)
  • IM route effective if no IV access (onset ~5–10 min IM)
  • Monitor airway closely; may cause respiratory depression

Post-intubation sedation / prolonged hold

  • Bolus 2–5 mg IV then infusion 1–5 mg/hr
  • Titrate to sedation depth; combine with opioid for analgesia

Indications

  • Procedural sedation and anxiolysis
  • Seizure management (first-line benzodiazepine for status epilepticus)
  • Post-intubation sedation
  • Agitation or combative patient requiring chemical sedation
  • Adjunct to reduce ketamine emergence reactions

Contraindications / cautions

  • Contraindications: Known hypersensitivity to benzodiazepines, acute narrow-angle glaucoma, severe respiratory depression or airway obstruction without ventilatory support
  • Use with caution: Elderly (higher sensitivity; start low dose), concurrent CNS depressants (opioids, alcohol; increased respiratory depression risk), renal/hepatic impairment (prolonged duration), COPD or sleep apnea (respiratory depression risk), pregnancy/breastfeeding
  • Paradoxical reactions: Rarely causes agitation, aggression, or disinhibition (especially elderly or psychiatric patients)
  • Respiratory depression: Dose-dependent; worse with rapid IV push or concurrent opioids; have airway equipment and bag-valve-mask ready

Preparation & administration

  • Administer slow IV push over 1–2 minutes (rapid push increases respiratory depression risk)
  • Titrate in small increments (0.5–1 mg); wait 2–3 minutes between doses to assess effect
  • IM route acceptable if no IV access (onset slower; 5–10 min)
  • Compatible with normal saline, D5W, lactated Ringer's
  • Protect from light; do not use if precipitate or discoloration present
  • Have flumazenil (reversal agent) and airway equipment available

Monitoring

  • Continuous pulse oximetry, respiratory rate, and level of sedation
  • Cardiac monitoring and blood pressure
  • Airway patency (may cause airway obstruction or apnea; have suction and BVM ready)
  • Monitor for paradoxical reactions (agitation, aggression)
  • Capnography (ETCO2) strongly recommended during procedural sedation

Adverse effects

  • Respiratory depression and apnea (dose-dependent; highest risk with rapid IV push or concurrent opioids)
  • Sedation, drowsiness, amnesia (expected; anterograde amnesia common)
  • Hypotension (especially in volume-depleted or elderly patients)
  • Paradoxical reactions: agitation, aggression, disinhibition (rare; more common in elderly or psych patients)
  • Nausea, vomiting, hiccups

At-sea considerations

  • Midazolam is a versatile sedative for procedural sedation, seizures, and agitation management
  • Respiratory depression risk: Titrate slowly in small increments; avoid rapid push; monitor continuously
  • Excellent amnesia properties (useful for procedural sedation but patient may not recall instructions afterward)
  • Combine with ketamine for procedural sedation to reduce emergence reactions and provide analgesia
  • IM route effective for seizures or agitation when IV access unavailable (onset 5–10 min)
  • Have flumazenil available for reversal if severe respiratory depression occurs (0.2 mg IV initial dose)
  • Use conservative dosing in elderly, volume-depleted, or concurrent opioid use
  • Label syringes clearly; document dose, time, route, indication, and sedation response
  • Monitor for prolonged sedation in prolonged-hold scenarios; may accumulate with repeated dosing
