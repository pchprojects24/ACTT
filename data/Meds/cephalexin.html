Cephalexin

Stocked on board

  • Cephalexin 500mg Tablets

Adult dosing

  • Skin and soft tissue infections (uncomplicated; cellulitis, abscesses when oral appropriate)
    • 500 mg PO every 6 hours OR 1 g (2 x 500 mg) PO every 12 hours
    • Duration typically 5–10 days depending on severity/response
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis (when beta-lactam appropriate and penicillin allergy/alternative needed)
    • 500 mg PO every 12 hours for 10 days
  • Uncomplicated UTI (cystitis; when appropriate for local resistance patterns)
    • 500 mg PO every 6 hours OR 1 g PO every 12 hours for 7 days (or per local guidance)
  • Respiratory tract infections (when appropriate pathogen coverage)
    • 500 mg PO every 6 hours; duration varies by indication (typically 5–10 days)

Indications

  • Susceptible skin and soft tissue infections (S. aureus, Streptococcus spp., when MSSA coverage appropriate)
  • Streptococcal pharyngitis (alternative to penicillin)
  • Uncomplicated urinary tract infections (appropriate susceptibility)
  • Selected respiratory tract infections (otitis media, sinusitis, CAP when pathogen appropriate)
  • Bone and joint infections (when susceptible organism and oral therapy appropriate)

Contraindications / cautions

  • History of immediate hypersensitivity to cephalosporins
  • History of severe (anaphylaxis/severe cutaneous reaction) penicillin allergy: use caution; cross-reactivity risk ~1–3% (assess individual history/risk)
  • Renal impairment: dose adjustment required when CrCl <30 mL/min (follow product monograph or local guidance)
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea risk: reassess if significant diarrhea develops during or after therapy
  • Pregnancy/lactation: generally considered safe when indicated; consult product monograph for specific guidance

Adverse effects

  • GI upset (diarrhea, nausea, abdominal pain)
  • Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, urticaria; rarely anaphylaxis)
  • C. difficile-associated diarrhea (important but uncommon)
  • Vaginitis/yeast infections

Preparation & administration

  • May take with or without food; taking with food may reduce GI upset
  • Take at evenly spaced intervals to maintain blood levels
  • Counsel: finish the prescribed course unless told otherwise; do not share leftover antibiotics

Monitoring

  • Clinical response within 48–72 hours (symptoms, fever, local signs of infection)
  • Allergy/rash: stop and seek help if signs of severe reaction or anaphylaxis
  • Diarrhea severity (consider C. difficile if severe/persistent/bloody)
  • If prolonged course or renal impairment: consider renal function monitoring when feasible

At-sea considerations

  • First-generation cephalosporin: good staphylococcal and streptococcal coverage for skin/soft tissue infections
  • If diagnostics are limited and infection is not responding within 48–72 hours, reassess for alternate diagnosis, resistant organism, or need for escalation/evacuation
  • Document start date, dose, planned duration, and allergy status clearly
  • In penicillin-allergic patients: clarify history of reaction type (rash vs anaphylaxis) to guide safety of cephalosporin use
