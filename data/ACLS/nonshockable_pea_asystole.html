ACLS Algorithm

Adult Cardiac Arrest — Non-shockable (PEA / Asystole)

Entry criteria

  • Unresponsive
  • Not breathing normally
  • No palpable pulse
  • Monitor shows PEA (organized electrical activity without a pulse) or asystole

Immediate actions (first moments)

  • Start high-quality CPR immediately
    • Rate 100–120/min, depth 5–6 cm, full recoil
    • Minimize interruptions; rotate compressor about every 2 minutes
  • Apply monitor/defibrillator and confirm rhythm (leads attached; gain/filters appropriate)
  • Provide oxygen; ventilate with BVM; avoid hyperventilation
  • Establish IV access (or IO if IV delayed)
  • Attach waveform capnography if available (especially once advanced airway placed)

Core cycle (repeat every 2 minutes)

1) CPR for 2 minutes

  • Continue compressions with minimal pauses
  • Assign roles: airway/ventilation, access/meds, rhythm checks, reversible causes

2) Epinephrine

  • 1 mg IV/IO as soon as possible
  • Repeat every 3–5 minutes while in arrest
  • Do not pause compressions to give medications

3) Rhythm + pulse check (brief)

  • If still PEA/asystole → resume CPR immediately and continue epinephrine schedule
  • If becomes shockable (VF/pVT) → switch to shockable algorithm
  • If organized rhythm with a pulse → treat as ROSC and move to post–cardiac arrest care

Concurrent priorities during cycles

  • Search for and treat reversible causes (Hs & Ts) continuously — click each item for details:
    Hypoxia

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Cyanosis, low SpO₂
    • Respiratory distress or absent chest rise
    • Witnessed aspiration or airway obstruction

    Labs / iSTAT

    • ABG/VBG: low PaO₂, possible respiratory acidosis
    • ETCO₂ waveform absent or abnormal

    Reversal

    • Secure airway (BVM, supraglottic device, or ETT)
    • Ventilate with high-flow O₂ (100%)
    • Confirm tube placement with waveform capnography
    • Suction if secretions or foreign body suspected
    Hypovolemia

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Flat neck veins, dry mucous membranes
    • Evidence of trauma, external or internal bleeding
    • Narrow-complex PEA (fast, narrow QRS without pulse)

    Labs / iSTAT

    • Low Hgb / Hct
    • Elevated lactate
    • Base deficit on ABG/VBG

    Reversal

    • Rapid IV/IO crystalloid bolus
    • Blood products if hemorrhagic (consider massive transfusion protocol)
    • Identify and control bleeding source (tourniquet, pressure, surgical)
    Hydrogen ion (acidosis)

    Signs & Symptoms

    • History of DKA, renal failure, sepsis, or prolonged arrest
    • Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing) if spontaneous respirations present

    Labs / iSTAT

    • ABG/VBG: low pH (< 7.2), low HCO₃⁻
    • Elevated lactate, abnormal anion gap
    • Glucose (rule out DKA)

    Reversal

    • Ensure adequate ventilation (blow off CO₂)
    • Consider sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq IV if severe acidosis (pH < 7.1) or prolonged arrest
    • Treat underlying cause (fluids for sepsis, insulin for DKA, dialysis for renal failure)
    Hypo-/hyperkalemia

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Hyperkalemia: peaked T waves, widened QRS, sine wave pattern, cardiac arrest
    • Hypokalemia: flattened T waves, U waves, prolonged QT, arrhythmias
    • History of renal failure, dialysis, medications (ACE-I, K-sparing diuretics, digoxin)

    Labs / iSTAT

    • iSTAT K⁺ level (critical values: < 2.5 or > 6.5 mEq/L)
    • 12-lead ECG for characteristic changes

    Reversal

    • Hyperkalemia: calcium chloride 1 g IV (or calcium gluconate), sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq IV, insulin 10 units + dextrose 25 g IV; see Hyperkalemia pathway
    • Hypokalemia: IV potassium replacement (KCl infusion), check and replace magnesium
    Hypothermia

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Cold exposure history, cold skin
    • Core temperature < 35 °C (< 30 °C increases arrest risk significantly)
    • Osborn (J) waves on ECG

    Labs / iSTAT

    • Core temperature measurement (esophageal or rectal preferred)
    • Glucose (hypothermia may mask hypoglycemia)
    • Electrolytes (K⁺ may shift with rewarming)

    Reversal

    • Active rewarming: warm IV fluids (38–42 °C), warm blankets, forced warm air
    • If core temp < 30 °C: may withhold further defibrillation attempts until temp rises above 30 °C
    • Continue resuscitation — "not dead until warm and dead"; see Hypothermia pathway
    Tension pneumothorax

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Unilateral absent breath sounds
    • Tracheal deviation (away from affected side; late sign)
    • JVD, hypotension, difficulty ventilating
    • History of trauma, central line placement, or positive-pressure ventilation

    Labs / iSTAT

    • Clinical diagnosis — do not delay treatment for imaging
    • Bedside ultrasound may show absent lung sliding if available

    Reversal

    • Needle decompression: 2nd ICS midclavicular line or 4th–5th ICS anterior axillary line (affected side)
    • Follow with finger thoracostomy or chest tube
    • Reassess breath sounds and hemodynamics after intervention
    Cardiac tamponade

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Beck's triad: muffled heart sounds, JVD, hypotension
    • PEA arrest with narrow-complex rhythm
    • History of chest trauma, recent cardiac surgery, pericarditis, malignancy

    Labs / iSTAT

    • Bedside ultrasound (subxiphoid view): pericardial effusion with RV collapse
    • Electrical alternans on ECG (variable QRS amplitude)

    Reversal

    • Pericardiocentesis (needle aspiration of pericardial fluid)
    • Even removal of small amounts of fluid (20–50 mL) can improve hemodynamics significantly
    • Definitive treatment: surgical drainage / thoracotomy if available
    Toxins

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Toxidrome clues: pupil size, skin findings, vital sign patterns
    • Medication bottles, patches, substance history
    • ECG: wide QRS (sodium-channel blockers/TCA), prolonged QT, bradycardia (beta-blockers/CCBs)

    Labs / iSTAT

    • Glucose (always check), electrolytes
    • 12-lead ECG (QRS width, QT interval, conduction abnormalities)
    • Blood gas to assess acid–base status

    Reversal

    • Opioids: naloxone 2 mg IV/IN (repeat as needed); see Opioid Toxicity pathway
    • TCAs / sodium-channel blockers: sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus (target QRS narrowing)
    • Beta-blockers / CCBs: glucagon, high-dose insulin/dextrose, calcium, vasopressors
    • Local anesthetic toxicity: 20% lipid emulsion (Intralipid) bolus + infusion
    • Digoxin: digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind/DigiFab)
    Thrombosis (coronary or pulmonary)

    Signs & Symptoms

    • Coronary: preceding chest pain, known CAD, STEMI on ECG
    • Pulmonary embolism: sudden collapse, preceding dyspnea/pleuritic pain, DVT risk factors, RV strain on ECG (S1Q3T3, right axis, RBBB)

    Labs / iSTAT

    • 12-lead ECG: ST changes (STEMI), RV strain pattern
    • Bedside ultrasound: RV dilation (PE), wall-motion abnormalities (MI)
    • Troponin if available (may be delayed)

    Reversal

    • Coronary thrombosis: standard ACLS; emergent PCI if possible; consider fibrinolytics if PCI unavailable
    • Pulmonary embolism: consider systemic fibrinolytics (e.g., tPA 50 mg IV bolus) during arrest; extend CPR to at least 60–90 min after thrombolytics; surgical/catheter embolectomy if available
  • If a cause is strongly suspected, treat immediately (do not wait for multiple cycles)

Airway/ventilation checkpoints

  • If no advanced airway: ventilate appropriately with BVM; avoid excessive ventilation
  • If advanced airway placed:
    • Confirm placement with waveform capnography
    • Continue continuous compressions with ventilations at an appropriate rate
  • Use ETCO₂ trends to prompt CPR quality checks and reassess reversible causes (very low/falling ETCO₂ is concerning for poor perfusion/technique)

Exit paths

  • ROSC → Post–Cardiac Arrest Care
  • Shockable rhythm develops → VF/pVT pathway
  • Persistent PEA/asystole despite optimized CPR and reversible-cause treatment → continue cycles and consider escalation/termination per local protocol and context
