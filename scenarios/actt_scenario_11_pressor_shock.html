Pressor-Dependent Shock With Resource Strain

Septic Shock Vasopressors Resource Strain Prolonged Holding Sustainability

A sailor in their 30s presents to Sick Bay with 24 hours of fever, rigors, weakness, and progressive hypotension. Initial fluid resuscitation improves blood pressure only briefly. As the patient transitions to pressor-dependent shock, the clinical challenge shifts from sepsis recognition to an operational problem — can the ship sustain the monitoring burden of a norepinephrine infusion with limited staffing, equipment, and delayed evacuation?

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize when persistent hypotension has progressed beyond simple fluid-responsive shock and requires vasopressor support.
  • Apply bedside decision-making from first presentation through escalation, resource management, and evacuation planning.
  • Practice safe norepinephrine preparation, initiation, monitoring, and reassessment in a shipboard environment.
  • Identify when a plan is clinically correct but operationally unsustainable because of line security, infusion-pump limits, staffing strain, or delayed evacuation.
  • Reinforce prolonged-holding principles: trend monitoring, device checks, explicit handover, early teleconsultation, and recognition of worsening pressor dependence.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Sick Call Deterioration

A sailor in their 30s is brought to Sick Bay by a supervisor and a crewmate. Over the last 24 hours they have had fever, rigors, generalized weakness, and progressive shortness of breath. They now look pale, ill, and increasingly fatigued. They complain of dizziness when sitting up and say they feel "like I am going to pass out." On arrival: the patient is alert but slowed. Skin is warm with delayed capillary refill. There is a productive cough and pleuritic discomfort, making pneumonia one likely source.

At sea, moderate sea state. Helicopter available only by hoist, not deck landing. Estimated earliest extraction window is 6 to 8 hours if the weather holds. Available support: RCN PA, one assisting medic, one non-clinical helper, finite medication stock, limited infusion pumps, and standard monitoring — but not enough for ICU-style continuous one-to-one observation indefinitely.
Operational Note
Place on monitor immediately. Establish IV access quickly — if peripheral IV is difficult, do not waste excessive time; escalate to IO if needed. Begin oxygen if saturation is falling. Obtain bedside data: glucose, temperature, focused lung exam.
STAGE 2 Initial Resuscitation & Fluid Phase

The team initiates crystalloid resuscitation. After the first bolus, blood pressure briefly improves to 92/58 but then drifts back down. The patient remains tachycardic and reports worsening dizziness. Capillary refill is still delayed. Work of breathing has not changed significantly.

  • Give initial crystalloid bolus and reassess: BP, mental status, capillary refill, work of breathing, oxygen requirement.
  • Start empiric antibiotics early — do not let "more data" delay treatment. Document exact time started.
  • If crackles, rising oxygen need, or worsening respiratory distress appear after fluids, slow down and reassess rather than pushing more fluid automatically.
  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics ONLY if doing so does not meaningfully delay antibiotics.
Decision Point 1 of 5
After initial crystalloid bolus, BP briefly improves to 92/58 then drifts back. The patient remains tachycardic with delayed capillary refill. What is your next priority?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. Early antibiotics within the sepsis/septic shock treatment window is critical. Continued fluid resuscitation with reassessment prevents both under-resuscitation and fluid overload. Preparing for pressor need shows anticipatory planning.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. A second bolus may be appropriate, but reflexively giving repeated large volumes without reassessing respiratory cost is a common error. Watch for crackles, rising oxygen need, and worsening respiratory distress.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. In septic shock, delaying antibiotics for diagnostic certainty increases mortality. Use the source-appropriate empiric regimen and update later if the picture changes. Timing matters more than precision at this stage.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Starting a pressor after only one fluid bolus without adequate initial resuscitation is premature. Norepinephrine is indicated for persistent hypotension AFTER initial resuscitation, not as a first-line replacement for fluids.
Teaching Note
Once severe sepsis or septic shock is suspected, antibiotics belong early in the sequence. The key teaching point is timing: do not let "more data" delay appropriate empiric treatment.

This is the key pivot point: the patient may require norepinephrine, but starting a pressor commits the team to a much heavier monitoring burden.

Decision Point 2 of 5
After adequate initial fluid resuscitation, the patient remains hypotensive with MAP below 65. Mentation is drifting and urine output is poor. What is your next step?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. Persistent hypotension after initial resuscitation with ongoing poor perfusion is the clear indication for norepinephrine. If central access is not available, peripheral administration is acceptable but the IV site must be monitored frequently for extravasation.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. One more bolus is reasonable if the respiratory cost is still manageable, but do not continue reflexive fluid loading when perfusion remains poor. At some point, persistent hypotension requires pressor support.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Norepinephrine is the recommended first-line vasopressor for septic shock. Dopamine has a higher rate of arrhythmias and is not preferred.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Antibiotics will not improve hemodynamics in the acute phase. The patient is failing to maintain pressure and perfusion NOW — waiting for antibiotics to work while the patient deteriorates is dangerous.
⚠ Inflection Point
This is where the scenario becomes a shipboard systems problem. Starting norepinephrine may be clinically appropriate, but you must immediately ask whether your line, pump, monitoring, and staffing plan are sustainable for the next several hours.
STAGE 4 Resource Strain & Monitoring Burden

Norepinephrine is running. MAP has improved to 67 mmHg. The patient is slightly more alert. But the team is now managing an infusion pump, frequent BP cycling, peripheral IV site checks, and ongoing assessment — with one PA, one medic, and one non-clinical helper. The earliest extraction is still 4-6 hours away.

Decision Point 3 of 5
Norepinephrine is running, MAP has improved to 67. The patient looks slightly better. But you notice the peripheral IV site is slightly swollen. What do you do?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. Any suspicion of extravasation with norepinephrine requires immediate action. Stop the infusion, assess the site, and prepare for rescue treatment. Tissue injury from pressor extravasation can occur quickly. Establish a new IV line before restarting.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable only if the swelling is truly minimal and you are actively preparing the backup. But "watching" an extravasating pressor while it continues to run is a recipe for tissue injury. The safer default is to stop first.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Extravasation of norepinephrine is a serious complication that can cause tissue necrosis. Slight swelling at a peripheral pressor site is not normal — it is a warning sign that demands immediate assessment.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. There are no oral vasopressors appropriate for acute septic shock management. This is not a viable alternative.
Decision Point 4 of 5
The IV is resited and the pressor restarted. However, the medic reports fatigue after continuous monitoring, and the non-clinical helper is needed for other ship duties. The patient requires BP checks every 15 minutes, IV site checks, ongoing reassessment, and the extraction window is still 3-4 hours away. What do you decide?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. A correct clinical intervention can expose a weak operational system. The right plan is the plan that can be safely maintained by the actual team you have. Sometimes the most important decision is recognizing that the ship can no longer safely sustain the monitoring burden.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Extending intervals is a pragmatic compromise, but it increases the risk of missing rapid deterioration or extravasation. If chosen, it should be paired with clear escalation criteria and a push for additional support.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. A pressor-dependent patient is NOT stable — they are fragile. Reducing monitoring to hourly checks for a patient on norepinephrine creates unacceptable risk for unrecognized deterioration.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Stopping the pressor because of monitoring burden rather than clinical readiness will likely result in immediate hemodynamic collapse. The correct response to unsustainable care is to get more resources or evacuate — not to remove the treatment keeping the patient alive.
Teaching Note
A patient who is temporarily supported by pressors is not "solved." They are often more fragile, not less, because the plan now depends on continuous maintenance. The blood pressure may look better while perfusion, oxygenation, and reserve still worsen.
STAGE 5 Evacuation Decision & Teleconsultation

The patient has been on norepinephrine for 2 hours. MAP is maintained at 66-68 with a gradually increasing dose. Urine output remains poor. The extraction window is now 2-3 hours away if weather holds. The team must decide whether to continue the hold or push harder for evacuation.

Decision Point 5 of 5
The patient's norepinephrine requirement is gradually increasing. Urine output remains poor. The extraction window is 2-3 hours away. During teleconsultation, what is your recommended plan?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. Pressor dependence with increasing dose requirement should lower the threshold for early evacuation. State the clinical AND operational problem clearly: limited staff, limited pumps, at-sea constraint, and concern about sustainability. Package early rather than waiting for the patient to worsen.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Consulting about dose escalation is clinically appropriate, but the teleconsultation should also include the operational sustainability concern and evacuation planning — not just medication management.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Describing a patient with increasing pressor requirement and poor urine output as "stable" misrepresents the clinical trajectory. Pressor dependence should push toward urgency, not routine scheduling.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. An increasing pressor requirement is not improvement — it is the opposite. Antibiotics take time to work, but the patient's hemodynamic trajectory is worsening NOW. Continued holding without acknowledging deterioration is dangerous.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Increasing pressor requirement despite initial response. Peripheral IV extravasation with sudden loss of hemodynamic support. Progressive respiratory failure after fluids and ongoing sepsis. Worsening confusion indicating perfusion failure or sepsis progression. Missed antibiotic timing because the team became fixated on the blood pressure. Poor handover causing dose changes, missed site checks, or duplicated fluids.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Continuing reflexive fluid boluses after respiratory cost rises and benefit plateaus.
  • Delaying antibiotics while waiting for diagnostic certainty.
  • Starting a pressor without considering the monitoring and staffing burden it creates.
  • Ignoring peripheral IV site changes during pressor infusion — extravasation can cause rapid tissue injury.
  • Assuming a better blood pressure means the patient is truly improving — track perfusion and trend, not just numbers.
  • Reducing monitoring intensity for a pressor-dependent patient because of staffing fatigue.
  • Failing to communicate the operational sustainability problem to command and teleconsultation early.
Key Takeaways
  • 01Persistent hypotension after initial fluid resuscitation should prompt deliberate reassessment for pressor-dependent shock.
  • 02Starting norepinephrine is both a clinical decision and a systems decision — line security, pump availability, staffing, and reassessment burden all matter.
  • 03A better blood pressure alone is not enough; perfusion and trend determine whether the patient is truly improving.
  • 04Pressor dependence should lower the threshold for early evacuation planning and teleconsultation.
  • 05If evacuation is delayed, convert immediately to a structured prolonged-hold plan with explicit monitoring, documentation, and handover rhythm.
  • 06The right plan is the plan that can be safely maintained by the actual team you have.
Side Notes

A patient who is temporarily supported by pressors is not "solved." They are often more fragile, not less, because the plan now depends on continuous maintenance.

Peripheral norepinephrine may be necessary, but it creates a hidden second emergency risk: extravasation. If the line infiltrates and no one notices, tissue injury can occur quickly and the patient may abruptly lose hemodynamic support.
