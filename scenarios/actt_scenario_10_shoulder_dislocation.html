Shoulder Dislocation During Shipboard Work

Orthopedic Injury Procedural Sedation Neurovascular Exam Reduction Operational Decision

A 29-year-old sailor slips during upper-deck stores handling in moderate sea state, grabs awkwardly with the right arm, and is pulled forward against a rail. He immediately clutches his right shoulder, reports severe pain, and says ‘it popped out.’ The team must assess an isolated but painful musculoskeletal injury without losing sight of hidden red flags, decide whether to reduce onboard or defer, and manage safe analgesia — all while remembering that not every casualty needs maximal intervention or urgent evacuation.

Learning Objectives
  • Run a shipboard scene assessment for an isolated but painful musculoskeletal injury without losing sight of hidden red flags.
  • Decide what must be done on scene before movement and what can safely wait for Sick Bay.
  • Perform and document the pre-reduction neurovascular examination and repeat it after every intervention.
  • Choose between analgesia only, sedation-assisted reduction, or no reduction onboard.
  • Select a reasonable reduction technique for a likely anterior shoulder dislocation and know when to stop.
  • Build a safe onboard plan when immediate MEDEVAC is not required but follow-up still matters.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Upper Deck Injury

At 1540 local, the ship is at sea, several hours from the next port. During routine upper-deck stores handling in moderate sea state, a 29-year-old sailor slips while controlling a load, grabs awkwardly with the right arm, and is pulled forward against a rail. There is no report of direct head strike, loss of consciousness, or chest trauma. He immediately clutches his right shoulder, reports severe pain, and says "it popped out."

Teaching Note
This scenario is intentionally different from the earlier high-acuity cases. Not every casualty needs helicopter hoist or urgent jetty transfer simply because the injury is painful. The teaching point is disciplined restraint — correct diagnosis framing, careful neurovascular checks, controlled analgesia, and safe technique selection.
Operational Note
Confirm this is truly an isolated extremity injury and not part of a broader fall/trauma pattern. Control the environment first: prevent a second fall, clear loose gear. Do a fast ABC check before narrowing onto the shoulder.
STAGE 2 Scene Assessment & Movement Decision

The casualty is standing with assistance, pale and anxious, leaning forward slightly, with the right arm held adducted and protected against the body. He refuses to move the shoulder. There is visible flattening of the normal shoulder contour. No major bleeding is seen. He is breathing comfortably and speaking full sentences.

  • Check distal pulse, perfusion, capillary refill.
  • Assess motor function: hand grip, finger extension.
  • Assess sensation: light touch over deltoid patch and hand (axillary nerve distribution).
  • Support the arm in position of comfort.
  • Document initial neurovascular exam findings.
  • Give simple analgesia if available and appropriate, without creating unnecessary monitoring burden.
Decision Point 1 of 5
The casualty has an obvious shoulder deformity, intact distal pulses, and preserved hand function. He is hemodynamically stable. What is your immediate plan?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. For a stable patient with an isolated shoulder injury, brief on-scene stabilization followed by controlled movement to Sick Bay is the correct approach. The deck is a poor location for sedation or prolonged manipulation. Perform the initial neurovascular exam on scene — this is your baseline.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Getting to Sick Bay quickly is reasonable, but skipping the initial neurovascular exam on scene means you lose the pre-movement baseline. If the neurovascular status changes during transit, you won't know when it happened.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. The upper deck in moderate sea state is a poor environment for reduction attempts, which may require sedation and careful positioning. Move to a controlled environment first.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. An isolated shoulder dislocation with intact neurovascular status is not a helicopter evacuation case. This overreaction uses critical resources unnecessarily and is a key teaching point of this scenario.
Side Note
The most important bedside actions for orthopedic injuries on a ship are: correct diagnosis framing, careful neurovascular checks, controlled analgesia, and safe technique selection. Do not overescalate for a lower-acuity case.
STAGE 3 Sick Bay Assessment & Working Diagnosis

In Sick Bay, the team has a better environment for pain control, monitoring, and a fuller exam. The likely working diagnosis is anterior shoulder dislocation, but the scenario should be taught as a diagnostic checkpoint, not an automatic assumption.

  • Repeat ABCs and obtain baseline vitals.
  • Inspect the shoulder for deformity, swelling, skin tenting, ecchymosis, or open injury.
  • Palpate gently for clavicle, scapular, proximal humerus, and AC joint tenderness.
  • Repeat the neurovascular exam: radial pulse, capillary refill, hand motor function, sensation in axillary nerve distribution and distally.
  • Ask for prior dislocations, prior surgery, connective tissue history, and whether this has reduced spontaneously before.
Decision Point 2 of 5
Sick Bay assessment confirms classic anterior shoulder dislocation with intact neurovascular status. No fracture red flags are identified. What is your management approach?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. A cautious onboard reduction is reasonable when the presentation is classic for uncomplicated anterior dislocation, neurovascular status is intact, the team can monitor safely, and the operator is comfortable with a gentle, lower-force technique.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Immobilization and deferral is a safe option, especially if diagnostic uncertainty remains, sedation risk outweighs benefit, or staffing is thin. The patient tolerating the deformity while awaiting shore assessment is a valid path.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. The Kocher maneuver is specifically rejected in this teaching scenario due to higher complication risk. Use a gentle technique with low complication risk such as external rotation.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Deep dissociative sedation creates a monitoring burden that may exceed what this low-acuity case actually needs. For an uncomplicated shoulder, oversedating the patient can create more risk than the dislocation itself.
⚠ Red-Flag Danger
Red flags that should push AWAY from routine onboard reduction: suspected fracture-dislocation, open injury, absent distal pulse, progressive neurologic deficit, unclear mechanism, or need for sedation that cannot be safely monitored with current staffing or sea conditions.
STAGE 4 Reduction Attempt & Post-Reduction Care

The decision is made to attempt a cautious onboard reduction using external rotation technique. The patient is positioned supine with the elbow flexed to 90 degrees and the arm adducted. Slow, steady external rotation is applied without jerking. After careful, gradual movement, the patient reports sudden pain relief and a palpable relocation is felt.

Decision Point 3 of 5
After successful reduction, the patient reports significant pain relief. What is your immediate priority?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. The post-reduction neurovascular exam is not optional — it must be performed and documented immediately. A new neurologic deficit after reduction is a critical complication. Also reassess for sedation effects if any medications were given.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Immobilization is correct, but delaying the neurovascular exam by 30 minutes risks missing an immediate complication. The exam should happen right after reduction.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Skipping the post-reduction neurovascular exam is a critical error. Return to duties after a dislocation without proper assessment and follow-up planning is unsafe.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Full range-of-motion testing immediately after reduction is painful, unnecessary, and risks re-dislocation. The priority is neurovascular exam, immobilization, and monitoring.
Decision Point 4 of 5
The post-reduction neurovascular exam is intact. The patient is comfortable in a sling. During reduction, you gave titrated analgesia. What do you watch for now?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. Post-procedure monitoring is essential — patients can become quietly more sedated, nauseated, or vasovagal after analgesia and the stress of reduction. Document timing, technique, medications given, and pre/post neurovascular findings.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Pain management and imaging planning are both appropriate, but the immediate priority is ensuring the patient is not having an adverse reaction to medications or the procedure itself.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Sending the patient away without proper monitoring after analgesia administration and a procedural intervention is unsafe. A lower-acuity case still needs a deliberate disposition decision.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Closed reduction of a shoulder dislocation does not require prophylactic antibiotics. This would be an unnecessary intervention.
STAGE 5 Disposition & Follow-Up

The patient has had a successful reduction with intact post-procedure neurovascular status. Pain is controlled. The ship is several hours from port. The team must now make a deliberate disposition decision.

Decision Point 5 of 5
Successful reduction, intact neurovascular exam, pain controlled, stable vitals. The ship is several hours from the next port. What is your disposition plan?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. A successfully reduced dislocation with intact neurovascular status does not require urgent evacuation. The correct plan is continued onboard care with a clear reassessment schedule and arranged follow-up at the next opportunity for imaging.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Requesting transfer at the next port is reasonable and may be appropriate if there is concern about associated fracture or if the patient has recurrent dislocations requiring specialist assessment.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. This is the key teaching point of this scenario: not every painful casualty needs urgent evacuation. An isolated, successfully reduced dislocation with intact neurovascular status does not warrant helicopter resources.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. All dislocations require follow-up imaging to rule out associated fractures and plan ongoing rehabilitation. Failing to arrange follow-up is a disposition error.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Re-dislocation during movement or sleep. Missed associated fracture causing delayed complications. Late-onset neurovascular compromise from swelling or positioning. Medication side effects from analgesia given during reduction. Team dismisses the case as ‘minor’ and skips scheduled reassessments.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Assuming every deformed shoulder is a simple dislocation — failing to consider fracture-dislocation.
  • Failing to document the neurovascular exam before trying to reduce.
  • Using more sedation than the case requires, creating an avoidable airway-monitoring problem.
  • Applying forceful or outdated reduction maneuvers that increase complication risk.
  • Calling the case "minor" and skipping reassessment after the reduction attempt.
  • Failing to recognize that a lower-acuity case still needs a deliberate onboard disposition decision.
  • Requesting urgent evacuation for a stable, successfully reduced dislocation.
Key Takeaways
  • 01This scenario teaches disciplined restraint — not every painful casualty needs maximal intervention or urgent evacuation.
  • 02The most important bedside actions are correct diagnosis framing, careful neurovascular checks, controlled analgesia, and safe technique selection.
  • 03On a ship, the environment determines whether an otherwise simple reduction is safe to attempt onboard.
  • 04For isolated orthopedic problems, the shipboard decision is often about sustainable care and timing of follow-up, not only emergency extraction.
  • 05Document the neurovascular exam before AND after every reduction attempt — this is not optional.
  • 06A lower-acuity case still needs a deliberate disposition plan including reassessment schedule and follow-up arrangements.
