Scenario 08 of 12

Polytrauma With Delayed Evacuation and 24–72 Hour Hold

Multi-System Prolonged Holding Reassessment Rhythm Device Maintenance Team & Documentation

A sailor suffers multi-system trauma in a shipboard industrial accident — head injury, blunt chest trauma, and a long-bone fracture with developing shock. With weather preventing immediate extraction, the team must stabilize at the scene, move deliberately to Sick Bay, and then convert from acute resuscitation into an organized 24–72 hour holding plan with structured reassessment, device maintenance, medication continuity, and disciplined handover.

Learning Objectives
  • Apply an integrated scene-to-Sick Bay approach to a multi-system trauma patient.
  • Prioritize life threats while avoiding fixation on a single injury.
  • Decide what must be done immediately at the scene versus what can wait until movement to Sick Bay.
  • Convert a resuscitated but still unstable casualty into an organized 24–72 hour holding plan when evacuation is delayed.
  • Use structured reassessment, device maintenance, medication continuity, and clear handover in prolonged shipboard care.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Major Shipboard Accident

A 34-year-old sailor is struck by shifting equipment in a machinery-related workspace during heavy weather at sea. Witnesses report the casualty was thrown against a bulkhead, briefly pinned, then collapsed to deck. The patient is found supine, pale, tachypneic, and confused. There is visible bleeding from a scalp laceration, bruising over the left lateral chest, and obvious pain with movement of the right thigh. One witness reports the patient "was talking fine for a minute, then got quieter."

Operational Context
The ship is well outside a rapid shore transfer window. Flight conditions are poor — rotary-wing support is unavailable for several hours. The working assumption is that the patient may need to be held onboard for up to 72 hours. Environment: moving platform, narrow passageways, ladder wells, noise, intermittent space constraints. No definitive trauma bay, limited staffing, finite monitoring continuity.
⚠ Obvious Concerns
Head injury with declining mentation, blunt chest injury with possible pneumothorax or hemothorax, long-bone fracture with hemorrhage contribution, developing shock, and possible respiratory compromise — all in a single casualty requiring simultaneous attention.
Teaching Note
The initial tactical question is: what must be done where the casualty is found, and what can wait until the patient is moved safely to Sick Bay? The wrong extreme is either to rush movement before life threats are addressed, or to build a full treatment bay at the scene while the patient deteriorates in a poor environment. The best answer is usually a short, decisive stabilization phase followed by deliberate movement.
STAGE 2 Initial Stabilization & Problem List

The team takes control of the scene and assigns roles. A primary survey is underway. The airway is currently patent — the patient is speaking but confused. The lateral chest bruising, tachypnea, and reduced SpO₂ may represent pain, pulmonary contusion, developing pneumothorax, or early hemothorax. The right thigh injury raises concern for major femoral bleeding. The team must now prioritize across multiple systems simultaneously.

  • Take control of the scene and assign roles immediately.
  • Perform a primary survey focused on airway, breathing, circulation, disability, and exposure.
  • Control active external bleeding and rapidly identify hidden life threats.
  • Provide oxygen and initiate monitoring as soon as practical.
  • Assess whether the patient can be moved now or needs a life-saving intervention before movement.
  • Auscultate and compare chest rise side to side — be prepared to decompress before movement if marked unilateral reduction with worsening hemodynamics.
  • Stabilize the right thigh with temporary splinting or traction if it can be done quickly and safely.
Teaching Note
The GCS of 12 is not yet an automatic airway trigger, but the trend matters more than the isolated number. Repeat the neurologic check within minutes. If the patient drifts downward, your threshold for early airway planning drops significantly. The scalp wound should be controlled with direct pressure, but it is unlikely to be the main cause of shock — the right thigh and chest are higher concerns.
Decision Point 1 of 5
The casualty has simultaneous head injury, chest trauma, long-bone fracture, and developing shock. How do you approach multi-system prioritization?
✓ Optimal
Correct. In shipboard polytrauma, the first win is not a perfect diagnosis — it is recognition of the problems that can kill the patient first. Build a structured problem list, prioritize life threats, perform on-scene interventions that immediately change survival (oxygen, hemorrhage control, fast primary survey, chest reassessment, rapid splinting), and then move to a better treatment environment. Do not stay on scene for lower-yield tasks that can be done better in Sick Bay.
~ Acceptable
Addressing visible injuries is reasonable and hemorrhage control is critical, but an approach that starts with the most visible problem risks missing the most dangerous one. The chest injury or evolving shock may be the more immediate threat. A systematic primary survey ensures life threats are found in order of lethality, not visibility.
✕ Suboptimal
Single-system fixation is a key pitfall in polytrauma. The head injury matters, but an unrecognized tension pneumothorax or uncontrolled hemorrhage will kill the patient before the brain injury does. The primary survey exists precisely to prevent this tunnel vision — airway, breathing, and circulation must all be assessed before narrowing focus.
✕ Suboptimal
Attempting everything at once without prioritization leads to disorganized care and delayed recognition of the most critical problem. In a resource-limited shipboard environment with a small team, splitting attention equally across all systems means nothing gets done well. Prioritization — not parallel chaos — is what saves polytrauma patients.
STAGE 3 Transition to Organized Holding

The casualty is moved to Sick Bay. During movement, the patient becomes more drowsy — GCS drops to 9 (E2 V2 M5), SpO₂ 90% despite oxygen, HR 136, BP 92/58. In Sick Bay, the team repeats the primary survey, re-documents mental status and pupils, reassesses chest findings, establishes durable IV access, and begins a focused secondary survey. The working problem list is now clear: traumatic brain injury with falling GCS, blunt chest trauma with risk of pneumothorax or hemothorax, likely long-bone fracture with hemorrhage, and shock. Weather confirms evacuation is not possible for the foreseeable future. The team must now decide when and how to convert from acute response to prolonged care.

Teaching Note
This is the inflection point. The question is no longer whether evacuation is ideal — it is whether the patient can survive the delay. Once delayed evacuation is clear, the team must stop improvising and deliberately enter prolonged-care mode. Continuing to operate in pure acute resuscitation mode indefinitely exhausts the team, wastes resources, and loses the structure needed for a 24–72 hour hold.
Decision Point 2 of 5
Acute stabilization is underway but evacuation is confirmed delayed for up to 72 hours. When and how do you convert from acute response to prolonged care?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Converting to prolonged care requires a deliberate, explicit transition. The hold conversion checklist includes: defining the active problem list, setting explicit targets, deciding what requires continuous versus scheduled monitoring, establishing medication timing and device checks, creating a documentation rhythm, and defining deterioration triggers. This structure is what prevents a "rescued" patient from quietly deteriorating hours later.
~ Acceptable
A gradual transition is better than no transition, but without a formal structure the team risks losing track of scheduled reassessments, medication timing, and device checks. The explicit conversion point forces the team to acknowledge they are now in a different mode of care with different demands.
✕ Suboptimal
Maintaining crisis-level intensity for 24–72 hours is unsustainable. The team will exhaust itself, documentation will suffer, handover will be chaotic, and critical reassessments will be missed when fatigue sets in. Prolonged care requires a sustainable rhythm, not indefinite emergency mode.
✕ Suboptimal
Initial stabilization is not the same as durable stability. A temporary improvement in blood pressure can create false reassurance. Unrecognized worsening pneumothorax, delayed neurologic decline, ongoing occult hemorrhage, and device failures can all develop insidiously during a hold. Reducing vigilance because the first hour went well is a key pitfall.
STAGE 4 Prolonged Hold — 24–72 Hours

Hours into the hold, the team must maintain structured care across multiple systems simultaneously. The airway strategy depends on whether the patient was intubated (tube security, sedation, ventilation targets, ongoing confirmation) or remains spontaneously breathing (repeated reassessment mandatory — a falling GCS can become an airway emergency hours into the hold if the team is complacent). Perfusion must be trended — every fluid decision linked to response. Serial neuro checks track GCS trend, pupils, and gross motor response. And every tube, line, drain, and drug creates an ongoing maintenance burden.

Airway Strategy During the Hold
If intubated: tube security, post-intubation sedation, suction access, ventilation targets, and ongoing confirmation become major burdens. If not yet intubated: repeated reassessment is mandatory. Track SpO₂ and work of breathing continuously. Use EtCO₂ if available. For suspected TBI, avoid hypoxia and keep ventilation controlled — reserve hyperventilation for brief rescue use only if herniation is suspected.
Perfusion Strategy During the Hold
Trend blood pressure, heart rate, mental status, skin signs, and urine output. Do not keep giving blind boluses — every fluid decision must be linked to response. If the patient remains hypotensive after reasonable resuscitation, ask whether the problem is ongoing bleeding, occult thoracic compromise, neurogenic issues, or a need for more advanced support. Reassess after each bolus. Avoid worsening oxygenation with uncontrolled fluid loading. For suspected TBI, support systolic BP above 110 mmHg if possible.
Decision Point 3 of 5
You are now 6 hours into the hold. How do you structure reassessment rhythm and monitoring for a multi-system trauma patient held for up to 72 hours?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Structured reassessment at defined intervals is the backbone of prolonged care. Serial neuro checks should include GCS trend, pupils, and gross motor response. Trend data, documented on a flowsheet, is what reveals deterioration before it becomes obvious. Escalation criteria defined in advance prevent hesitation when things worsen — the team knows exactly what triggers a call for help.
~ Acceptable
Periodic reassessment is better than none, but without a schedule, checks drift as the team fatigues. "Every hour or so" becomes every two hours, then every three. Subtle deterioration — a slowly dropping GCS, a gradually rising heart rate, a quietly worsening chest exam — gets missed when reassessment depends on someone remembering to do it.
✕ Suboptimal
Reactive monitoring is a key failure mode in prolonged care. By the time a polytrauma patient visibly worsens, the window for early intervention has already closed. Neurologic decline, evolving hemothorax, and worsening shock all present subtly before they present dramatically. The whole point of structured reassessment is to detect trends before they become crises.
✕ Suboptimal
Over-frequent checks exhaust the team without adding clinical benefit. A small shipboard medical team cannot sustain 5-minute assessments for 72 hours. Exhaustion leads to errors, missed findings, and handover failures — the very problems structured reassessment is designed to prevent. The rhythm must be frequent enough to catch trends but sustainable enough to maintain for days.

Beyond reassessment rhythm, the team must manage every device and medication as an ongoing task. Every tube, line, drain, and drug creates a maintenance burden that must be planned for immediately — not discovered when something fails.

Neurologic Strategy During the Hold
Document time and direction of each neurologic change. If the patient worsens, think first about hypoxia, hypotension, expanding intracranial injury, seizure, or missed chest/circulatory deterioration. Do not assume sedation or fatigue explains every mental-status change. If impending herniation signs appear, apply brief rescue measures while escalating urgently.
Decision Point 4 of 5
You have IV lines, a possible chest tube, a urinary catheter, splinting, and multiple medications running. How do you manage device maintenance and medication continuity during the prolonged hold?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Every device and medication is an ongoing task, not a one-time intervention. Chest tubes can kink, occlude, or dislodge. IOs can extravasate. Foleys can obstruct. Medications can be missed or duplicated during handover. A structured check integrated into each reassessment cycle — with fluids in/out logged, medication timing documented, and temperature and repositioning managed — prevents the slow failures that turn a stable hold into a crisis.
~ Acceptable
Checking devices during assessments is better than ignoring them, but without a formal tracking system, medication timing drifts and fluid balance becomes unknowable. Over 24–72 hours, "general awareness" degrades as fatigue accumulates and team members rotate. A simple written log or checklist prevents the errors that informal awareness cannot.
✕ Suboptimal
Device failure is one of the most common causes of deterioration during prolonged holds. Line infiltration, chest tube dysfunction, dislodgement during repositioning, and Foley obstruction all happen silently. Assuming devices are "fine" because they were placed correctly is a setup for an avoidable crisis hours later.
✕ Suboptimal
Completely separating device management from clinical assessment disconnects the team. A chest tube output change is a clinical finding. A medication timing error affects the neurologic exam. Device and medication management must be integrated into the clinical reassessment cycle, not siloed. On a small team, everyone must share awareness of the full picture.
STAGE 5 Handover & Team Management

A 24–72 hour hold requires team rotation. No single provider can maintain effective care for that duration without rest. The question is not whether handover will happen, but whether it will be structured or chaotic. Polytrauma cases fail quickly when the team has no shared mental model. One person must hold the plan and keep stating it.

  • State the working problem list out loud at every handover.
  • Assign who is responsible for airway watch, medication timing, trend documentation, and device checks.
  • Use closed-loop communication for all critical medications and procedures.
  • Document what was done, when it was done, and what changed afterward.
  • When calling for teleconsultation or renewed evacuation support, present a concise timeline, current status, and what may fail next.
Teaching Note
Documentation and handover failures during a long hold are among the most common reasons a stabilized patient quietly deteriorates. The information that matters most at handover is: what is the current problem list, what is the trend, what was last done and when, what is due next, and what would trigger escalation. If the receiving team member cannot answer all five of these questions after handover, the handover was incomplete.
Decision Point 5 of 5
You are 18 hours into the hold and need to hand over care to a rested team member for a scheduled rest period. How do you structure the handover?
✓ Optimal
Correct. A structured bedside handover ensures the incoming team member sees the patient, hears the plan, reviews the devices, understands the trends, and knows exactly what to do next and when to escalate. The read-back confirmation closes the loop. This is the standard that prevents the slow information loss that turns a stable hold into a quiet disaster.
~ Acceptable
A verbal summary is better than nothing, but moving handover away from the bedside loses the opportunity to physically review devices, point to trend data, and confirm the incoming provider's understanding in context. Relying on independent documentation review introduces a gap — the incoming team member may not know what to look for or what the documentation doesn't capture.
✕ Suboptimal
Written documentation alone is never sufficient for a complex polytrauma hold. Notes cannot convey clinical gestalt, subtle trends the outgoing provider noticed but didn't write down, or the "what I'm most worried about" context that drives appropriate vigilance. Verbal handover at the bedside is not redundant — it is essential.
✕ Suboptimal
A fatigued provider makes errors. After 18+ hours of managing a critically ill polytrauma patient, cognitive function degrades significantly. The continuity argument sounds appealing, but a rested provider with a good handover will outperform an exhausted provider with perfect continuity. Team sustainability is a clinical decision, not an administrative one.
STAGE 6 Evacuation Trigger Recognition

Throughout the prolonged hold, the team must continuously evaluate whether the patient's condition demands renewed urgency for evacuation. The fact that a hold was initiated does not mean it should continue indefinitely. Certain developments should trigger immediate re-escalation.

⚠ Evacuation Escalation Triggers
Progressive loss of airway as GCS falls further. Unrecognized worsening pneumothorax or hemothorax after partial initial improvement. Shock worsens because a temporary improvement in blood pressure created false reassurance. Delayed neurologic decline including worsening pupils or seizure activity. Pain and agitation become harder to control, increasing monitoring burden. Device failure: line infiltration, chest tube dysfunction, dislodgement during movement, Foley obstruction.
Teaching Note
When calling for teleconsultation or renewed evacuation, present a concise timeline of what happened, the current status including all vital sign trends, the active problem list, what interventions have been performed and their results, and — critically — what you believe may fail next. This last element is what separates a report from a request: it tells the receiving team why urgency has changed.
Preparation for Extraction
If a hoist becomes available: stabilize only what is necessary to survive packaging and transfer. Secure the airway if loss is likely before or during extraction. Decompress the chest before transfer if indicated. Control bleeding, stabilize long-bone injury as feasible, and communicate the exact current trend to the receiving team. Every device must be secured for movement — tube dislodgement during extraction can be fatal.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Fixating on one injury system while others deteriorate unrecognized.
  • Staying in acute resuscitation mode indefinitely instead of converting to structured prolonged care.
  • Relaxing monitoring because initial stabilization went well — a temporary BP improvement creates false reassurance.
  • Only reassessing when the patient visibly worsens — subtle trends are missed until crisis arrives.
  • Neglecting device maintenance — chest tube kinks, IO extravasation, Foley obstruction develop silently.
  • Pain control neglected because case acuity is high — untreated pain worsens agitation, O₂ demand, and handling.
  • Documentation and handover failures during the long hold — information loss across team rotations.
  • Exhausting the team with unsustainable monitoring intensity rather than building a rhythm that lasts 72 hours.
Key Takeaways
  • 01In shipboard polytrauma, the first win is not a perfect diagnosis — it is recognition of the problems that can kill the patient first.
  • 02Do only the on-scene interventions that change survival, then move deliberately to the place where you can do more safely.
  • 03A falling GCS in trauma is a trend problem — do not wait for complete collapse before preparing for airway control.
  • 04If evacuation is delayed, do not keep operating in pure resuscitation mode. Convert early to an explicit holding plan.
  • 05Every tube, line, drain, and drug creates an ongoing maintenance burden. Plan for that burden immediately.
  • 06Trend data, structured reassessment, and disciplined handover are what keep a "rescued" patient from quietly deteriorating hours later.
Medication Notes

Do not let pain control be neglected just because the case is high acuity. Untreated pain worsens agitation, oxygen demand, and handling difficulty. However, every sedative or opioid choice must be linked to airway monitoring and repeated reassessment, especially when the neurologic exam matters.

In a prolonged hold, medication continuity matters as much as initial medication choice. Likely needs include analgesia for long-bone injury and overall trauma burden, sedation if intubated or severely agitated, RSI medications if airway control is performed, ICP-directed therapy only if clinical deterioration justifies it, and antiemetic and supportive medications as needed.
