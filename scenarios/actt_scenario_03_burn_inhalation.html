Major Burn With Inhalation Risk in a Shipboard Fire

Burns Airway Burn Risk Rule of 10s Fluid Titration Prolonged Hold

During a machinery-space fire at sea, a sailor suffers facial burns with soot inhalation, partial-thickness burns across the chest, neck, arms, and hands, and a rapidly worsening airway. The team must decide when to secure the airway before edema makes it impossible, initiate burn fluid resuscitation using the Rule of 10s, manage pain in a critically burned casualty, and plan evacuation or prolonged holding — all within a shipboard environment hours from definitive care.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize when initial fire-scene actions must happen before movement to Sick Bay.
  • Identify airway warning signs that support early definitive airway planning in burns and smoke exposure.
  • Use the Rule of 10s as an initial fluid starting point, then adjust using urine output and clinical response.
  • Decide between immediate local transfer, hoist extraction, or prolonged shipboard holding.
  • Anticipate the ongoing workload created by a burned, potentially intubated casualty over the next several hours.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Shipboard Fire Casualty

The ship is at sea in cold conditions, several hours from a practical port diversion. During a small machinery-space fire, a sailor is exposed to heat and smoke while assisting with boundary cooling before the space is made safe. He is removed from the compartment and found sitting against a bulkhead, anxious, in pain, and coughing black-tinged sputum. A damage-control sailor and another crew member are already with him. You are called to the scene with a portable medical bag, oxygen, and radio support, but the full Sick Bay setup is not yet at the casualty location.

The casualty is alert but frightened, speaking in short sentences. Facial burns with singed nasal hairs and soot around the mouth are immediately visible. His voice is hoarse with a persistent cough and increasing throat discomfort. Partial-thickness burns are observed across the anterior chest, upper arms, neck, and scattered across both hands. He is progressively more restless and no obvious major external hemorrhage is seen.

Teaching Note
At this stage, the wrong move is to focus on dressings first while underestimating the airway. Burn dressings matter, but in an airway-threatening burn casualty, airway trajectory is often the highest-risk problem. The combination of facial burns, soot, hoarseness, and cough strongly raises concern for inhalation injury. You are trying to answer one question early: is this a patient whose airway may be significantly worse by the time he reaches Sick Bay?
Operational Note
The fire scene environment limits sustained care. If the route to Sick Bay is practical and the patient can be moved with oxygen and monitoring support, the preferred approach is brief scene stabilization followed by prompt movement. Burned casualties lose temperature control quickly — expose only as needed for assessment, then protect from heat loss. Assign roles before moving: one at the airway/head, one managing oxygen/monitoring, one managing navigation, one additional helper if available.
STAGE 2 Early Airway Decision

The team packages and begins moving the casualty toward Sick Bay with oxygen and airway access preserved. Halfway through the route, the patient becomes more hoarse, develops louder coughing, and now struggles to speak full phrases. SpO₂ drops to 91% despite supplemental oxygen. He is more agitated and repeatedly reaches toward his neck and mask.

On arrival in Sick Bay, the casualty is placed on the monitor and exposed for a more deliberate assessment. Estimated burn size is roughly 24% TBSA involving anterior chest, neck, bilateral upper arms, and both hands. He now has inspiratory noise, worsening hoarseness, and persistent soot-stained secretions.

⚠ Inflection Point
This is the central teaching decision. The patient is not yet in extremis, but the pattern strongly supports progressive airway edema risk: facial burns, soot inhalation, worsening hoarseness, increasing work of breathing, and expected delayed edema. In many burn casualties, the best time to intubate is before the airway becomes anatomically difficult. Waiting for obvious collapse is unsafe.
  • Reassess airway aggressively — this patient has progressive signs of inhalation injury.
  • Establish vascular access early: large-bore IV preferred; IO acceptable if IV is delayed and patient is deteriorating.
  • Begin analgesia cautiously — any sedative or opioid must be paired with airway monitoring readiness.
  • Prepare full RSI setup: suction, oxygen, ETT, bougie, confirmation tools, backup airway devices, and surgical airway kit.
Decision Point 1 of 5
The patient has facial burns, soot inhalation, progressive hoarseness, inspiratory noise, and worsening work of breathing. Do you intubate now or continue to monitor?
✓ Optimal
Correct. The safer teaching answer is early intubation now, before swelling progresses. The supporting signs are facial burns, soot, hoarseness, worsening voice, increasing work of breathing, and expected delayed edema. The expected sequence is: pre-brief the team, prepare full RSI setup with backup plan stated aloud, pre-oxygenate, perform RSI, confirm placement with EtCO₂, secure the tube with cotton ties (standard adhesive tape is unreliable on burned tissue), and start post-intubation sedation immediately. A technically successful intubation without sustained sedation creates awareness, agitation, and high risk of accidental extubation.
~ Acceptable
Preparing all equipment and monitoring closely is reasonable as a very brief bridge, but the trajectory is already clear. The risk is that the airway worsens rapidly during the monitoring period, converting a controlled intubation into a crisis. If this approach is taken, the threshold to proceed must be extremely low and the team must be fully ready.
✕ Suboptimal
In burn casualties, the airway can look acceptable just before it becomes difficult. Waiting for obvious collapse is the primary teaching hazard of this scenario. Trend and anticipation matter more than a single reassuring moment. By the time the patient visibly decompensates, the anatomy may already be too swollen for a safe intubation.
✕ Suboptimal
Burn dressings matter, but airway trajectory is the highest-risk problem. Dressings should not delay airway management. A patient with progressive inhalation injury can lose the airway while the team focuses on wounds. Airway before edema is the major procedural lesson.
Intubation Walkthrough
Step 1: Pre-brief the team — state the indication is progressive airway threat, not current complete obstruction. Name operator, medication support, monitor/recorder, and backup plan.
Step 2: Full RSI setup — suction, O₂, ETT, bougie, confirmation tools, backup devices, surgical airway kit immediately available.
Step 3: Pre-oxygenate and monitor closely — use this oxygenation window.
Step 4: Perform RSI per protocol. If difficulty is encountered, do not burn through repeated attempts while swelling worsens.
Step 5: Confirm with EtCO₂ and clinical confirmation. Secure with cotton ties — not adhesive tape on burned skin.
Step 6: Start post-intubation sedation and analgesia immediately.
STAGE 3 Burn Fluid Resuscitation

With the airway now addressed, the team turns to burn resuscitation. The estimated TBSA is 24%. The patient has been tachycardic and borderline hypotensive since the scene, and tissue edema is expected to worsen over the coming hours. Without adequate fluid resuscitation, burn shock will develop. But unmonitored over-resuscitation carries its own dangers — worsening edema, harder ventilation, and harder transfer.

Rule of 10s — Quick Reference
The Rule of 10s provides a practical starting point for burn fluid resuscitation in adults 40–80 kg: TBSA% × 10 = initial mL/hr. For this patient: 24% × 10 = 240 mL/hr of isotonic crystalloid as the starting rate. This is a starting point only, not a fixed order. The formula must be adjusted based on clinical response — primarily urine output.
  • Start isotonic crystalloid at the calculated initial rate (240 mL/hr for 24% TBSA).
  • Place Foley catheter so urine output becomes the main bedside resuscitation guide.
  • Target urine output of 30–50 mL/hr in an adult as the primary endpoint.
  • Adjust rate in roughly 25% increments up or down if output is persistently low or high.
  • Watch the neck and chest burns for progressive tightness — circumferential compromise becomes a procedural problem if missed early.
Decision Point 2 of 5
The patient has ~24% TBSA burns and is tachycardic with borderline hypotension. How do you initiate burn fluid resuscitation?
✓ Optimal
Correct. The Rule of 10s gives a practical starting rate. Placing a Foley early is essential — without urine output data, fluid titration becomes much less reliable. The formula is a starting point, not a fixed rate. Titrate based on response: increase by ~25% if urine output is persistently low, decrease by ~25% if persistently high. Avoid fluid creep — burn casualties can become more edematous, harder to ventilate, and harder to transfer if fluid is poured in without repeated reassessment.
~ Acceptable
Starting fluids is correct, but without a calculated starting rate and a Foley for urine output monitoring, the risk of both under- and over-resuscitation increases. Clinical appearance alone is insufficient to guide burn fluid titration over hours. A structured approach with measurable endpoints is preferred.
✕ Suboptimal
Aggressive wide-open boluses without monitoring create fluid overload risk. Burn casualties can become progressively more edematous, harder to ventilate, and significantly harder to transfer. The Parkland formula and Rule of 10s exist specifically because uncontrolled resuscitation causes harm. Fluid creep is a recognized and dangerous complication.
✕ Suboptimal
Withholding fluids in a 24% TBSA burn is dangerous. Without resuscitation, burn shock will develop and compound the existing hemodynamic instability. The concern about edema is valid but is managed through titration, not fluid avoidance. Under-resuscitation is immediately life-threatening in major burns.
Fluid Creep Warning
Burn casualties can become more edematous, harder to ventilate, and harder to transfer if fluid is poured in without repeated reassessment. Use urine output as your primary guide. The formula is a starting point — not a fixed order for the next several hours.
STAGE 4 Sick Bay Management — Pain, Sedation & Prolonged Hold

The airway is secured and fluids are running at the calculated rate. However, the work is far from over. The patient requires continuous sedation and analgesia, the tube must remain secured on burned tissue, urine output needs repeated reassessment, and the team must plan for what may become a prolonged holding situation lasting up to 72 hours if weather or distance prevent timely evacuation.

Medication Integration
Analgesia: Even intubated burn patients require ongoing analgesia — sedation alone is not pain control. Low-dose ketamine is often attractive for its analgesic properties without the same hypotension risk. Opioids may be used but their respiratory burden must be weighed if the airway were not yet secured.
Sedation: Post-intubation sedation should be continuous or deliberately re-dosed on schedule. Do not wait for severe agitation to restart sedation.
Supportive: Ondansetron for nausea. If soot and secretion burden are high, suction planning matters as much as drug choice.
Decision Point 3 of 5
The patient is intubated with a secured airway. How do you manage pain control, tube security, and ongoing monitoring in a burn casualty with damaged skin?
✓ Optimal
Correct. A burn casualty who is intubated but poorly sedated is not stabilized — the plan is incomplete. Continuous or scheduled analgesia addresses pain, not just awareness. Tube securement with ties rather than adhesive is critical on weeping or burned skin. Hourly urine output guides ongoing fluid titration, and regular reassessment of ventilation and burn progression catches complications before they become emergencies.
~ Acceptable
PRN pain management and intermittent monitoring are better than nothing, but a burn patient requires structured, scheduled care. Waiting for breakthrough pain or agitation before medicating creates cycles of distress and risk. Structured intervals for reassessment and medication dosing are more reliable.
✕ Suboptimal
Securing the airway is a step, not the end of management. The intubated burn patient has ongoing needs: sedation, analgesia, fluid titration, ventilation monitoring, tube security checks, temperature management, and complication surveillance. Focusing only on wound care neglects the most dangerous ongoing risks.
✕ Suboptimal
Deep unmonitored sedation carries its own dangers — hypotension, loss of protective reflexes, and masking of clinical changes. The goal is adequate, titrated sedation and analgesia, not chemical paralysis of all awareness. Sedation must be balanced against hemodynamic stability and the ability to detect clinical deterioration.

Hours pass with no realistic evacuation window. Sea state and distance prevent helicopter hoist, and port diversion is not practical. The scenario converts into a prolonged-hold burn case — potentially up to 72 hours. This immediately raises the team's workload and forces structured planning.

Decision Point 4 of 5
Evacuation is delayed up to 72 hours. How does the team manage the prolonged holding burden for this critically burned, intubated casualty?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Prolonged holding of a major burn patient is survivable, but only if the team treats it as an active care plan, not a waiting period. Structured reassessment rhythm includes: urine-output-guided fluid titration, sedation and analgesia continuity, airway and device security checks, temperature control, wound reassessment, and teleconsultation for remote expert guidance. Document exact times and trends for handover.
~ Acceptable
Frequent checks and adjustments are better than passive observation, but without a structured schedule, critical tasks can be missed — especially across crew watch rotations. Explicit reassessment intervals and documented checklists reduce drift and omission during prolonged holding.
✕ Suboptimal
A major burn patient's condition evolves continuously. Fluid needs change, sedation wears off, tubes can loosen on burned skin, edema progresses, and temperature regulation fails. Reactive management that waits for visible change misses the gradual drift that causes preventable deterioration.
✕ Suboptimal
Reducing monitoring is the opposite of what prolonged holding requires. The workload increases, not decreases, when evacuation is delayed. Crew fatigue is real, but the answer is structured watch rotations, not reduced vigilance. A burned, intubated casualty left under-monitored for hours will deteriorate.
STAGE 5 Evacuation & Transfer Decision

Weather has improved enough that helicopter hoist extraction is now feasible, though conditions remain demanding. The casualty is intubated, on IV fluids with Foley monitoring, and receiving continuous sedation and analgesia. The team must now decide on the evacuation strategy — considering that a hoist is not just a transport issue but increases the importance of tube security, sedation continuity, and concise handover.

Evacuation Branches
Branch A — Alongside: If alongside with ambulance available, priority is early transfer after initial stabilization. Do not delay for low-yield onboard tasks.
Branch B — Helicopter Hoist: Hoist adds time, movement, and equipment-security demands. Package the casualty to tolerate movement with all devices secured. Sedation continuity and tube security become even more critical.
Branch C — Prolonged Hold (up to 72 hr): If extraction remains impossible, maintain the active care plan. This branch is survivable only if treated as an ongoing management problem, not a waiting period.
Decision Point 5 of 5
Helicopter extraction is now available. The patient is intubated with ongoing IV resuscitation and sedation. What is your evacuation strategy?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Hoist preparation requires deliberate packaging: all airway devices, oxygen, lines, and dressings must be secured for vertical and lateral movement. Sedation must be adequate to tolerate the stress of transfer. A concise handover with estimated TBSA, current airway status, current fluid rate, urine output trend, vitals, and realistic shipboard limits enables the receiving team to continue care seamlessly. Documentation of exact times, medications, and trend changes is essential.
~ Acceptable
Speed is important, but rushing transfer preparation risks device dislodgement, inadequate sedation during hoist, and a poor handover. A few extra minutes of deliberate packaging and structured handover significantly reduce complications during and after extraction.
✕ Suboptimal
A major burn patient with inhalation injury needs definitive burn center care that cannot be provided onboard. Every additional hour of holding increases the cumulative risk of complications. When extraction becomes available, it should be used — the shipboard team's job is to stabilize and transfer, not to provide definitive burn management.
✕ Suboptimal
Controlling the airway is not the end of the urgency — it is a stabilization step. Wound care optimization before transfer is a low-yield reason to delay extraction in a major burn with inhalation injury. The patient needs a burn center, and the weather window may not last. Delaying for non-essential tasks risks losing the evacuation opportunity.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Delayed airway edema if the team waited too long to intubate. Accidental extubation if the tube is secured poorly on burned skin. Fluid overload with worsening edema and ventilation burden if the formula is treated as fixed. Under-resuscitation if urine output is not tracked or the Foley is nonfunctional. Pain and agitation causing device dislodgement or loss of team control. Heat loss and secondary physiologic deterioration if exposure is not managed.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Waiting too long to secure the airway — the airway can look acceptable just before it becomes impossible.
  • Focusing on burn dressings while underestimating the progressive airway threat.
  • Treating the Rule of 10s as a fixed fluid order rather than a starting point requiring titration.
  • Fluid overload from unmonitored resuscitation, worsening edema and ventilation burden.
  • Under-resuscitation because urine output is not tracked or the Foley is not placed.
  • Securing the ETT with adhesive tape on burned or weeping skin, leading to accidental extubation.
  • Providing sedation without analgesia — an intubated burn patient is not pain-free just because they are sedated.
  • Treating prolonged holding as a waiting period rather than an active care plan.
  • Delaying evacuation for low-yield wound care when a transfer window is available.
Key Takeaways
  • 01Recognize inhalation injury early and decide on airway control before edema worsens — the best time to intubate is before the airway becomes anatomically difficult.
  • 02Use rapid scene priorities, then move deliberately to the best treatment location without unnecessary delay.
  • 03Start burn fluids with a formula (Rule of 10s), then let urine output and clinical response drive adjustments.
  • 04Plan evacuation and prolonged holding early — the transport branch changes the workload immediately.
  • 05In shipboard burn care, anticipation prevents the most dangerous failures. Trend matters more than any single reassuring moment.
  • 06Movement on the ship should be thought of as a medical intervention with risks, not just a transport chore.
Teaching Pearls

In burn cases, the airway can look acceptable just before it becomes difficult. That is why trend and anticipation matter more than a single reassuring moment.

The Rule of 10s is a practical entry point, not a substitute for reassessment. A burn casualty who is intubated but poorly sedated is not stabilized — the plan is incomplete.

If consulting remotely, have ready: estimated TBSA, airway findings, current airway status, current fluid rate, urine output trend, vitals, and realistic shipboard limits.
