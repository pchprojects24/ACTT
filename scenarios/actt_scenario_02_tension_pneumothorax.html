Chest Trauma With Suspected Tension Pneumothorax

Thoracic Trauma Needle Decompression Chest Tube Movement Risk Evacuation Decision

A sailor suffers blunt chest trauma on the upper deck when struck by shifting equipment. The casualty rapidly develops signs of tension pneumothorax with obstructive shock. The team must decide whether to decompress on scene before movement, execute needle thoracostomy correctly, transition to definitive chest tube management in Sick Bay, and plan evacuation — all while managing the risk of recurrent tension physiology during every phase of care and transport.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize the clinical pattern of tension pneumothorax in an unstable casualty without waiting for perfect confirmation.
  • Decide what must be done on scene before movement and what can wait until arrival in Sick Bay.
  • Execute immediate needle thoracostomy logic, then transition to definitive chest tube planning.
  • Plan safe movement of an unstable casualty across the ship using available carry systems.
  • Choose among jetty transfer, helicopter hoist, or short shipboard holding based on response to treatment and operational constraints.
  • Identify common failure points, including recurrent tension physiology after temporary improvement.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Chest Trauma on Upper Deck

A sailor is struck in the right lateral chest by shifting equipment during deck work on the upper deck. The ship is at sea in moderate swell, not within immediate helicopter landing capability — the flight deck is red deck only, and helicopter extraction is possible only by hoist. The casualty is found seated against the bulkhead, anxious, tachypneic, clutching the right chest, and stating, "I cannot catch my breath." The assisting sailor reports the casualty was initially talking normally but became visibly more distressed over the last two to three minutes.

Teaching Note
This is a clinical diagnosis. In an unstable casualty with compatible mechanism and findings — severe shortness of breath, unilateral absent breath sounds, tachycardia with weak pulse and falling blood pressure, and distended neck veins — you do not wait for imaging, auscultation perfection, or transport to Sick Bay before deciding whether decompression is required. Agitation may reflect hypoxia, rising intrathoracic pressure, pain, or impending decompensation.
Operational Note
Available at scene: one assisting sailor, basic first-response kit, radio contact, oxygen cylinder that can be brought, and Stokes litter plus medsled if movement is required. Sick Bay has fuller monitoring and procedural capability, including decompression supplies and chest tube setup. Ensure the scene is safe — shifting gear and ongoing deck hazards must be controlled before clinical care begins.
STAGE 2 Scene Recognition & Decompression Decision

The scene is controlled and you have established yourself as clinical lead. The casualty continues to deteriorate — breathing is more laboured, agitation is worsening, and the radial pulse is becoming harder to palpate. The assisting sailor has radioed for oxygen and the decompression kit. The transport route to Sick Bay involves ladders and narrow corridors. You must now decide: treat the chest on scene, or move first?

  • Perform a rapid ABC check with primary emphasis on airway patency, ability to oxygenate, and signs of obstructive shock.
  • Call for immediate assistance to bring oxygen, decompression kit, and movement equipment.
  • Place the casualty on supplemental oxygen as soon as it is physically possible.
  • Decide whether tension physiology is present strongly enough that decompression must occur before movement.
  • State aloud: "This may be tension pneumothorax with shock. Prepare immediate decompression."
Decision Point 1 of 5
The casualty has unilateral absent breath sounds, tachycardia, falling blood pressure, and distended neck veins after blunt chest trauma. The transport route is long and awkward. What is your immediate approach?
✓ Optimal
Correct. This casualty should be treated on scene first if the decompression kit can be brought quickly. The combination of respiratory distress, unilateral findings, and shock indicators makes suspected tension pneumothorax the immediate life threat. Needle thoracostomy is a rapid, high-yield intervention that should not be delayed simply to relocate the patient. If the casualty is deteriorating rapidly and the transport route is long or awkward, decompression may be the intervention that makes movement survivable.
~ Acceptable
This may seem faster if Sick Bay is close and the crew are already mobilized, but performing a needle decompression during movement through ladders and narrow corridors is technically difficult and risky. Ideally, decompress on scene where you can landmark carefully, then move a marginally more stable casualty. However, preparing equipment during movement is acceptable if the scene truly cannot support the procedure.
✕ Suboptimal
Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis in an unstable casualty. Waiting for imaging confirmation is a classic and dangerous error. The casualty may arrest or worsen during transfer. In an unstable patient with compatible mechanism and findings, you do not wait for X-ray before deciding whether decompression is required. Moving first without treating the chest can convert a critically ill casualty into an arrest during transport.
✕ Suboptimal
While oxygen is essential and should be applied as soon as possible, it does not treat the underlying mechanical problem. In tension pneumothorax, rising intrathoracic pressure compresses the mediastinum and impairs venous return — this is obstructive shock physiology, not simply a ventilation problem. Oxygen alone will not reverse the hemodynamic collapse. Decompression is the intervention that addresses the life threat.
Side Note
A common but unsafe tendency when uncertain is to attempt prolonged assessment before deciding. This is dangerous because tension physiology progresses rapidly. The distinction here is between two errors: waiting too long to decompress, and moving an unstable casualty without addressing the immediately life-threatening condition first.
STAGE 3 Needle Decompression & Movement

The decompression kit and oxygen have arrived. High-flow oxygen is applied. You have confirmed the affected side clinically and are preparing for needle thoracostomy. The assisting sailor is preparing the Stokes litter for movement once the procedure is complete. The team is aligned and waiting for your lead.

  • Expose and confirm the affected side — avoid wrong-side decompression.
  • Landmark carefully: 2nd intercostal space mid-clavicular line or 4th/5th intercostal space anterior/mid-axillary line.
  • Use a 14-gauge, 3.25-inch catheter — insert over the superior rib margin to reduce neurovascular injury risk.
  • Reassess immediately: look for air release, improved work of breathing, improved pulse strength, improved mentation, and improved blood pressure.
  • If little or no improvement: recheck landmarking, consider catheter failure, consider bilateral injury, broaden differential to include hemothorax or cardiac tamponade.
Decision Point 2 of 5
You are performing needle thoracostomy on scene. What is your approach to the procedure and immediate reassessment?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Either the 2nd intercostal space mid-clavicular line or the 4th/5th intercostal space anterior/mid-axillary line is acceptable depending on access and body habitus — in many adults, the lateral approach is more practical and reliable. Using a 14-gauge, 3.25-inch catheter inserted over the superior rib margin reduces neurovascular injury risk. Immediate reassessment is critical: look for air release, improved work of breathing, improved pulse strength, improved mentation, and improved blood pressure. If there is little or no improvement, do not assume the problem is solved.
~ Acceptable
Correct landmarking and technique is the essential step, but skipping the immediate reassessment is a significant gap. A temporary catheter can kink, fail, or only partially decompress. Without structured reassessment, you may not recognize that the intervention has failed or that the casualty has an additional pathology such as hemothorax or cardiac tamponade. Always pause briefly to confirm effect before committing to movement.
✕ Suboptimal
A posterior approach is not the standard landmark for needle thoracostomy. The correct sites are the 2nd intercostal space at the mid-clavicular line or the 4th/5th intercostal space at the anterior or mid-axillary line. Wrong-site decompression is a recognized procedural error that wastes time and may injure underlying structures without resolving the tension physiology.
✕ Suboptimal
This casualty has established tension physiology with shock. Moving without decompressing is the intervention most likely to convert a critically ill casualty into an arrest during transport. The decision to decompress on scene has already been made — the decompression kit is present and the procedure takes only moments. Delaying for better conditions risks losing the patient entirely during the transfer.
Teaching Note
Needle thoracostomy is a temporizing intervention, not a definitive fix. A successful decompression should trigger definitive planning, not false reassurance. The catheter may kink, fail, or only partially decompress — the patient may worsen again. In real shipboard movement, the procedure is not over once the catheter is in. Movement itself is a high-risk period for re-tension, line pullout, loss of monitoring, and loss of situational awareness.
Movement Planning
Use the Stokes litter if the route is awkward, the casualty is unstable, or there is risk of sudden deterioration. Use the medsled if the route and configuration make a lower-profile drag or slide safer. Secure oxygen, any lines, and the decompression site before movement. Assign one person specifically to airway and breathing observation during transport — not just lifting. Reassess immediately before movement starts and again immediately on arrival.
STAGE 4 Arrival to Sick Bay — Chest Tube Decision

The casualty arrives in Sick Bay after on-scene needle decompression. There was some initial improvement — breathing eased slightly and the pulse felt stronger — but the casualty remains tachypneic and anxious. Do not assume stability because the patient survived transport. This is where the temporary fix must be converted into a definitive plan.

⚠ Critical Transition Point
The needle decompression was a temporizing intervention. A chest tube is the definitive pleural drainage procedure and should be prepared early once the casualty is in a setting where it can be performed safely. A successful needle decompression should trigger definitive planning, not false reassurance. The key mental shift: temporary improvement does not equal definitive treatment.
  • Repeat primary survey immediately — do not assume the scene assessment remains valid.
  • Confirm persistent improvement or persistent instability after needle decompression.
  • Place on full monitoring: SpO₂, cardiac monitoring, blood pressure cycling, and EtCO₂ if airway support escalates.
  • Establish vascular access — if peripheral IV fails and the casualty is unstable, move to IO early rather than wasting time.
  • Prepare for chest tube thoracostomy: 5th intercostal space near midaxillary line, deliberate landmarking, prep, incision, blunt dissection, pleural entry, finger sweep, tube advancement, connection to drainage, securement, and immediate reassessment.
Decision Point 3 of 5
The casualty is in Sick Bay with marginal improvement after needle decompression. SpO₂ is 89–92%, BP 92/60, and the patient remains tachycardic. What is your definitive management approach?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Needle thoracostomy is a temporizing intervention. A chest tube is the definitive pleural drainage procedure and should be prepared early once the casualty is in a setting where it can be performed safely. The likely site is the 5th intercostal space near the midaxillary line with deliberate landmarking. Core sequence: prep, incision, blunt dissection over the superior rib margin, pleural entry, finger sweep, tube advancement, connection to drainage, securement, and immediate reassessment. Establish vascular access and full monitoring before or during the procedure.
~ Acceptable
Preparing the chest tube as standby is reasonable, but in a casualty with borderline hemodynamics and persistent abnormal vital signs, waiting for further deterioration before placing a definitive chest tube carries risk. The needle catheter can kink, fail, or only partially decompress at any time. Early definitive intervention is preferable to reactive intervention after re-collapse.
✕ Suboptimal
Do not misread brief improvement as full resolution. A temporary catheter can kink, fail, or only partially decompress. The patient may worsen again without warning. A successful needle decompression should trigger definitive planning, not false reassurance. Skipping the chest tube when clinical suspicion remains high leaves the casualty at risk for recurrent tension physiology.
✕ Suboptimal
A second needle decompression does not provide definitive drainage and carries the same failure risks as the first — catheter kinking, inadequate decompression, and temporary effect only. When the casualty is in a setting where a chest tube can be performed safely, the correct transition is to definitive management, not repetition of a temporizing measure.
Decision Point 4 of 5
After chest tube placement, the casualty initially improves but then shows signs of re-deterioration: increasing tachycardia, falling SpO₂, and rising anxiety. What is your priority?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Re-deterioration after chest tube placement must trigger immediate reassessment for recurrent tension physiology. Check for chest tube malfunction, blockage, or dislodgement. A persistent shock state after decompression may mean hemothorax, cardiac injury, or another cause is also present. Do not stop at the first diagnosis if the casualty stays shocked. Recurrent tension physiology due to failed or inadequate decompression is a recognized complication.
~ Acceptable
Increasing oxygen and fluid resuscitation are appropriate supportive measures, but waiting five minutes without investigating the cause of re-deterioration can be dangerous. In a casualty with a chest tube who is worsening, the priority is to determine whether the tube is functioning and whether a new or evolving pathology is present — not to treat empirically and wait.
✕ Suboptimal
Pain control matters, but if hypotension persists after decompression, reassess whether the casualty has additional pathology rather than reflexively treating this as pain alone. Administering analgesia without investigating the cause of hemodynamic deterioration risks creating respiratory depression in a casualty who is already borderline, and may mask a surgical emergency such as hemothorax or cardiac tamponade.
✕ Suboptimal
A chest tube in place does not guarantee ongoing decompression. Tubes can malfunction, block, or become dislodged. Dismissing re-deterioration as transient anxiety in a chest trauma casualty is a dangerous assumption. Many shipboard teams perform the right initial intervention then lose the gain because no one is assigned to reassess. Worsening vital signs after chest tube placement demand active investigation, not passive monitoring.
Medication Notes
Analgesia: Pain control matters, but avoid creating respiratory depression in a casualty who is already borderline. Titrate carefully and reassess after each dose.
Procedural analgesia/sedation: If the casualty needs a chest tube and is conscious, choose the safest achievable analgesia plan based on respiratory status, monitoring capacity, and the need to preserve spontaneous ventilation.
Do not let analgesia delay decompression in an unstable tension pneumothorax. Life threat first, comfort second.
STAGE 5 Evacuation Decision

The chest tube is in place and draining. The casualty has shown some improvement but remains hemodynamically tenuous. The ship is at sea — jetty transfer is not immediately available, and helicopter extraction is possible only by hoist with marginal weather. The team must now decide on evacuation strategy. This is the point where evacuation choice becomes dynamic, not fixed at the first decision.

Decision Point 5 of 5
The casualty has a functioning chest tube but remains hemodynamically borderline. You are at sea with helicopter hoist available but weather is marginal. What is your evacuation strategy?
✓ Optimal
Correct. If the casualty remains unstable, needs escalating oxygen, or re-deteriorates, this is an urgent evacuation problem. The request should go in early — it can always be cancelled if conditions are impossible, but you cannot get back time lost by waiting. Package for hoist with all tubes and lines secured, and prepare the team for the movement risks: hoist movement can destabilize chest devices and airway. Give a concise handover sequence: chest trauma mechanism, worsening respiratory distress, on-scene decompression performed, current response, current oxygen need, current hemodynamics, and whether a chest tube has been placed.
~ Acceptable
If the casualty stabilizes significantly after chest tube placement and weather truly blocks hoist, a short shipboard hold while arranging extraction may be reasonable — but only with a clear monitoring plan and explicit escalation triggers. This requires disciplined monitoring for re-tension, bleeding, and shock recurrence. Repeated respiratory reassessment, chest device checks, and oxygen planning are mandatory. This is a bridge, not a destination.
✕ Suboptimal
A functioning chest tube does not end the emergency. Recurrence risk and transport risk remain high throughout. Chest tube malfunction, blockage, or dislodgement can occur at any time. Worsening shock from associated hemorrhage, hypoxia during preparation, and pain-related respiratory decline all remain active threats. Evacuation decisions must be revisited after each major change in the casualty's response, not made once and forgotten.
✕ Suboptimal
Waiting for port in an unstable chest trauma casualty with ongoing risk of re-tension and hemorrhage introduces unacceptable delay. While hoist movement carries risks to chest devices and airway, these can be mitigated with careful packaging and team preparation. The delay of waiting for berth access — which may not be immediately achievable — exposes the casualty to hours of additional risk without definitive surgical capability.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Recurrent tension physiology due to failed or inadequate decompression. Chest tube malfunction, blockage, or dislodgement after initial placement. Worsening shock from associated hemorrhage. Hypoxia during movement or hoist preparation. Pain, agitation, or sedation-related respiratory decline if medication is given without adequate monitoring.
Communication & Handover
A clear clinical lead must call the likely diagnosis early. One person should be tasked specifically with respiratory observation during movement. Before movement, state aloud what may fail next: recurrent tension, worsening hypoxia, or circulatory collapse. If seeking teleconsultation or preparing evacuation handover, give a concise sequence: chest trauma mechanism, worsening respiratory distress, on-scene decompression performed, current response, current oxygen need, current hemodynamics, and whether a chest tube has been placed.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Waiting for certainty — tension pneumothorax is treated on suspicion when the casualty is unstable. Waiting for perfect confirmation is a classic error.
  • Misreading brief improvement as full resolution — a temporary catheter can kink, fail, or only partially decompress. The patient may worsen again.
  • Forgetting the move itself is hazardous — many teams perform the right intervention then lose the gain during transport because no one is assigned to reassess.
  • Not stopping at the first diagnosis if the casualty stays shocked — a persistent shock state after decompression may mean hemothorax, cardiac injury, or another cause is also present.
  • Allowing analgesia to delay decompression in an unstable tension pneumothorax — life threat first, comfort second.
  • Treating a functioning chest tube as the end of the emergency — recurrence risk and transport risk remain high throughout.
  • Failing to revisit the evacuation decision after each major change in the casualty's response.

Clinical Summary

This casualty has a high-probability tension pneumothorax after chest trauma and should be managed as an unstable obstructive-shock chest emergency. Immediate on-scene decompression is the correct first major action when equipment can be brought quickly.

Once partially stabilized, the casualty should be moved to Sick Bay for full reassessment and likely chest tube thoracostomy. Evacuation urgency depends on response to definitive treatment, but recurrence risk and transport risk remain high throughout.

If evacuation is delayed, the hold plan must include repeated respiratory reassessment, chest device checks, oxygen planning, pain control, and explicit escalation triggers.
