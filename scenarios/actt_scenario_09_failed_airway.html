Scenario 09 of 12

Failed Airway / Cannot Intubate, Cannot Oxygenate

Failed Airway Cricothyroidotomy Facial Trauma CICO Rescue Airway

A sailor is struck in the face and neck by a recoiling line under tension during deck work. He collapses with copious oral bleeding, severe facial distortion, stridor, and progressive cyanosis. The team must recognize a rapidly failing airway, decide whether oral intubation is realistic, set failure triggers before any attempt, and convert to surgical cricothyroidotomy when the airway cannot be secured — all in a narrow upper-deck passageway at sea at night.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize a failed airway early and stop repeated low-yield attempts.
  • Distinguish when oral intubation remains reasonable versus when immediate surgical airway is required.
  • Run a can't intubate, can't oxygenate sequence in a shipboard environment with limited space and movement.
  • Package the casualty for ongoing airway management, movement, and evacuation after a rescue airway is placed.
  • Use closed-loop communication and explicit failure triggers during a rapidly deteriorating airway event.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Facial Trauma and Airway Crisis

A 32-year-old sailor is struck in the face and neck by a recoiling line under tension during deck work. Crew report immediate collapse to knees, copious oral bleeding, obvious facial trauma, and rapidly worsening noisy respirations. By the time you arrive, he is sitting against a bulkhead, agitated, blood pooling in the oropharynx, with severe facial distortion, stridor, and progressive cyanosis.

Teaching Note
At sea, night operations, moderate sea state. The ship has a red deck, so rotary-wing extraction is by hoist only. The casualty is located in a narrow upper-deck access passage near damage-control equipment staging.
Operational Note
Take command and declare the main problem out loud: "This is a rapidly failing airway with facial trauma." Assign immediate roles: airway lead, suction/BVM support, monitor/med prep, scene control and lighting.
STAGE 2 Scene Priorities & Airway Decision

Despite suction and oxygen, the patient becomes more obtunded. SpO₂ falls to 79%. Jaw thrust improves flow only minimally. Facial trauma is severe, landmarks are distorted, and the oropharynx is obscured by blood. The patient is now barely following commands.

  • Apply oxygen immediately; suction aggressively and repeatedly.
  • Position for best airway access while maintaining manual stabilization if cervical concern exists.
  • Do a fast decision check: can the patient oxygenate, can the patient ventilate, can standard oral intubation realistically work?
  • This casualty should NOT be moved first if the airway is clearly failing — moving a patient with a rapidly collapsing upper airway before securing it may convert a difficult airway into an unrecoverable one.
Decision Point 1 of 5
The patient has severe facial trauma, blood-filled oropharynx, SpO₂ 79% and falling, and is barely following commands. What is your immediate airway plan?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. One controlled attempt with a declared stop point is the safest strategy when there is still a possibility of a view. The critical element is stating the failure trigger out loud BEFORE starting: this protects decision-making and ensures the team transitions faster. The surgical cric kit must be open before the first attempt.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Going directly to surgical airway is defensible when facial trauma is this severe — the oral route may be doomed. However, if a rapid video laryngoscope attempt is feasible and oxygenation can still be briefly supported, one controlled attempt with a strict limit is still reasonable.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Every repeated failed attempt worsens hypoxia, swelling, and time loss. Do not chase repeated views in a bloody facial trauma airway. This is the most common and most dangerous error in failed-airway scenarios.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. This patient is not going to stabilize — the airway is actively failing. BVM is increasingly ineffective because of facial instability and obstruction. Waiting for improvement that will not come burns the remaining oxygenation time.
Teaching Note
Severe facial trauma plus heavy bleeding may make standard oral intubation unrealistic. If oral intubation is not likely to succeed, do not waste the airway with repeated attempts. In a difficult airway, one controlled attempt is the maximum if you believe a view is still possible and oxygenation can be supported.
STAGE 3 Failed Attempt & CICO Declaration

Video laryngoscopy yields only blood and distorted tissue. There is no reliable glottic view. Oxygen saturation drops further to 72%. BVM is increasingly ineffective because of facial instability and obstruction. This is now a can't intubate, can't oxygenate event.

⚠ CICO Declared
This is now a can't intubate, can't oxygenate event. Do not make a second speculative attempt. Do not keep "trying to get a better look" while saturation falls. Do not delay for perfect sterility or ideal setup. The rescue airway is now the life-saving step.
Decision Point 2 of 5
Failed intubation attempt. SpO₂ 72% and falling. BVM ineffective. You have declared CICO. What is your next action?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. Once CICO is declared, the surgical airway is the life-saving step. The sequence is disciplined: one assistant stabilizes the larynx, another maintains suction and passes equipment. Midline vertical skin incision to find the membrane despite blood and swelling, enter the cricothyroid membrane, place the tube.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable only if extremely brief. A bougie-assisted attempt can be reasonable as a single last bridge — but only if it takes seconds, not minutes. The danger is that any further oral attempt delays the definitive rescue step while saturation continues to fall.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. In severe facial trauma with blood-filled airway, a supraglottic device is unlikely to seal effectively and does not address the obstruction. The surgical airway is the appropriate rescue when both intubation and oxygenation have failed.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Delay for setup optimization kills patients in CICO. The rescue airway must happen now with what is immediately available. A technically imperfect but timely cric saves more lives than a delayed perfect one.
STAGE 4 Post-Cricothyroidotomy Management

The surgical cricothyroidotomy is performed. EtCO₂ confirms tube placement. Chest rise is visible. SpO₂ begins recovering to 88%, then 92%. The immediate crisis has been addressed — but this is not the end of the problem. The patient now has a rescue airway that requires continuous maintenance, sedation, and evacuation planning.

  • Confirm placement: EtCO₂, chest rise, bilateral breath sounds.
  • Secure the tube with ties suitable for movement — not just temporary hand control.
  • Start controlled ventilation.
  • Begin post-airway sedation and analgesia as clinically appropriate.
  • Reassess oxygenation, EtCO₂, hemodynamics, bleeding, and any additional facial/neck injuries.
  • Monitor continuously — a technically successful cric can still fail from dislodgement, blockage, bleeding, or poor ventilation.
Decision Point 3 of 5
The surgical airway is confirmed and the patient's SpO₂ is recovering. What is your immediate post-procedure priority?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. The rescue airway creates a new maintenance burden. Secure the tube, sedate the patient to prevent agitation and dislodgement, reassess for other injuries (facial, neck, possible C-spine), and then prepare a controlled package for movement.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. Optimizing ventilation matters, but it should not delay concurrent actions like tube securement and sedation. A poorly secured tube that dislodges during movement is a worse outcome than slightly imperfect ventilation parameters.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Converting to an oral ETT in a patient with severe facial trauma and blood-filled airway is extremely high risk and unnecessary at this point. The surgical airway is working — do not create a second airway emergency.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Movement before securing the tube, starting sedation, and reassessing creates high dislodgement risk. Transporting an unsecured rescue airway in a shipboard passageway can convert a salvageable situation into an unsalvageable one.
STAGE 5 Evacuation Decision & Communication

The patient is now in Sick Bay with a secure surgical airway, sedated, ventilated, and hemodynamically stable for the moment. The ship is at sea with helicopter hoist available. This casualty almost certainly requires urgent off-ship higher-level care — the question is timing and packaging.

Decision Point 4 of 5
The patient has a rescue surgical airway, is sedated and ventilated. You are at sea with hoist-only extraction available. What is your evacuation plan?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. This casualty has major facial trauma, a failed oral airway, and a rescue surgical airway. They need definitive surgical care that cannot be provided onboard. Request extraction early — it can always be cancelled if conditions are impossible, but you can't get back time lost by waiting.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. If extraction is being arranged, organized holding is appropriate as a bridge. But the emphasis must remain on urgency — this is a high-acuity casualty, not a stable hold.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. A rescue surgical airway is not definitive care — it creates a maintenance burden. The patient needs higher-level airway management, facial trauma repair, and ongoing ICU care that cannot be sustained onboard.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. A complete secondary survey is important but must not delay the evacuation request. Catalogue injuries while preparing for extraction, not instead of it.
Decision Point 5 of 5
During teleconsultation, what is the most critical information to communicate about this patient?
✓ Optimal
Optimal. The handover must include: what happened (facial trauma, failed oral airway), what was done (surgical cric with timing), current status (ventilated, confirmed, sedated, hemodynamics), and what is needed (urgent extraction). This is closed-loop, structured communication.
~ Acceptable
Acceptable. This captures the essentials but lacks important details — timing of the failed attempt, timing of cric placement, method of confirmation, and specific hemodynamic data. In a high-acuity handover, precision matters.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. This dramatically understates the severity and urgency. A rescue surgical airway after failed oral intubation for major facial trauma is not "routine." This framing could delay appropriate response from receiving teams.
✕ Suboptimal
Suboptimal. Converting the surgical airway is not a shipboard priority and would create unnecessary risk. The teleconsultation should focus on evacuation, not on reversing a working rescue intervention.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Tube dislodgement during packaging or movement. Persistent hypoxia because the tube is misplaced, obstructed, or the patient also has thoracic injury. Bleeding around the cric site causing airway maintenance difficulty. Unrecognized need for additional sedation, leading to agitation and self-injury. False reassurance after initial oxygen improvement while ventilation remains inadequate. Delayed evacuation despite local stabilization.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Chasing repeated intubation views in a bloody facial trauma airway while saturation falls.
  • Failing to state a declared failure trigger before the first airway attempt.
  • Not opening the surgical cric kit before the first intubation attempt.
  • Delaying the surgical airway for better sterility or setup while the patient desaturates.
  • Assuming the rescue airway solves the problem — it creates a new maintenance burden.
  • Moving the patient before securing the tube, starting sedation, and reassessing.
  • Understating severity during teleconsultation and evacuation requests.
Key Takeaways
  • 01In a true failed airway, decisiveness saves the patient — delay kills.
  • 02Massive facial trauma plus blood can make oral intubation a poor plan from the start.
  • 03One controlled attempt with a declared stop point is safer than repeated speculative attempts.
  • 04A surgical airway is not the end of the problem — it creates a new maintenance burden.
  • 05Shipboard movement and evacuation planning must follow airway rescue, not replace it.
  • 06Communication to command and receiving facility should convey true severity and urgency.
Side Notes

Do not chase repeated views in a bloody facial trauma airway — every repeated failed attempt worsens hypoxia, swelling, and time loss.

A declared failure trigger protects decision-making: the team transitions faster when the threshold for abandoning Plan A is said aloud in advance.

Rescue airway equipment must be open before the first attempt — preparation delay is a common and avoidable cause of airway death.
