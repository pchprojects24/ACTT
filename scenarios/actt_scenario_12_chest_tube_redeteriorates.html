Scenario 12 of 12

Chest Tube Patient Who Re-Deteriorates During Holding

Chest Tube Device Maintenance Recurrent Tension Trend Recognition Prolonged Holding

A sailor sustained blunt chest trauma earlier in the day. A tension pneumothorax was suspected, needle decompression was performed on scene, and a chest tube was inserted in Sick Bay. The patient initially improved. Three hours into the prolonged holding phase, the patient begins to deteriorate again — SpO₂ drifting down, rising tachycardia, worsening pain. The team must shift from maintenance mode back to acute reassessment and determine whether the chest tube has failed, the tension has recurred, bleeding has worsened, or another cause explains the deterioration.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize when a patient with an apparently functioning chest tube is becoming unstable again.
  • Distinguish between recurrent tension physiology, tube obstruction, tube dislodgement, ongoing hemorrhage, and alternate causes of deterioration.
  • Apply structured reassessment before repeating interventions.
  • Manage chest tube troubleshooting in a shipboard environment with delayed evacuation and limited monitoring.
  • Decide when local management is sufficient and when evacuation or urgent teleconsultation is required.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Stable Post-Chest Tube Patient

The ship is at sea in moderate weather. A sailor sustained blunt chest trauma earlier in the day during a heavy-equipment mishap on the vehicle deck. A left-sided tension pneumothorax was suspected, needle decompression was performed on scene, and the casualty was moved to Sick Bay where a left chest tube was inserted. The patient initially improved and has been observed for the past three hours.

Teaching Note
This scenario teaches that initial improvement after chest tube placement does not remove the need for ongoing surveillance. A chest tube that was functioning earlier can fail later. Never treat initial improvement as proof of ongoing stability.
Operational Note
Evacuation requested, but earliest likely extraction remains several hours away. Pain managed with titrated analgesia. Left chest tube connected to drainage system, secured and initially bubbling with improvement in breath sounds.
STAGE 2 Re-Deterioration — The Trigger

Three hours into observation, the monitor shows SpO₂ drifting down to 90–92% despite unchanged oxygen delivery. The patient becomes more tachycardic, more anxious, and reports worsening left chest pain and increasing shortness of breath. Blood pressure trends down to 96/60. The team must assume that the patient is no longer stable and shift from maintenance mode back to acute reassessment.

  • Reassess airway, breathing, and circulation immediately — do not assume the chest tube is still solving the original problem.
  • Increase observation intensity: pulse oximetry, cardiac monitor, repeat blood pressure, repeat focused respiratory exam.
  • Assign explicit roles: one person reassesses the patient, one checks the chest tube system from patient to collection chamber, one prepares backup equipment.
  • Call out the key concern: "Chest trauma patient with chest tube is re-deteriorating — reassessing for recurrent tension, blockage, dislodgement, hemorrhage, or alternate cause."
Decision Point 1 of 5
The patient's SpO₂ has dropped to 90%, HR is rising, and BP is falling — three hours after a functioning chest tube was placed. What is your first action?
✓ Optimal
A re-deteriorating chest trauma patient requires immediate structured reassessment of BOTH the patient and the entire drainage system. The key is not to assume the chest tube is still functioning just because it was working earlier.
~ Acceptable
Increasing oxygen is reasonable as an immediate measure, but waiting 10 minutes to reassess a deteriorating trauma patient with dropping saturation and falling blood pressure is too passive. The situation demands immediate investigation.
✕ Suboptimal
Attributing deterioration to pain or anxiety without ruling out mechanical failure, recurrent tension, or bleeding is a dangerous assumption. Initial improvement after chest tube placement does not guarantee ongoing stability.
✕ Suboptimal
Jumping to a new intervention without first checking the existing device is a common error. The existing tube may have failed — check it first before adding new procedures on the opposite side.
STAGE 3 Chest Tube System Assessment

The team performs a structured assessment. On inspection, the chest tube dressing appears intact, but the tubing has a dependent loop that has collected fluid. There is no bubbling in the collection chamber. The tube marking at skin level appears unchanged. Breath sounds on the left are decreased compared to the post-insertion exam. The patient is becoming more tachypneic and agitated.

  • Trace the entire system from insertion site to collection device.
  • Look for kinks, dependent loops, clamped tubing, disconnected segments, and drainage unit positioned incorrectly.
  • Inspect for ongoing bubbling, loss of expected fluctuation, sudden cessation of output, or obvious clot burden.
  • Confirm the tube is still well secured at the skin and has not partially migrated.
  • Inspect for new subcutaneous emphysema, leakage around the tube, loose sutures, or dressing disruption.
  • Check whether the tube marking at skin level has changed from the original documented depth.
Decision Point 2 of 5
You find a dependent loop in the tubing with collected fluid, no bubbling in the collection chamber, and worsening breath sounds on the left. What do you do?
✓ Optimal
A dependent loop is a simple mechanical problem with an immediate fix. Correct it first and reassess — many tube "failures" are actually tube maintenance problems. If drainage and bubbling resume and the patient improves, you've solved the problem without a new procedure.
~ Acceptable
This approach can work, but briefly clamping a chest tube in a patient with possible recurrent tension carries risk. The simpler approach is to lift and straighten the tubing without clamping.
✕ Suboptimal
A dependent loop filled with fluid can obstruct drainage and allow recurrent pneumothorax or tension. This is not a benign finding — it is a correctable cause of the deterioration.
✕ Suboptimal
Removing a chest tube before attempting to correct a simple mechanical problem is premature escalation. Many tube problems can be fixed without replacement.
Teaching Note
No procedure ends at placement. In delayed MEDEVAC situations, device maintenance becomes part of critical care. The procedure is not finished at insertion — chest tube function checks must be built into every reassessment cycle during prolonged holding.

Decision Point 3 of 5
After correcting the tube, respiratory status improves but blood pressure remains low and bloody drainage has increased. What is your assessment?
✓ Optimal
A patient can have BOTH respiratory re-deterioration from tube obstruction AND worsening blood loss from hemothorax. Do not anchor on one explanation too early. The persistent hypotension with increased bloody drainage suggests the patient needs volume resuscitation and close hemorrhage monitoring.
~ Acceptable
The tube correction helped the respiratory status, but ignoring the hemodynamic picture and increasing bloody output is dangerous. Both problems need to be addressed simultaneously.
✕ Suboptimal
Clamping a chest tube in a patient with pneumothorax risks recurrent tension. The bleeding is from the injury, not the tube. The tube is draining blood that needs to come out — clamping it traps it in the chest.
✕ Suboptimal
Increasing bloody drainage with falling blood pressure and pallor is not "expected old blood." This is a concerning finding that requires active hemodynamic management.
Decision Point 4 of 5
One hour later, the patient has received fluids but remains hypotensive. Bloody drainage continues. SpO₂ is maintained at 93% but the patient is becoming increasingly confused. What is your decision?
✓ Optimal
A patient whose hemodynamic status is worsening despite fluid resuscitation with ongoing bloody chest tube output likely has intrathoracic hemorrhage requiring surgical intervention. This exceeds what can be managed onboard — escalate evacuation urgency NOW.
~ Acceptable
Continued resuscitation is correct management while awaiting transfer, but the critical action is escalating the evacuation priority. This is no longer a routine hold.
✕ Suboptimal
The system is draining blood, but the patient is bleeding faster than they can be resuscitated. "Working as designed" does not mean the clinical situation is acceptable.
✕ Suboptimal
Removing the chest tube will trap blood and air in the pleural space, potentially causing tension physiology. The tube is draining a hemorrhagic collection — removing it makes the situation worse, not better.
STAGE 5 Evacuation Escalation & Communication

The patient now requires continuous monitoring, ongoing fluid resuscitation, and possible pressor support. The chest tube is functioning but bloody drainage continues. Evacuation has been escalated to urgent priority. The team must prepare for transfer while maintaining care.

Decision Point 5 of 5
During preparation for urgent hoist extraction, what are your critical packaging and handover priorities for this chest tube patient?
✓ Optimal
During any casualty movement with a chest tube, one assigned person should protect the tube and tubing only. The drainage system must be secured against tipping, pulling, or kinking during ship movement and hoist transfer. The handover must include the full timeline and all interventions.
~ Acceptable
IV access is important, but the chest tube is the higher-risk device during movement. If the tube dislodges or the system fails during hoist transfer, the patient can rapidly deteriorate. Both need explicit protection plans.
✕ Suboptimal
Clamping a chest tube during transport of a patient with both pneumothorax and hemothorax risks recurrent tension physiology. The tube should remain unclamped and the drainage system should be secured for movement.
✕ Suboptimal
Removing a functioning chest tube before transport in a patient with ongoing hemothorax and possible pneumothorax is extremely dangerous. The receiving team needs the tube in place.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Re-tension after temporary improvement. Progressive hemothorax with falling blood pressure. Tube dislodgement during hoist transfer. Sedation-related masking of worsening respiratory failure. Team assumes "the tube is in, so the chest problem is handled" and misses slow deterioration.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Assuming initial improvement after chest tube placement means the problem is permanently solved.
  • Failing to perform regular chest tube system checks during prolonged holding.
  • Attributing deterioration to pain or anxiety without investigating the drainage system.
  • Anchoring on one explanation (pneumothorax OR bleeding) when both may be present.
  • Clamping a chest tube during transport of a patient with ongoing pleural pathology.
  • Reducing monitoring intensity because the patient was previously stable.
  • Delaying evacuation escalation when the clinical trajectory clearly exceeds onboard capability.
Key Takeaways
  • 01Initial improvement after chest tube placement does not remove the need for ongoing surveillance.
  • 02A re-deteriorating chest trauma patient requires immediate reassessment of the patient AND the entire drainage system.
  • 03If recurrent tension physiology is likely in an unstable patient, act clinically — do not delay because a chest tube is already present.
  • 04At sea, device function and securement are part of life-saving care, not minor maintenance tasks.
  • 05A patient can have BOTH respiratory and hemorrhagic deterioration simultaneously — do not anchor on one explanation.
  • 06A deteriorating chest tube patient should trigger earlier escalation, tighter monitoring, and renewed evacuation planning.
Side Notes

A chest tube that was functioning earlier can fail later. In delayed MEDEVAC situations, device maintenance becomes part of critical care. The procedure is not finished at placement.

At sea, tube securement matters more than on land — movement, repositioning, stretcher transfers, and fatigue all increase dislodgement risk.

Build chest tube checks into every reassessment cycle during prolonged holding.
