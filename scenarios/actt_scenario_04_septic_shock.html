Septic Shock in a Sailor With Infection and Delayed Evacuation

Sepsis Antibiotics Fluid vs Overload Vasopressors Prolonged Monitoring

A 31-year-old sailor is found febrile, confused, tachycardic, and hypotensive in berthing at 0215. The ship is at sea in deteriorating weather with uncertain flight operations. The team must recognize sepsis early, start empiric antibiotics within the first hour, titrate fluids without causing respiratory overload, initiate vasopressors when fluid-unresponsive, and sustain organized care through a possible 72-hour evacuation delay.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize sepsis and septic shock early in a sailor with evolving systemic illness.
  • Decide what can be done at the scene, what should wait until Sick Bay, and when movement itself creates risk.
  • Start a sepsis plan practical for the shipboard environment: access, fluids, antibiotics, reassessment, and pressor support.
  • Balance fluid resuscitation with the risk of worsening respiratory status in a limited-resource setting.
  • Decide between jetty transfer, hoist extraction, or holding the patient up to 72 hours when evacuation is delayed.
  • Use clear communication, documentation, and teleconsultation to support prolonged casualty management.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — Ill Sailor

At 0215, a 31-year-old sailor is reported to be "shaking, weak, and not making sense" in berthing. Shipmates state that he had a sore throat and cough for the last day, skipped part of supper, and became more lethargic over the last two hours. On arrival, he is sitting forward on the bunk, flushed, diaphoretic, and speaking in short answers. He looks ill, is slow to respond, and says he feels cold despite feeling hot to the touch. No obvious major trauma. No chest pain. Cough is productive. He reports right-sided pleuritic chest discomfort.

Teaching Note
This is a common shipboard trap. A septic patient can still appear "talking and awake," which can falsely reduce urgency. The combination of altered mentation, hypotension, tachycardia, fever, and poor capillary refill should already move the team into a shock mindset. Do not treat this as simple fever or dehydration.
Operational Note
The ship is at sea in deteriorating weather, with flight operations uncertain for several hours. Available assets include Sick Bay, routine oxygen, IV and IO access capability, limited point-of-care testing, onboard formulary, and one infusion pump immediately available. Personnel at first contact: one duty medic, one assisting sailor, and the PA responding from Sick Bay.
  • Confirm this is a medical deterioration with possible shock, not just uncomplicated fever.
  • Address airway and oxygenation first — apply oxygen by face mask or nasal route to improve SpO₂ and reduce work of breathing.
  • Obtain point-of-care blood glucose if immediately available — altered mentation should not be attributed to sepsis alone without excluding hypoglycemia.
  • Attempt IV access if quickly achievable; if repeated failure or difficult environment delays care, move now and establish access in Sick Bay.
  • Package the casualty for movement with assistance; do not let him walk unassisted.
  • Document time of first clinical contact and first abnormal vital signs — exact timing matters for antibiotic window tracking.
STAGE 2 Sepsis Recognition & Antibiotic Decision

At 0225, the patient reaches Sick Bay. Oxygen has improved SpO₂ to 96%, but he still looks ill and has become more drowsy. Repeat observations show worsening vitals. Lung exam reveals coarse crackles at the right base. He is not yet peri-arrest, but he is clearly trending worse. This is now a high-probability septic shock picture until proven otherwise.

⚠ Clinical Problem List
Probable sepsis with evolving septic shock. Hypotension with poor perfusion. Altered mental status likely due to systemic illness and reduced perfusion. Possible lower respiratory source — likely pneumonia. Risk of worsening respiratory function if fluid is given aggressively without reassessment. Potential need for vasopressors if hypotension persists after initial fluid resuscitation.
  • Place the patient on monitor immediately: SpO₂, BP cycling, and cardiac rhythm.
  • Establish IV access rapidly. If IV fails and the patient is deteriorating, transition early to IO rather than wasting time.
  • Insert a Foley catheter if prolonged monitoring is likely and shock is significant enough that urine output will guide management.
  • Antibiotics should be started within 1 hour of recognition of severe sepsis or septic shock — this is part of resuscitation, not a secondary task.
  • If blood cultures are obtainable without delaying treatment, obtain them first. If not, prioritize antibiotics.
Antibiotic Guidance
Probable pneumonia source: ceftriaxone 2 g IV plus azithromycin 500 mg IV is a practical empiric choice.
If source truly unclear: a broader unknown-source regimen such as ceftriaxone plus metronidazole may be used depending on the clinical picture.
Record exact administration times — the antibiotic clock matters in both teaching and real care.
Decision Point 1 of 5
The patient has fever, altered mentation, tachycardia, hypotension, and crackles at the right base — a high-probability septic shock picture. What is your antibiotic strategy?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Antibiotic timing is part of resuscitation in sepsis. It is not a secondary task to do "later when things settle down." Given this presentation, a source-directed empiric regimen for pneumonia is reasonable. Start within one hour and document the exact time — this matters for both teaching and real clinical care.
~ Acceptable
Starting any antibiotic early is better than waiting, but narrow-spectrum coverage in undifferentiated septic shock risks undertreating the source. In a shipboard environment with limited diagnostics, empiric broad-spectrum coverage is the safer choice for a patient this unstable.
✕ Suboptimal
Waiting for cultures in a patient with evolving septic shock delays a life-saving intervention. Onboard, definitive microbiologic diagnosis may never arrive. Empiric treatment should not wait for perfect certainty when the clinical picture is this clear.
✕ Suboptimal
Fluids are important, but antibiotics are equally part of initial resuscitation in sepsis. Deferring antibiotics while giving only fluids misses a core element of sepsis management. Every hour of delay in antibiotic administration worsens outcomes.
STAGE 3 Fluid Resuscitation vs Overload

An initial crystalloid bolus has been started. The patient is hypotensive and poorly perfused — fluid resuscitation is appropriate. However, after the first bolus, the BP rises briefly to 94/58 but 10 minutes later falls again to 88/52. The patient remains tachycardic, still confused, and now requires more oxygen to maintain saturation. This is not stable improvement — it is transient response with ongoing shock.

Fluid Caution
Do not let a brief BP bump after fluids create false reassurance. Septic shock often shows transient improvement before drifting again. After each bolus, reassess blood pressure, capillary refill, mental status, work of breathing, oxygen needs, and lung exam. If oxygen requirement rises, crackles worsen, or respiratory distress increases, do not continue giving fluid reflexively.
Teaching Note
In limited-resource care, the most dangerous error is not always doing too little — it is doing the same thing repeatedly without reassessment. Trend recognition matters more than a single "better" blood pressure. This should trigger a second structured reassessment, not automatic repeated large fluid boluses.
Decision Point 2 of 5
After the first crystalloid bolus, BP improved briefly then fell again. Crackles are worsening and oxygen requirement is rising. What is your fluid strategy now?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Give fluids deliberately, not automatically. A transient response followed by recurrent hypotension with worsening respiratory status signals that the patient is becoming fluid-limited. A targeted challenge with structured reassessment — and active consideration of vasopressors — is the appropriate next step.
~ Acceptable
Continuing moderate boluses with monitoring is reasonable in the short term, but the worsening lung exam and rising oxygen requirement suggest the patient is already approaching a fluid limit. The key is to not simply repeat the same intervention without recognizing when the approach needs to change.
✕ Suboptimal
Continuing large-volume fluids despite worsening respiratory burden is a common and dangerous error. Aggressive fluids in a patient who is not fluid-responsive will worsen pulmonary edema without fixing the perfusion problem. The next step is vasopressors, not more volume.
✕ Suboptimal
While recognizing the risk of fluid overload is important, completely withholding fluids in a hypotensive septic patient who has received only one bolus is premature. The answer is targeted, measured resuscitation with reassessment — not an all-or-nothing approach.
STAGE 4 Vasopressor Initiation & Ongoing Management

The patient remains hypotensive with poor perfusion after initial fluid resuscitation. Crackles are worse and oxygen requirement has risen. The clinical team must now decide on vasopressor support. Onboard realities matter: pressor use increases monitoring burden, requires reliable access, and raises the question of whether the patient can still be safely held.

Vasopressor Guidance
Target MAP: at least 65 mmHg as a practical minimum in septic shock.
Agent of choice: norepinephrine when hypotension persists after initial resuscitation and the patient remains underperfused.
Access: use an infusion pump if available. Central access is preferred in ideal settings, but peripheral administration may be necessary as a bridge if carefully monitored.
Critical safety point: if using a peripheral line, someone must explicitly own IV site checks for extravasation and tissue injury.
Decision Point 3 of 5
The patient remains hypotensive (MAP ~55 mmHg) with persistent perfusion deficit despite initial fluid resuscitation. Crackles are worsening. What is your vasopressor decision?
✓ Optimal
Correct. When hypotension persists after initial resuscitation with evidence of ongoing underperfusion, norepinephrine is the preferred next step. Start with a practical initial dose, titrate to a target MAP of at least 65 mmHg, and assign clear responsibility for monitoring the IV site, perfusion markers, and urine output. A pressor decision is also a manpower decision — starting norepinephrine means you must be able to monitor what you started.
~ Acceptable
One more targeted fluid challenge is not unreasonable if the patient has not yet received adequate initial volume, but the worsening respiratory picture suggests fluid tolerance is diminishing. Do not use repeated fluid trials as a way to avoid making the pressor decision. If this bolus fails, act without further delay.
✕ Suboptimal
Continuing large fluid boluses despite worsening respiratory burden is a dangerous error. Peripheral vasopressor use requires careful monitoring, but it is an accepted bridge when central access is unavailable. The risk of untreated refractory hypotension in septic shock outweighs the risk of carefully monitored peripheral norepinephrine.
✕ Suboptimal
Vasopressors without any fluid resuscitation is inappropriate. In septic shock, initial volume replacement is a necessary first step because vasoconstrictors are less effective in a profoundly volume-depleted patient. The correct sequence is measured fluid resuscitation followed by pressors when fluid alone is insufficient.

The norepinephrine infusion has been started. The patient's MAP is now hovering around 62–66 mmHg. Antibiotics are running. The team must now plan for sustained management. The evacuation timeline is uncertain — weather has worsened and helicopter hoist may not be available for many hours. The clinical team must convert from acute resuscitation to organized prolonged care.

Prolonged Hold Planning
Define reassessment intervals for vitals, perfusion, mental status, oxygen requirement, urine output, and IV site/device function. Maintain antibiotic schedule precisely. Track cumulative fluids in and urine out. Review whether the current pressor plan is sustainable with available personnel and monitoring. Recheck lung exam and work of breathing frequently — fluid-associated respiratory decline may be the next major problem.
Decision Point 4 of 5
Evacuation is delayed indefinitely. The patient is on norepinephrine via peripheral IV, antibiotics are running, and MAP is borderline. How do you organize ongoing management?
✓ Optimal
Correct. When evacuation is delayed, the team must convert quickly from a resuscitation mindset to an organized prolonged-care mindset. This means structured documentation, defined reassessment intervals, teleconsultation, clear escalation criteria, and sustainable workload distribution. When a ship cannot evacuate promptly, the clinical team must think in timelines: what can fail in the next 30 minutes, next 6 hours, and next 24 hours?
~ Acceptable
Continuing current management with close monitoring is reasonable in the short term, but without formal structure — documentation, fluid tracking, defined escalation criteria, and teleconsultation — care will drift and deterioration may be recognized late. The difference between "monitoring" and "organized prolonged hold" is the structure, not the intent.
✕ Suboptimal
The acute phase is not over. A patient on vasopressors with evolving septic shock requires more monitoring, not less. Failing to define clear escalation thresholds while the patient is pressor-dependent is a critical safety gap. Poor documentation of medication times, fluid totals, and reassessment findings during a prolonged hold is a listed pitfall.
✕ Suboptimal
Stopping vasopressors because monitoring is burdensome will result in refractory hypotension and likely cardiovascular collapse. The correct response to team fatigue is to reorganize workload and request additional personnel support, not to withdraw a life-sustaining treatment.
STAGE 5 Evacuation Decision

Weather conditions have shifted. A helicopter hoist window may open within the next two hours, but it is not guaranteed. The patient is pressor-dependent, on empiric antibiotics, and has an increasing oxygen requirement. Teleconsultation is ongoing. The team must decide how to manage the evacuation decision under uncertainty — balancing the risk of continued onboard holding against the risk and logistics of hoist extraction with an unstable patient.

Evacuation Framework
If alongside: this patient likely warrants urgent transfer to the jetty after immediate stabilization. If pressor-dependent or severely unstable, transfer should not be delayed for minor onboard optimization. Stabilize enough for movement, then move early.
If at sea with hoist only: the team must continue active treatment while preparing the patient for possible hoist once available. Packaging, line securement, and maintaining a simplified but durable monitoring plan become part of clinical treatment, not a separate logistic issue.
If evacuation is delayed up to 72 hours: maintain antibiotic schedule precisely, track cumulative fluids and urine output, and review whether the pressor plan is sustainable with available personnel.
Decision Point 5 of 5
A hoist window may open in 2 hours but is not guaranteed. The patient is pressor-dependent with rising oxygen needs. How do you manage the evacuation decision?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Prepare early, even before the window is confirmed. Packaging, line securement, and transport-ready monitoring are part of clinical treatment. The request should go in early — it can always be cancelled if conditions are impossible, but you can't get back time lost by waiting. Be ready to provide teleconsultation with: suspected source, vital sign trend, current oxygen requirement, total fluids given, antibiotic timing, pressor status, urine output, and current shipboard limitations.
~ Acceptable
Continuing structured care while awaiting confirmed availability is reasonable, but waiting until the hoist is confirmed to begin packaging wastes precious minutes. In a deteriorating patient, preparation should happen in parallel with treatment, not after confirmation. The patient may not be more stable later.
✕ Suboptimal
A pressor-dependent patient with rising oxygen needs and septic shock is unlikely to improve significantly with continued shipboard management alone. Refusing evacuation keeps the patient in a resource-limited environment where the next deterioration may be unrecoverable. The patient needs definitive care that cannot be provided onboard.
✕ Suboptimal
Stopping vasopressors and antibiotics for transport convenience risks cardiovascular collapse and loss of the antibiotic treatment window. Transport preparation must work around ongoing treatment, not interrupt it. Simplify the monitoring plan, not the treatment plan.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Further hemodynamic collapse despite pressors. Worsening respiratory failure from fluid-associated pulmonary edema. Extravasation injury from peripheral vasopressor infusion. Loss of IV access during packaging or movement. Rising pressor requirement beyond sustainable onboard capability. Equipment failure during prolonged hold.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Treating the case as simple fever or dehydration instead of recognizing evolving shock.
  • Delaying antibiotics while waiting for diagnostic certainty.
  • Continuing large fluid boluses despite worsening respiratory burden.
  • Starting vasopressors without assigning line monitoring responsibility.
  • Failing to define a clear escalation threshold if the patient becomes more oxygen- or pressor-dependent.
  • Poor documentation of medication times, fluid totals, or reassessment findings during a prolonged hold.
  • Letting a brief BP bump after fluids create false reassurance — septic shock often shows transient improvement before drifting again.
Key Takeaways
  • 01Recognize septic shock early from the whole pattern — altered mentation, hypotension, tachycardia, fever, poor perfusion — not a single number.
  • 02Start treatment where found, but move early to the place where sustained care can actually happen.
  • 03Give fluids deliberately, not automatically. Reassess after every bolus and recognize when the patient is fluid-unresponsive.
  • 04Start empiric antibiotics early and document exact timing — antibiotic administration is part of resuscitation, not a secondary task.
  • 05Use norepinephrine when hypotension persists after initial resuscitation, but only with a monitoring plan and assigned personnel.
  • 06In delayed evacuation, convert quickly from a resuscitation mindset to an organized prolonged-care mindset.
  • 07Trend recognition, communication, and handover are as important as the first intervention.
Teleconsultation Trigger

This is a strong scenario for early teleconsultation, especially once the patient shows refractory hypotension, rising oxygen need, or pressor dependence. The call should not wait until the patient is peri-arrest.

Be ready to provide: suspected source, vital sign trend, current oxygen requirement, total fluids given, antibiotic timing, pressor status, urine output, and current shipboard limitations.

Specific questions should include whether current source coverage is adequate, whether higher perfusion targets are needed, whether additional diagnostics would change management, and whether continued onboard holding remains acceptable.
