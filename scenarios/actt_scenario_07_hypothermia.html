Hypothermic Casualty Recovered From Cold Water

Hypothermia Altered Arrest Logic Rewarming Staging Evacuation Threshold

A sailor is recovered after unplanned cold-water exposure during upper-deck operations. The casualty is wet, cold, confused, and at risk of rapid deterioration. The team must prevent further heat loss, stage hypothermia clinically, choose an appropriate rewarming strategy, manage a potential transition into hypothermic cardiac arrest with altered arrest logic, and decide between helicopter hoist, jetty transfer, or prolonged shipboard holding.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize and stage accidental hypothermia (HT I–IV) after cold-water recovery.
  • Prioritize on-scene actions that prevent further heat loss while preserving airway, breathing, and circulation.
  • Decide what treatment must occur at the recovery point versus what can safely wait until movement to Sick Bay.
  • Apply modified arrest logic and rewarming priorities for severe hypothermia.
  • Choose between hoist extraction, jetty transfer, or shipboard holding based on physiology, location, and available support.
  • Anticipate deterioration during transport, rewarming, and prolonged holding.
STAGE 1 Initial Recovery — Cold Water Casualty

The ship is at sea in cold weather. A sailor has been recovered after an unplanned cold-water exposure during upper-deck operations. The helicopter cannot land on deck, so any aviation evacuation requires hoist. Sea state is moderate, wind chill is significant, and the recovered casualty is wet, cold, and at risk of rapid deterioration. First contact occurs at the recovery point on deck near the rescue area.

The casualty is conscious but confused and slowed after cold-water recovery. He is shivering initially, speech is slurred, and he is not reliably following complex commands. He is pale, soaked, and intermittently combative when handled. Respirations are spontaneous. Pulse is present but slow. He complains of exhaustion, chest tightness, and severe cold.

Teaching Note — Hypothermia Staging
Clinical staging guides treatment intensity:
HT I (Mild): Conscious, shivering, oriented — core temp typically 32–35 °C.
HT II (Moderate): Impaired consciousness, reduced or absent shivering, slowed function — core temp typically 28–32 °C.
HT III (Severe): Unconscious or near-unconscious, vital signs still present — core temp typically 24–28 °C.
HT IV (Arrest): Absent vital signs / cardiac arrest — core temp typically < 24 °C.
In this primary version, the casualty most likely fits HT II initially: altered mental status, cold exposure, preserved but impaired spontaneous function.
Operational Note
The recovery point on deck is exposed to wind, spray, and cold. It is medically inferior but operationally unavoidable for initial care. Do only what improves immediate safety there. Available immediate equipment: blankets, heat-conserving wraps, oxygen, monitor, suction, bag-valve-mask, IV/IO supplies, warmed fluids if preparation time allows, Stokes litter, medsled.
STAGE 2 On-Scene Priorities & First Contact

The casualty has been safely recovered and is no longer exposed to ongoing water. The team must now perform controlled first contact. Rough movement can worsen instability and may precipitate ventricular arrhythmias in significant hypothermia. The immediate priority is protection from further cooling plus ongoing ABC support.

  • Confirm the casualty is safely recovered and no longer exposed to ongoing water or spray.
  • Minimize handling — rough movement can precipitate ventricular arrhythmias in significant hypothermia.
  • Perform a focused ABC assessment at the recovery point.
  • Remove the patient from wind; strip wet outer layers only if practical without creating more exposure time.
  • Dry and insulate quickly with blankets, wraps, or an improvised warming package — prioritize torso insulation.
  • Keep the patient horizontal if possible.
  • Apply oxygen if indicated and if this can be done without delaying more important heat-conservation steps.
Side Note
Cold, altered patients can look near-arrested. Do not assume bradycardia equals arrest. In profound hypothermia, pulse checks may need to be deliberate and prolonged. Slow does not equal dead — avoid premature termination logic.
Decision Point 1 of 5
The casualty is conscious but confused, shivering, bradycardic, soaked, and exposed to wind on the recovery deck. What is your immediate priority?
✓ Optimal
Correct. The first thermal win is preventing additional heat loss. Gentle handling prevents arrhythmia risk. A focused ABC assessment ensures no immediate life threat is missed, while insulation and wind protection stop further cooling. Rewarming starts by stopping further cooling, not by chasing complex warming interventions before the basics are done.
~ Acceptable
Some heat-loss prevention is better than none, but insulating over wet clothing is far less effective than replacing wet layers with dry insulation. Skipping a focused assessment risks missing airway or perfusion problems that could worsen during movement.
✕ Suboptimal
Rushing movement without controlling heat loss or assessing airway and perfusion first is the wrong instinct in hypothermia. Rough, rapid movement can precipitate ventricular arrhythmias. The transfer itself is a physiologic event — secure, brief, deliberate movement is safer than rushed improvised movement.
✕ Suboptimal
Aggressive direct external heat application before assessment is inappropriate. Do not apply aggressive direct external heat in severe hypothermia, especially at the recovery point without monitoring. The immediate priority is preventing further heat loss and performing an ABC assessment, not chasing complex warming interventions.
STAGE 3 Staging Assessment & Movement Decision

The casualty is now insulated and shielded from wind. ABC assessment shows spontaneous breathing and a palpable pulse. The team must determine the hypothermia stage and plan movement to Sick Bay. In hypothermia, the move itself is a physiologic event. The method, speed, and handling during transfer all matter.

Teaching Note — Movement Principles
Use a movement method that protects the casualty from further exposure and minimizes jostling. A Stokes litter is appropriate for controlled deck-to-Sick Bay movement. A medsled may be useful where dragging or constrained movement is safer than repeated lifting through awkward passageways. Assign one person to watch airway/consciousness, one to watch lines and monitoring, and one to coordinate route and movement.
Decision Point 2 of 5
The casualty is insulated with spontaneous breathing and pulse. You suspect HT II (moderate hypothermia). How do you plan staging and movement?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Clinical staging drives treatment intensity. Deliberate, controlled movement with assigned roles minimizes arrhythmia risk from rough handling while getting the patient to a better treatment environment. Insulation must be maintained throughout the transfer — the move itself is a physiologic event.
~ Acceptable
The urgency to reach Sick Bay is understandable, but rushing movement and handling roughly creates avoidable arrhythmia risk in a hypothermic patient. Secure, brief, deliberate movement is safer than rushed improvised movement. Speed matters less than control.
✕ Suboptimal
Staging absolutely changes management. HT I requires different intensity than HT III or IV. Treating all hypothermia the same risks under-treating severe cases and over-treating mild ones. Clinical staging is the framework that drives every subsequent decision.
✕ Suboptimal
Extended on-scene workup at an exposed recovery point delays reaching a better treatment environment while heat loss continues. IV access and detailed exam can happen in Sick Bay. Do essential stabilization at scene, then move — do not stay for nonessential tasks.
STAGE 4 Sick Bay Rewarming & Deterioration

The casualty arrives in Sick Bay. The team repeats the primary survey: mental status remains altered, pulse is bradycardic, blood pressure is low, and oxygen saturation is borderline. Core temperature is obtained and confirms moderate hypothermia. The team must now initiate stage-appropriate rewarming while maintaining close monitoring.

  • Repeat primary survey immediately on arrival — do not assume the scene assessment remains valid.
  • Obtain core temperature if available.
  • Reassess mental status, pulse, blood pressure, respiratory pattern, oxygen requirement, and aspiration concerns.
  • Begin controlled active rewarming appropriate to stage.
  • Establish IV access if practical; consider IO if the patient is deteriorating and access is urgently needed.
  • Administer warmed IV fluids (~38–42 °C) if IV fluids are indicated and can be given safely.
Teaching Note — Rewarming Strategy
Mild–Moderate (HT I–II) with preserved circulation: Continue external insulation and active external warming as available. Use warmed IV fluids (~38–42 °C) if indicated.
Severe (HT III): Continue gentle handling, escalate monitoring. More invasive rewarming adjuncts may be needed depending on capability and consultant guidance.
Caution: Do not over-treat a stable, perfusing casualty with unnecessary aggressive interventions simply because the temperature is low. Match treatment intensity to physiology, not anxiety.
Decision Point 3 of 5
Core temperature confirms moderate hypothermia (~30 °C) with preserved circulation. What is your rewarming approach?
✓ Optimal
Correct. For moderate hypothermia with preserved circulation, active external rewarming with insulation, warmed IV fluids, and continuous monitoring is the appropriate approach. The key is matching treatment intensity to the clinical stage while maintaining vigilance for deterioration. Gentle handling remains essential throughout.
~ Acceptable
Basic warming is better than nothing, but room-temperature IV fluids miss the opportunity for safe thermal benefit. Periodic monitoring may miss a rapid transition to severe hypothermia or arrhythmia. Active external warming and warmed fluids are readily achievable and should be used.
✕ Suboptimal
Aggressive invasive rewarming is not indicated for moderate hypothermia with preserved circulation. Over-treating a stable, perfusing casualty with unnecessary aggressive interventions risks complications. Match treatment intensity to physiology, not anxiety. Invasive methods are reserved for severe or arrest scenarios.
✕ Suboptimal
Passive rewarming alone is insufficient for moderate hypothermia. At HT II, the body's own heat-generating capacity is already impaired. Active external warming and warmed IV fluids are needed to support recovery. Passive measures alone risk prolonged hypothermia and further deterioration.

Scenario branch: While being rewarmed, the casualty becomes less responsive. Pulse becomes extremely difficult to detect. Respirations become agonal. The team reassesses carefully — if absent vital signs are confirmed and severe hypothermia is the leading cause, this is now an HT IV / hypothermic arrest scenario.

⚠ Inflection Point — Hypothermic Arrest
The casualty has transitioned from moderate to severe hypothermia or arrest. Standard warm-patient arrest assumptions no longer apply. Severe hypothermia changes arrest logic: prolonged resuscitation is justified, epinephrine and defibrillation are limited, and gentle handling remains critical. Do not terminate resuscitation prematurely — "they're too cold" is not a reason to stop.
Decision Point 4 of 5
The casualty deteriorates into cardiac arrest during rewarming. Core temperature is severely hypothermic. How do you manage this arrest?
✓ Optimal
Correct. Severe hypothermia justifies prolonged resuscitative effort, especially if advanced rewarming or meaningful evacuation options remain possible. Limiting epinephrine and defibrillation to up to three doses/attempts before meaningful rewarming follows hypothermia-specific arrest logic. The heart may not respond to standard pharmacology until rewarmed.
~ Acceptable
Starting CPR and limiting defibrillation is reasonable, but following standard ACLS epinephrine timing without temperature consideration risks medication stacking in a heart that cannot respond until rewarmed. The hypothermic arrest framework specifically modifies drug timing and delivery.
✕ Suboptimal
Standard warm-patient arrest protocols do not account for the unique physiology of hypothermic arrest. Repeated defibrillation and epinephrine in a profoundly cold heart is ineffective and may worsen outcome. Hypothermia changes arrest logic — treatment must reflect the temperature.
✕ Suboptimal
Premature termination of resuscitation in hypothermic arrest is a critical error. "No one is dead until they are warm and dead" is the guiding principle. Profound hypothermia can be neuroprotective, and patients have survived with good neurologic outcomes after prolonged hypothermic arrest with appropriate rewarming.
STAGE 5 Evacuation Decision

The team must now decide on the evacuation strategy. The ship is at sea with helicopter hoist available but conditions are challenging. The casualty is either in severe hypothermia with ongoing resuscitation or has achieved return of circulation but remains critically unstable. The decision must balance physiology, weather, distance, and the ship's ability to sustain safe care.

Teaching Note — Evacuation Options
Jetty ambulance (if alongside): Best for a perfusing casualty who needs hospital evaluation but can tolerate a short transfer. Fast handoff to local EMS, but only possible if alongside or able to offload safely.
Helicopter hoist (at sea): Best for an unstable or high-risk casualty needing faster higher-level care. Shortens time to advanced rewarming and ICU support, but hoist prep is manpower-intensive and movement/packaging create added risk.
Hold up to 72 hours: Best when the casualty has relative physiologic stability with a manageable rewarming and monitoring burden. Avoids dangerous transfer if extraction conditions are poor, but requires disciplined reassessment — deterioration can be gradual.
Decision Point 5 of 5
The casualty is in hypothermic arrest with ongoing CPR, or has achieved ROSC but remains critically unstable. You are at sea with helicopter hoist available but conditions are marginal. What is your evacuation strategy?
✓ Optimal
Correct. A critically unstable hypothermic casualty — especially one who has arrested — needs advanced rewarming capability and ICU support that cannot be provided onboard. Early extraction request should go in immediately; it can be cancelled if conditions are impossible, but you cannot recover time lost by waiting. Packaging and timing should be coordinated early so the clinical team does not lose time after the decision is made.
~ Acceptable
If weather truly prevents extraction, organized holding with structured reassessment and continued rewarming is the correct bridge. But this should be treated as a bridge, not a final plan. Continue pressing for extraction — a patient who survives initial recovery can still deteriorate later. Repeated reassessment is the real safety net.
✕ Suboptimal
Holding indefinitely underestimates the monitoring burden and the limitations of shipboard rewarming for severe hypothermia. Deterioration can be gradual and insidious. The ship's ability to sustain safe intensive care is limited — definitive rewarming and ICU support require higher-level care.
✕ Suboptimal
Terminating resuscitation in hypothermic arrest based on perceived poor prognosis is a critical error. Severe hypothermia can justify prolonged resuscitative effort. Advanced rewarming at a receiving facility may still restore circulation. No one is dead until they are warm and dead.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Progressive decline in mental status leading to airway loss. Ventricular dysrhythmia triggered by rough movement or worsening physiology. Delayed recognition that the casualty has transitioned from moderate to severe hypothermia. False reassurance after initial warming response while the patient still drifts hemodynamically. Underestimating the monitoring burden if prolonged holding becomes necessary.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Rushing movement without controlling heat loss or assessing airway and perfusion first.
  • Rough handling that precipitates ventricular arrhythmias in a hypothermic patient.
  • Treating all hypothermia the same regardless of clinical stage.
  • Aggressive invasive rewarming for mild-to-moderate hypothermia with preserved circulation.
  • Passive rewarming only for severe hypothermia — insufficient when the body cannot generate its own heat.
  • Applying standard warm-patient arrest protocols without modification for hypothermia.
  • Terminating resuscitation prematurely because the patient "is too cold."
  • False reassurance after initial warming response while the patient still drifts hemodynamically.
  • Underestimating the monitoring burden during prolonged shipboard holding.
Key Takeaways
  • 01In cold-water recovery, stop further heat loss first while preserving ABC priorities.
  • 02Handle the casualty gently and move deliberately — the transfer itself can worsen the patient.
  • 03Stage hypothermia clinically (HT I–IV) and let the stage drive the intensity of care.
  • 04Severe hypothermia changes arrest logic — do not apply routine warm-patient assumptions blindly.
  • 05Evacuation choice must balance physiology, weather, distance, and the ship's ability to sustain safe care.
  • 06A patient who survives initial recovery can still deteriorate later — repeated reassessment is the real safety net.
Side Notes

There is no single hypothermia drug that fixes the problem; care is primarily supportive — thermal, airway, and circulatory. Any sedating medication must be used cautiously because hypothermic patients can deteriorate quickly and may need airway support.

IV access may be difficult in cold, vasoconstricted patients — plan early if access is likely to matter. If cardiac arrest occurs, resuscitation and movement must be organized around hypothermic arrest principles, not routine warm-arrest assumptions.

If consulting remotely, communicate: exposure history, recovery timeline, core temperature if known, current mental status, pulse/respiratory status, rewarming measures, and whether the patient is improving or drifting.
