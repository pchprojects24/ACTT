Progressive Head Injury After Fall in a Machinery Space

TBI Airway Risk Neuro Monitoring Spinal Precautions Evacuation Decision

A sailor falls in a lower machinery space, striking the head against hard metal. The casualty is initially awake but confused with a bleeding scalp laceration, and the team must decide what to do at the scene, how to extract safely, and when a worsening neurologic trend becomes an airway emergency — all within a cramped, noisy, medically austere shipboard environment.

Learning Objectives
  • Recognize a worsening head-injured casualty before complete decompensation occurs.
  • Decide what must be done at scene before movement and what can wait until Sick Bay.
  • Apply TBI priorities: oxygenation, ventilation, blood pressure protection, temperature control, and serial neurologic reassessment.
  • Decide when declining mental status becomes an airway problem rather than a monitoring problem.
  • Choose the appropriate evacuation pathway: immediate jetty transfer, hoist, or only a short bridging hold while extraction is arranged.
STAGE 1 Initial Presentation — The Fall

A sailor slips while descending a ladder in the machinery space, falls several feet, and strikes the right side of the head against hard metal. Witnesses report a brief loss of consciousness of a few seconds, followed by groaning and confused speech. When first found, the casualty is awake but disoriented, attempting to sit up, and asking repetitive questions. There is a scalp laceration with moderate bleeding. No obvious catastrophic external hemorrhage is seen. The casualty complains of severe headache and nausea.

Teaching Note
The machinery space environment is intentionally chosen because it forces realistic shipboard thinking. The space is hot, noisy, cramped, with metal ladders and limited room to work. The patient cannot simply be wheeled directly into Sick Bay — extraction requires packaging, controlled movement, and role assignment.
Operational Note
Ensure scene safety first: machinery hazards, heat, rotating equipment, slippery surfaces, and limited footing must be controlled before any clinical care begins. Assign a clinical lead immediately — one person maintains the plan, others perform tasks.
STAGE 2 Scene Priorities & First Decision

The scene is controlled and a clinical lead has been identified. The casualty remains awake but confused, with ongoing scalp bleeding and repetitive questioning. The team must now decide what to do at the scene and when to begin extraction toward Sick Bay.

  • Control scalp bleeding with direct pressure and dressing — do not let the visible laceration distract from intracranial risk.
  • Perform a rapid primary survey: airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure.
  • Take manual cervical spine control if feasible and clinically justified by mechanism plus altered mentation.
  • Apply supplemental oxygen if SpO₂ is not clearly adequate or if monitoring is limited at the scene.
  • Obtain a rapid neurologic baseline: GCS, pupils, gross limb movement, obvious seizure activity or vomiting.
  • Quick glucose check if agitation seems disproportionate — do not let nonessential checks delay extraction.
Decision Point 1 of 5

✓ Optimal
This is correct. The default in this scenario is NOT to fully manage the patient in the machinery space. Do essential interventions — bleeding control, spinal-aware handling, quick neuro baseline, oxygen if needed, packaging — then move toward a better treatment location.
~ Acceptable
Moving quickly is reasonable if the patient is stable, but skipping even basic stabilization risks missing a rapidly evolving problem. At minimum, control visible bleeding, get a quick neuro check, and provide spinal-aware handling before extraction.
✕ Suboptimal
This is a common error. The machinery space is cramped, noisy, hot, and medically inferior. Prolonged scene time for non-essential tasks delays getting to a better treatment environment. The right answer is rarely to perform an elaborate ICU-style workup in a confined space.
✕ Suboptimal
Scalp wounds bleed dramatically and visually dominate the scene, but the laceration is not the main threat. The main threat is secondary brain injury from delayed recognition, hypoxia, hypotension, and unplanned airway loss. Don't let the visible wound distract from the intracranial risk.
Side Note
The machinery space environment forces the distinction between two common errors: (1) staying too long at scene doing nonessential tasks, and (2) moving too early without addressing an immediately life-threatening deterioration. The right answer lives between these extremes.
STAGE 3 Movement & Clinical Evolution

The team prepares to extract the casualty from the machinery space via ladders and tight corridors to Sick Bay. Movement is a treatment decision — the transfer is not administrative but part of the clinical plan. The patient can worsen during movement, and the route may increase dislodgement, aspiration, or hemodynamic stress.

Teaching Note
Movement is a treatment decision, not an administrative task. The transfer from scene to Sick Bay is part of the clinical plan. Reassess before movement starts and immediately after arrival — a patient who was stable at scene may have changed by the end of extraction.
  • Assign roles: one person at the head/airway, one to monitor and assist, one to carry or manage litter, one runner if available.
  • Secure the casualty before movement — if spinal concern remains, minimize uncontrolled neck motion without creating dangerous delays.
  • Bring immediate airway rescue basics during movement: suction if available, oxygen, BVM, and simple adjuncts.
  • Package for movement using the safest available method — Stokes litter or medsled depending on access and vertical movement needs.

STAGE 4 Sick Bay Assessment & TBI Management

Once in Sick Bay, the casualty becomes more lethargic. Speech is slower. The patient now opens eyes only to voice, obeys simple commands inconsistently, and localizes pain. One episode of vomiting occurs. Pupils are still reactive, but one may be slightly more sluggish than earlier.

⚠ Inflection Point
This is the inflection point of the case. The issue is no longer just a head injury requiring observation. It is now a deteriorating neurologic casualty who may soon lose airway protection and who requires strict prevention of secondary brain injury.
  • Repeat the primary survey from the beginning — do not assume the scene assessment remains valid.
  • Airway: determine whether the casualty can still protect the airway, especially after vomiting and declining GCS.
  • Breathing: confirm oxygenation and ensure there is no secondary chest issue from the fall.
  • Circulation: reassess blood pressure carefully — in TBI, avoiding hypotension is critical.
  • Disability: repeat GCS, pupils, and any focal deficits that can be assessed.
  • Exposure: look for other missed injuries once the immediate environment is controlled.
TBI Priorities
Prevent hypoxia — maintain oxygenation aggressively; avoid any prolonged desaturation.
Prevent hypotension — maintain SBP above neuroprotective threshold.
Avoid hypotonic fluids — do not worsen cerebral edema through poor fluid choice.
Avoid unnecessary hyperventilation — target controlled range, not driven down unless true herniation signs appear.
Elevate head of bed if feasible once spinal handling priorities are addressed.
Treat fever if present; avoid adding another secondary brain insult.
Decision Point 3 of 5
The patient's GCS has dropped to 10–11 with vomiting and one sluggish pupil. What is your primary management approach?
✓ Optimal
Correct. This is no longer just a head injury requiring observation. It is a deteriorating neurologic casualty who may soon lose airway protection. Apply all TBI priorities: prevent hypoxia, prevent hypotension, avoid hypotonic fluids, controlled ventilation, and prepare for definitive airway control. Early preparation, not delayed reaction, is what this scenario rewards.
~ Acceptable
Monitoring is important but passive observation alone at this point risks missing the window for proactive intervention. The scenario is designed to reward early preparation. The question isn't whether to act, but what to prepare for while continuing to assess.
✕ Suboptimal
Do not sedate a deteriorating TBI patient casually — you may mistake drug effect for neurologic decline. Fluid choice must be deliberate (avoid hypotonic), and sedation decisions should only follow a clear airway plan, not precede it.
✕ Suboptimal
Routine aggressive hyperventilation is harmful. It causes cerebral vasoconstriction and can worsen brain ischemia. Brief rescue hyperventilation is only indicated as a temporizing measure if true herniation signs are present, not as a default treatment for declining GCS.
Decision Point 4 of 5
The GCS continues to fall toward 8. The patient can no longer follow commands and has recurrent vomiting. What is your airway decision?
✓ Optimal
Correct. When GCS falls toward 8, the patient can no longer protect the airway, and vomiting recurs, this is no longer a monitor-and-transfer patient — it's an airway-control problem. The expected sequence is: pre-oxygenation, airway setup, suction readiness, RSI preparation, strict attempt planning, backup rescue plan stated aloud, and post-intubation confirmation with EtCO₂.
~ Acceptable
Temporizing measures may be acceptable if evacuation is truly imminent (minutes away), but if transport time is significant, relying on positioning alone with a GCS of 8 and active vomiting is precarious. The risk of uncontrolled aspiration during movement is high.
✕ Suboptimal
A confused, talking casualty can still be on the edge of decompensation. Treating a brief improvement as proof of stability is a listed pitfall in this scenario. An OPA alone doesn't protect against aspiration in an actively vomiting patient with declining consciousness.
✕ Suboptimal
Watching a falling GCS without preparing the airway plan is a key pitfall. Waiting until complete decompensation turns a controlled situation into an emergency. This scenario rewards early preparation, not delayed reaction.
STAGE 5 Evacuation Decision

The patient is now intubated and hemodynamically borderline. The team must decide on the evacuation strategy. The ship is at sea with helicopter hoist available but weather is marginal. Progressive intracranial deterioration usually pushes toward early transfer — but conditions may force a temporary hold. The airway is controlled, but the underlying intracranial process continues. Every hour matters in a deteriorating TBI.

Decision Point 5 of 5
The patient is intubated and hemodynamically borderline. You are at sea with helicopter hoist available but weather is marginal. What is your evacuation strategy?
✓ Optimal
Correct. A rapidly worsening neurologic casualty should trigger early teleconsultation and early extraction request rather than waiting until decompensation is complete. Even with marginal weather, the request should go in early — it can always be cancelled if conditions are impossible, but you can't get back time lost by waiting.
~ Acceptable
If weather truly prevents extraction, organized holding with structured reassessment is the correct bridge. But the key word is "bridge" — this should not be treated as a comfortable long-term plan for a worsening TBI. Continue pressing for extraction.
✕ Suboptimal
Progressive intracranial deterioration usually pushes toward early transfer. A short hold may be unavoidable, but it should be treated as a bridge, not as stability. The patient needs definitive neurosurgical care that cannot be provided onboard.
✕ Suboptimal
Controlling the airway is not the end of management — it's a step. The underlying intracranial process continues. Delayed transfer for nonessential observation when definitive care is needed is a key error. Every hour matters in a deteriorating TBI.
What Could Go Wrong Next
Further drop in GCS with sudden airway loss. Recurrent vomiting with aspiration. Seizure activity. Pupil asymmetry and impending herniation. Unrecognized hypotension from associated occult injury or poor resuscitation strategy. Deterioration during movement because the team assumed the patient was stable.
DEBRIEF Pitfalls, Takeaways & Score
Common Pitfalls
  • Focusing on the scalp laceration and missing the worsening intracranial picture.
  • Delaying movement because too many low-yield tasks are attempted in the machinery space.
  • Moving too early without stabilizing the immediately dangerous issue first.
  • Watching a falling GCS without preparing the airway plan.
  • Allowing oxygenation or blood pressure to drift while concentrating only on transport logistics.
  • Treating a brief improvement as proof that the patient is stable.
  • Waiting too long to request evacuation support.
Key Takeaways
  • 01A worsening head injury is a trend problem before it becomes a collapse problem.
  • 02The scene goal is essential stabilization plus safe extraction, not prolonged compartment-based management.
  • 03The patient should be moved early to the best treatment environment unless an immediate life threat must be addressed first.
  • 04Falling GCS, vomiting, and worsening mentation convert this from observation to airway-risk management.
  • 05If the patient is worsening, evacuation planning should start early — not after complete decompensation.
  • 06In shipboard care, transport logistics, device security, and communication are part of the medical treatment plan.
Side Notes

Scalp wounds can bleed dramatically and visually dominate the scene. In this case, the laceration is important, but it is not the main threat. The main threat is secondary brain injury from delayed recognition, hypoxia, hypotension, and unplanned airway loss.

A confused, talking casualty can still be on the edge of decompensation. The team should not confuse the presence of speech with durable stability.

If the scenario is run alongside, the correct lesson is often early transfer. If run at sea, the correct lesson is often early extraction planning plus tight bridge care, not passive observation.
